## The Path — Evolutionary Framework of Flourishing

### Introduction

The ethics of the Path is simple in structure but complex in application. It rests on three prohibitions (actions that are never permitted) and three principles (actions that are desirable but not required).

This document explains how to apply the ethics in daily life, in professions, in conflict, and in extreme situations.

---

### The Three Prohibitions: Why These?

The prohibitions are not arbitrary moral rules. They are **rational red lines** — actions that make long-term cooperation impossible.

| Prohibition | Why It Destroys Long-Term Cooperation |
|------------|--------------------------------------|
| **Do not kill intentionally** | Killing eliminates the possibility of future cooperation with the victim, their family, and witnesses. It creates cycles of revenge that undermine all trust. |
| **Do not torture** | Torture destroys victims, creates enemies (including bystanders), produces false information, and corrupts the torturer — making trust among all parties impossible. |
| **Do not enslave** | Enslavement removes agency, creativity, and motivation from the enslaved while generating resentment and rebellion — making mutual benefit and sustainable cooperation impossible. |

These prohibitions are absolute in principle but complex in application, as the edge cases below will show.

---

### Ethical Minimalism

The Path practices **ethical minimalism** — it provides a minimal moral floor, not a comprehensive ethical system. The three prohibitions and three principles are designed to enable cooperation among people with vastly different worldviews, religions, and philosophies.

This minimalism is intentional:

- **The Path does not answer all moral questions.** It only prevents the actions that make cooperation impossible. Questions of virtue, meaning, beauty, and spiritual fulfillment are left to other frameworks.
- **Other ethical systems can be layered on top.** A Buddhist can add compassion. A Stoic can add inner tranquility. A Christian can add love. The Path does not conflict with these — it provides the foundation.
- **The Path is agnostic on the good life.** It only prohibits the things that harm others. What you do with your freedom is your choice.

This approach respects human diversity while still providing actionable guidance for cooperation.

### Applying the Three Principles to Ethical Dilemmas

The three principles — **develop yourself**, **create and help**, and **think long-term** — provide guidance when prohibitions do not directly apply.

**Example 1: Choosing a career**

- **Prohibition check:** Does the career involve killing, torture, or enslavement? If no, it passes the minimum.
- **Develop yourself:** Does the career offer learning and growth?
- **Create and help:** Does the career produce value for others?
- **Think long-term:** Will the career be satisfying and sustainable in 20 years?

A job that pays well but involves exploiting others fails the long-term principle. A job that is meaningful but unsustainable fails the long-term principle. The Path guides you to find work that satisfies all three.

**Example 2: Responding to a friend in crisis**

- **Prohibition check:** Does your response involve harming, controlling, or manipulating? If no, proceed.
- **Develop yourself:** Can you grow by being present?
- **Create and help:** Are you offering genuine support?
- **Think long-term:** Is your response building the relationship or enabling dependency?

**Example 3: Investing money**

- **Prohibition check:** Are you investing in companies that violate prohibitions? If yes, do not invest.
- **Think long-term:** Will this investment contribute to sustainable value?
- **Create and help:** Does the investment support businesses that create positive value?
- **Develop yourself:** Does managing this investment develop your skills?

### Application in Daily Life

**Prohibition 1: Killing**

The prohibition on killing is nearly absolute. The only exception recognized by the Path is **direct, imminent, proven threat to life** — your own or another's.

*Example:* A home invader attacks you with a weapon. You have no escape. You have seconds. This meets the exception: direct (present now), imminent (about to kill), proven (the weapon and attack are evident). Afterward, there must be public review — a transparent examination of whether the exception truly applied.

**Graded Scenarios Table:**

| Scenario | Prohibition Applies? | Reasoning |
|----------|---------------------|-----------|
| Self-defense against imminent lethal attack | **No** (exception applies) | Direct, imminent, proven threat to life |
| Defense of another person from lethal attack | **No** (exception applies) | Same as self-defense |
| Revenge killing (after the threat has passed) | **Yes** | Threat is not imminent; revenge is prohibited |
| Capital punishment | **Yes** | Not self-defense; state killing violates prohibition |
| War between states (combatant in battle) | **Complex** | Combatants face direct threat; civilian targeting prohibited |
| Killing to protect property | **Yes** | Threat is not to life; property does not justify killing |
| Assisted suicide | **No** | Self-determination; killing *oneself* is not prohibited |

**Public Review Requirement:**

After any killing in self-defense, public review is required:

- **What it entails:** A community panel (3+ impartial members) reviews the circumstances.
- **Written justification:** The person who acted must provide a clear account.
- **Right to appeal:** Any witnesses or the family of the deceased may present evidence.
- **Purpose:** Not punishment — but accountability and learning. The community examines whether the exception truly applied and whether less harmful alternatives existed.

The Path does NOT permit killing for:

- Revenge (the threat is not imminent)
- Punishment (the threat is not imminent)
- Property (the threat is not to life)
- War between states (the threat is not direct and imminent to you personally — unless you are a combatant in an actual battle)

**Prohibition 2: Torture**

The prohibition on torture is absolute. There are no exceptions recognized by the Path — including for national security, information gathering, or punishment.

This is a moral absolute, not a pragmatic one. Even if torture "worked" (it doesn't reliably), the prohibition would stand — because it destroys something essential to cooperation: trust that you will not be harmed by those with power.

**Why Torture Is Prohibited Even If It "Might Save Lives":**

*Example:* You are a police officer with a captured terrorist who knows the location of a bomb. The Path says: Do not torture. Find another way — intelligence, negotiation, technology, patience. This is not naive — it is rational:

1. **False information:** Under duress, people will say anything to stop the pain. False confessions and false intelligence are well documented. The "information" gained is often useless or actively harmful.

2. **Creation of enemies:** Torture does not just affect the victim. Family members, friends, and witnesses become enemies. The terrorist organization grows stronger. The number of future attacks may increase.

3. **Destruction of the torturer's humanity:** Torture desensitizes those who perform it. It corrodes the moral fabric of the torturer, their colleagues, and the institution. The cost is borne by the torturing society itself.

4. **Loss of legitimacy:** Torture forfeits moral authority. The torturing nation loses the right to claim the moral high ground. Allies withdraw support. Enemies gain propaganda victories.

**What Constitutes Torture:**

The following actions constitute torture (list is not exhaustive):

- **Physical torture:** Beatings, burning, electrical shock, drowning (waterboarding), stress positions, suffocation.
- **Psychological torture:** Sensory deprivation, sensory overload, isolation, humiliation, death threats against family, forcing watching torture of others.
- **Sleep deprivation:** Prolonged wakefulness (over 48 hours) as interrogation technique.
- **Extended solitary confinement:** Over 15 days in isolation constitutes psychological torture under international standards.
- **Forced position:** Keeping a person in painful positions for extended periods.
- **Denial of medical care:** Withholding treatment to coerce information.
- **Use of animals:** Introducing dangerous animals to intimidate.

Any action designed to cause severe physical or psychological suffering to extract information or coerce behavior is torture.

**Prohibition 3: Enslavement**

The prohibition on enslavement is absolute. It includes forced labor, debt bondage, human trafficking, and any system that deprives a person of autonomy without free consent.

*Example:* A business owner threatens to fire an employee unless the employee works unpaid overtime. This is subtle enslavement — using economic power to coerce labor. The Path says: This violates the prohibition. Acceptable: asking for extra effort with fair compensation. Unacceptable: coercion through threat of harm.

**Examples of Modern Enslavement:**

- **Debt bondage:** A person works to pay off a debt, but the employer sets terms that make the debt impossible to repay (e.g., deducting living costs from wages, extending the debt indefinitely).
- **Forced labor in supply chains:** Products are made using forced labor in distant countries where companies deliberately ignore conditions to reduce costs.
- **Human trafficking:** Transporting people across borders (or within borders) through deception or coercion for exploitation — forced prostitution, forced labor, organ removal.
- **Child labor:** Children forced to work instead of attending school, deprived of education and development.
- **Marriage without consent:** Forcing someone into marriage through physical threat, financial coercion, or social pressure — removing the right to choose.
- **Prison labor without consent:** Using prisoners as labor force without fair compensation or the ability to refuse — especially for private profit.

Any system where a person cannot leave, cannot refuse work, or cannot negotiate terms is potentially enslaved.

**What Does NOT Count as Enslavement:**

Not all restrictions or consequences are enslavement:

- **Voluntary employment:** You chose the job, you can quit (with notice). The employer cannot physically stop you.
- **Contracts with consequences:** You agreed to terms. If you break the contract (e.g., leave early), there are consequences (e.g., you owe money). This is not enslavement — it's agreement.
- **Parental authority:** Parents have authority over children. This is not enslavement — it's protective custody until the child is mature enough for self-determination.
- **Legal consequences:** If you break the law, you face consequences (fines, imprisonment). This is not enslavement — it's social contract enforcement.
- **Economic choices:** Employers set wages. Employees can choose to accept or reject. Rejecting may mean choosing another job or accepting lower pay. This is not enslavement — it's market negotiation.
- **Military service (voluntary):** You chose to serve. You can leave (with discharge procedures). Coerced conscription is prohibited; voluntary service is not.

### Application in Professions

**Doctor:**

- You must not kill patients (except through compassionate refusal of extraordinary measures — see [Document 9]).
- You must not torture patients (no forced treatment, no experimentation without consent).
- You must not enslave patients (no coerced organ donation, no coerced research).

The three principles apply: Develop yourself (keep learning). Create and help (heal). Think long-term (preventive medicine, public health).

**Teacher:**

- You must not kill (no corporal punishment that risks death).
- You must not torture (no humiliation, degradation, or coercion).
- You must not enslave (no forced attendance beyond legal requirements, no coerced labor).

The three principles apply: Develop yourself (model curiosity). Create and help (educate). Think long-term (teach how to learn).

**Police:**

- You must not kill except in direct, imminent, proven threat.
- You must not torture (no coerced confessions, no excessive force).
- You must not enslave (no forced labor citations, no cash bail systems that imprison for debt).

The three principles apply: Protect and serve. Think long-term (prevent crime through community building).

**Engineer:**

- Your work must not kill (safety standards).
- Your work must not torture (no systems designed to cause suffering).
- Your work must not enslave (no systems that remove human agency without consent).

The three principles apply: Develop skills. Create useful things. Think long-term (sustainability).

**Politician:**

- You must not kill (no death penalty — the Path recommends abolition).
- You must not torture (no solitary confinement, no coerced policy).
- You must not enslave (no mandatory labor, no deprivation of rights without due process).

The three principles apply: Develop wisdom. Create just institutions. Think long-term (legacies).

**Business Leader:**

- You must not kill (no dangerous products, no unsafe conditions).
- You must not torture (no hostile work environments, no retaliation against whistleblowers).
- You must not enslave (no forced overtime, no coercive debt, no trafficking).

The three principles apply: Develop talent. Create value. Think long-term (sustainable business models).

### Conflict Resolution

When conflicts arise between people who share the Path, the process is:

1. **Identify the interest, not the position.** ("You want money" is a position. "You want security" is an interest.)
2. **Seek mutual benefit.** If both parties benefit, the conflict is resolved.
3. **If no mutual benefit is possible, use arbitration.** A neutral party hears both sides and makes a decision.
4. **If arbitration fails, exit.** The right to exit a community is absolute.

The Path forbids:

- Revenge (we do not "get even")
- Retaliation beyond self-defense (we do not "make them pay")
- Ostracism without due process (we do not "cancel" people)

### Extreme Situations

**War:**

The Path does not forbid defense of one's community. However, it recommends:

- War only as last resort, after all diplomacy fails.
- Discrimination between combatants and civilians.
- Proportionality (the response matches the threat).
- Minimum force necessary.

The Path does NOT permit:

- Torture of prisoners.
- Enslavement of conquered populations.
- Targeted killing of non-combatants.

**Self-Defense:**

The prohibition on killing is relaxed only in direct, imminent, proven threat to life. This exception is narrow:

- Must be direct (the threat is present now).
- Must be imminent (the threat is about to happen).
- Must be proven (the threat is evident, not imagined).
- Must be reviewed afterward (community examines whether the exception applied).

### Edge Cases and Dilemmas

**Abortion:**

The Path does not take a position on when life begins. It takes a position on the woman's right to choose — because forced pregnancy is a form of deprivation of autonomy (removal of autonomy, which is close to enslavement). However, the Path encourages reflection on the moral weight of potential life.

**Death Penalty:**

The Path recommends abolition. It violates the prohibition on killing, provides no public safety benefit (incapacitation can be achieved without death), and cannot be reversed if wrong. However, the Path does not forcibly prohibit it — it leaves the decision to local communities, who must live with the consequences.

**Assisted Suicide:**

The Path permits. The prohibition is on killing *another* without consent. Self-determination over one's own body is fundamental. See [Document 9] for full guidance on end-of-life choices.

**Mandatory Vaccination:**

The Path recommends but does not mandate. Vaccination protects the community (principle of helping others). However, forced medical treatment violates bodily autonomy (close to enslavement). The Path encourages education and incentive, not coercion.

**Self-Defense Against Non-Lethal But Severe Attack:**

*Scenario:* Someone attacks you with a weapon that is not lethal but will cause permanent serious injury (e.g., blinding you, breaking your arms, severe beating). You have the opportunity to use lethal force to prevent the attack.

*The Path's position:* The prohibition on killing is relaxed in direct, imminent, proven threat to *serious* injury, not just death. Permanent loss of function (blindness, paralysis, disfigurement) meets the threshold of a threat that permanently destroys quality of life. However, **proportionality** applies: if non-lethal force can stop the attack, use non-lethal force. Only use lethal force if no lesser option exists.

*Reasoning:* The threat to a meaningful life (ability to work, create, help) is serious enough to justify self-defense. The community review process evaluates whether proportionality was maintained.

**Autonomous Weapons in War:**

*Scenario:* A nation deploys autonomous weapons (AI drones, automated turrets) that can identify and kill enemies without direct human control.

*The Path's position:* Autonomous weapons are **not prohibited** — but their use must meet the same standards as human soldiers:

1. **Discrimination:** The weapon must distinguish combatants from non-combatants. If it cannot, it may not be used.
2. **Proportionality:** The weapon's use must be proportionate to the threat.
3. **Accountability:** Someone must be responsible for the weapon's actions. If an algorithm kills civilians, the commander who deployed it is responsible.
4. **Review:** The use of autonomous weapons must be subject to post-action review, just like self-defense.

*Reasoning:* The Path does not prohibit technology. It prohibits the actions that technology performs. Autonomous weapons can be used ethically — or unethically. The standards apply to the user, not just the tool.

**Bounty Hunting and Private Military Contractors:**

*Scenario:* A private company is hired to capture or kill a dangerous criminal. The company is paid to produce results, not to follow due process.

*The Path's position:* Private actors performing functions traditionally reserved for the state (arrest, detention, lethal action) must meet the same standards as the state:

- No torture (interrogation methods must be legal).
- No enslavement (contractors cannot be coerced).
- Due process must be followed (no extra-judicial killing without self-defense standard).
- Accountability must be clear (who authorized the action?).

*Reasoning:* The Path applies to all actors, not just governments. Private contractors do not have more latitude than governments — they have less, because they lack the state's legitimacy.

**Economic Coercion as Enslavement:**

*Scenario:* A employer threatens to blacklist an employee in the industry unless the employee drops a workplace safety complaint.

*The Path's position:* Using economic power to coerce someone into dropping a legitimate complaint is **enslavement** — it removes the person's ability to exercise their rights. Economic threats that prevent someone from seeking justice, reporting harm, or leaving a harmful situation are prohibited.

*Reasoning:* Freedom requires not just physical movement but the ability to speak, report, and seek remedy. Economic coercion that silences legitimate complaints is a form of control that destroys autonomy.
