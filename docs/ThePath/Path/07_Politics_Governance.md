## Introduction: Why Politics Matters in the Path

Politics is the exercise of power over a community — who makes decisions, how they're made, and what resources are allocated. For the Path, politics is not about ideology or partisanship — it's about creating **systems that enable cooperation** while respecting individual autonomy.

The Path does not prescribe a specific political system. It provides **principles** that can be applied to any system — democratic, monarchical, hybrid, or even (with adaptation) autocratic. The key is not the form of government but the **underlying values** it embodies.

## Part I: Core Political Principles

### 1.1 Subsidiarity

**Definition:** Decisions should be made at the lowest effective level.

**Implementation:**

| Level | Decisions |
|-------|-----------|
| Individual | Personal choices, lifestyle, beliefs |
| Family | Child-rearing, family decisions |
| Local community | Neighborhood, town decisions |
| Regional | Province, state, regional matters |
| National | National matters (defense, currency) |
| Global | Matters that affect everyone |

**Rationale:** Decisions made close to those affected are more responsive, more knowledgeable, and more accountable. Higher levels only handle what cannot be handled lower.

**Example:** If a neighborhood needs a park, the neighborhood should decide. If a region needs a highway connecting cities, the region should decide. If a country needs a defense policy, the country should decide.

**Note:** Subsidiarity is a guide, not a rigid rule. Some issues may require higher or lower levels depending on context — for example, pandemic response often requires national coordination, while local water systems are best managed locally.

### 1.2 Separation of Powers

**Definition:** No single body should hold all power.

**Implementation:**

| Power | Body |
|-------|------|
| Legislative | Makes rules |
| Executive | Implements rules |
| Judicial | Interprets rules |

**Rationale:** Concentrated power corrupts, even with good intentions. When one person or body holds all authority, the temptation to use it for personal benefit becomes almost irresistible — and even well-meaning leaders become corrupt over time. History shows this repeatedly: the Roman Empire's emperors accumulated power until corruption destroyed the entire system; the Soviet Union's Stalin consolidated all authority in himself, resulting in millions of deaths. Separation of powers creates checks and balances — not because people are evil, but because even good people become corrupted by unchecked power.

**Note:** The Path does not require Western-style separation — different cultures have developed different ways to distribute power. The key is that no one has total control.

### 1.3 Long-Term Council

**Definition:** An advisory body focused on 50–500 year consequences.

**Composition:** Not elected (or differently elected)

- Scientists, philosophers, futurists
- Representatives of future generations (conceptually)
- Long-term thinkers

**Function:** Provides input, not veto

- Advisory opinions on long-term impacts
- Warnings about long-term risks
- Recommendations for long-term planning

**Rationale:** Elected officials think in election cycles. We need people thinking about what happens after we're all gone.

**Example:** Consider nuclear waste disposal — radioactive materials remain dangerous for thousands of years. No elected official faces accountability for decisions that affect generations 10,000 years from now. A Long-Term Council would provide input on such issues, warning about long-term risks and recommending that current generations set aside resources for future management rather than deferring the problem.

**Note:** Council members should be chosen for expertise and long-term orientation, not through popular election. Popular elections create incentives for short-term thinking — officials must appeal to voters who will judge them within years, not decades. Instead, members should be selected for demonstrated long-term thinking, relevant expertise (e.g., climate science, demographic forecasting, ethics), and independence from political pressure.

### 1.4 Transparency

**Definition:** All government activities visible to citizens.

**Requirements:**

| Area | Transparency |
|------|--------------|
| Laws | Published, accessible, clear |
| Budgets | Open, public, understandable |
| Decisions | Recorded, with reasons |
| Statistics | Accurate, available |

**Rationale:** Secrecy breeds corruption. Citizens need information to hold government accountable.

### Why Secrecy Is Harmful

Government secrecy creates multiple problems:

1. **Hides corruption** — When decisions are made behind closed doors, officials can enrich themselves without detection. History is filled with examples where secrecy enabled massive corruption (e.g., the Watergate scandal, the 1MDB corruption case in Malaysia).

2. **Prevents accountability** — Citizens cannot hold officials responsible for decisions they cannot see. Without transparency, bad decisions go unquestioned and unfixed.

3. **Breeds distrust** — When government operates in secret, people assume the worst. This erodes the social trust necessary for cooperation and governance.

4. **Enables abuse** — Secretive systems are more easily weaponized against minorities, dissidents, and opponents. Transparency acts as a check on oppression.

The Path demands transparency not because government is inherently evil, but because transparency makes abuse much harder.

### 1.5 Right to Exit

**Definition:** Citizens may leave any jurisdiction.

**Requirements:**

- No exit taxes
- No confiscated property beyond reasonable debts
- Right to take legitimate property
- Documentation available for travel

**Rationale:** People should not be trapped. If a jurisdiction doesn't work for someone, they should be able to leave. This also disciplines governments — if people leave, something is wrong.

**Note:** Exit should not be punitive. People leaving a jurisdiction should not face confiscation of legitimate property, excessive taxation, or barriers to travel. The right to exit is meaningful only if leaving is genuinely free — if the cost of leaving is too high, the "right" becomes theoretical.

## Part II: Embedding the Path into Existing Institutions

### 2.1 Phase 1: Acknowledge the Prohibitions

The three prohibitions should be recognized as **inviolable in law**:

| Prohibition | Legal Implementation |
|-------------|---------------------|
| No killing | No murder, no death penalty (eventually), strict self-defense limits |
| No torture | No torture (even for national security), humane prison conditions |
| No enslavement | No forced labor, no trafficking, free movement |

**Method:** Constitutional recognition, statutory codification, judicial enforcement

### 2.2 Phase 2: Establish Long-Term Advisory Bodies

Create bodies that think about the long term:

- Future generations offices
- Long-term impact assessments
- Advisory councils on 50+ year issues

**Method:** Legislative creation, budget allocation, staff hiring

### 2.3 Phase 3: Create Transparency Mechanisms

Make government visible:

| Mechanism | Application |
|-----------|-------------|
| Freedom of information | Public access to government documents |
| Open data | Machine-readable government statistics |
| Public hearings | Decision-making open to observation |
| Asset disclosure | Officials' finances transparent |

**Method:** Legal requirements, technology systems, enforcement

### 2.4 Phase 4: Decentralize

Move decisions to lower levels where possible:

- Local control of local matters
- Regional control of regional matters
- Reduce centralized authority

**Method:** Constitutional reform, legislative delegation, local empowerment

### 2.5 No Violence Required

The Path advocates **evolution over revolution**:

- Work within existing systems
- Gradual reform
- Building alternative institutions
- Education and persuasion

The Path opposes violent revolution because:

- Violence creates cycles of violence
- Revolutions often produce tyranny worse than what they replaced
- There are always better alternatives through peaceful change

## The Path and Revolution

The Path explicitly opposes violent revolution. This is not a tactical preference — it is a principled position grounded in the Path's core values.

**Why the Path opposes violent revolution:**

1. **Violence creates cycles of violence.** Every revolution that succeeds through violence establishes the precedent that violence is an acceptable tool for political change. This creates ongoing instability as each new group uses violence to challenge the current order.

2. **Revolutions often produce tyranny worse than what they replaced.** History shows that revolutionary movements — however idealistic — frequently become oppressive. The French Revolution produced Napoleon. The Russian Revolution produced Stalin. The Path values outcomes, not intentions.

3. **Better alternatives exist.** Gradual reform through education, advocacy, and institution-building can achieve the Path's goals without the human cost of violence. Change that comes through peaceful transformation is more durable and less likely to create new forms of oppression.

4. **Violations of the prohibitions.** Violent revolution almost always involves killing, torture, or enslavement — all three of the Path's core prohibitions. Even "just" wars or "liberation movements" typically commit these violations in practice.

The Path therefore supports:

- Working within existing legal and political systems
- Gradual reform and institutional change
- Building alternative institutions (schools, communities, businesses) that embody Path values
- Education and persuasion to change hearts and minds
- International pressure, treaties, and diplomatic engagement
- Exit for those who cannot live under their current system

The Path does not accept that any end justifies the means. The means must be compatible with the ends — if the means violate the prohibitions, the resulting system will inherit that violence.

## Part III: Adaptation to Different Systems

The Path is designed to work within any political system. The focus is on **principles**, not forms — any system can adopt subsidiarity, separation of powers, transparency, long-term councils, and exit rights regardless of its specific structure.

### 3.1 Democratic Systems

| Element | Path Approach |
|---------|---------------|
| Elected officials | Acceptable, serve the people |
| Referenda | Good for major decisions |
| Transparency | Essential |
| Political parties | Acceptable, but no official preference |

**How to embed Path:** In liberal democracies, the Path's principles align closely with existing values of accountability, rule of law, and individual rights. The easiest approach is to advocate for Path principles through electoral politics — support candidates who commit to transparency, long-term thinking, and prohibition enforcement. Create citizen coalitions to push for transparency laws, future-generation offices, and exit rights. Work within parties or create new political movements that explicitly adopt Path principles without becoming ideologically rigid.

### 3.2 Monarchies

| Element | Path Approach |
|---------|---------------|
| Monarch | Acceptable if benevolent |
| Advisory councils | Essential for diverse input |
| Succession | Should be transparent and fair |
| Power limits | Essential — monarch must not have total control |

**How to embed Path:** In monarchies, focus on education and advisory institutions. Work to educate current and future monarchs about Path principles through scholarly engagement, advisory relationships, and demonstrated results in other nations. Create advisory councils that provide diverse input to the monarch, ensuring that power is not absolute. Build relationships with reform-minded members of the royal family. The Path does not demand republicanism — a benevolent monarchy that respects the five principles is fully compatible with the Path.

### 3.3 Hybrid Systems

| Element | Path Approach |
|---------|---------------|
| Elected executives | Good for responsiveness |
| Appointed judges | Acceptable |
| Traditional leaders | Can be integrated |
| Local customs | Should be respected |

**How to embed Path:** Many countries blend democratic, traditional, and authoritarian elements. In such systems, engage with multiple power centers — elected officials, traditional leaders, religious authorities — separately. Adapt Path language to local context, emphasizing how the five principles reinforce rather than conflict with existing values. Create hybrid institutions that combine modern transparency mechanisms with traditional decision-making processes. Build coalitions across different political actors who can agree on Path principles.

### 3.4 Autocratic Systems (Difficult)

| Element | Path Approach |
|---------|---------------|
| Limited engagement | Possible on specific issues |
| No endorsement | Cannot support tyranny |
| Exit support | Help people leave if needed |
| Gradual change | Work for opening over time |

**How to embed Path:** Autocratic systems present the greatest challenge. The Path cannot endorse any system that violates its core prohibitions (no killing, torture, enslavement), and autocracies almost always do. However, practical engagement is sometimes possible on specific issues — environmental cooperation, humanitarian aid, cultural exchange. Focus on supporting exit rights for those who wish to leave, building underground education networks, and applying international pressure through treaties and alliances. Never collaborate with tyrants in ways that legitimize their rule, but do not refuse all contact if it serves the Path's values.

## Conclusion

The Path provides political principles, not a political system. These principles can be applied to any government:

1. **Subsidiarity** — decisions at lowest effective level
2. **Separation of powers** — no concentrated authority
3. **Long-term council** — thinking beyond election cycles
4. **Transparency** — visible to citizens
5. **Right to exit** — freedom to leave

The Path does not tell you what to vote for — it tells you what limits any government must respect and what values it should embody.
