## The Path — Evolutionary Framework of Flourishing

### Why Evolution Is Built Into the Path

No system is perfect. The Path was designed with the recognition that even well-crafted principles may need adjustment as circumstances change, knowledge deepens, and communities grow. Building evolution into the structure prevents stagnation and dogmatism while still protecting the core from arbitrary revision. The core remains fixed to provide stability; everything else adapts to serve flourishing.

---

### The Core

The core (two axioms, three prohibitions, three principles) is fixed. Only a global supermajority can change it. This is intentional: the core provides the foundation of trust and common purpose that holds all communities together.

---

### Changing Non-Core Documents

Local communities may change any document except the core through a five-step process:

1. **Proposal:** A member proposes a change in writing.
2. **Discussion:** The community discusses for at least 2 weeks.
3. **Vote:** A supermajority (2/3) of participating members approves.
4. **Documentation:** The change is logged with the date and vote tally.
5. **Publication:** The change is published in the community log.

**Concrete Example:** A community in the northern hemisphere wants to change its spring festival from March 21 to March 20 because local climate patterns make March 20 more appropriate for celebrating renewal. A member writes a proposal explaining the change and its rationale. The community discusses it during two weekly meetings (or equivalent async discussion period). At the vote, 8 out of 12 members approve, 3 oppose, and 1 abstains—the proposal passes with 67% support. The change is logged: "Festival date changed from March 21 to March 20, effective 2025. Rationale: Local climate alignment." The updated calendar is published in the community log.

**Change Proposal Template:**

```
Proposed change: [Old text] → [New text].
Rationale: [Brief explanation of why this change improves flourishing].
Submitted by: [Name], [Date]
```

**Note:** Any proposed change can be rejected if community members determine it violates the core (the two axioms, three prohibitions, or three principles). The responsibility lies with the voting community to ensure compatibility with the core.

### Changing the Core

The core (two axioms, three prohibitions, three principles) can only be changed through a global process requiring extraordinary consensus:

1. **Proposal:** At least 10 communities independently propose the same change.
2. **Deliberation:** At least 1 year of global discussion, with the proposal published and accessible to all communities.
3. **Vote:** A 90% supermajority of communities must approve.
4. **Implementation:** After a transition period (specified in the vote), the change takes effect.

**Detailed Example:** Suppose a new global crisis emerges—such as a novel form of psychological manipulation that systematically undermines autonomous choice—and many communities conclude that a fourth prohibition is necessary to protect human flourishing. The process would unfold as follows:

- Ten or more communities independently draft proposals for a fourth prohibition against psychological manipulation.
- These proposals are compiled and published globally. For the next 12 months, communities discuss the proposal through forums, regional meetings, and global conferences.
- After the deliberation period, a global vote is held. At least 90% of all communities must approve for the change to pass.
- If it passes, a transition period (e.g., 6–12 months) allows communities to prepare for the new prohibition.

**Cooling-Off Provision:** Any core change proposal must be published for at least 6 months before the first vote. The vote must be held twice, separated by at least 3 months, to confirm that the result is consistent and not a momentary fluctuation. Both votes must achieve 90% supermajority.

The core is deliberately hard to change—not to make it impossible, but to ensure that any change has overwhelming support and widespread understanding. This protects stability while allowing for genuine evolution when circumstances demand it.

### Changelog Requirements

Every community must maintain a public changelog (digital or physical) for all document changes. The changelog must include:

- **Date of change** (YYYY-MM-DD)
- **Old text** (verbatim, or summary if too long)
- **New text** (verbatim, or summary if too long)
- **Vote tally** (for / against / abstain)
- **Rationale** (2–3 sentences explaining the reason)
- **Proposer** (name of member or body that initiated)

The changelog must be accessible to all community members and, where possible, linked to the global network for transparency.

---

### Handling Conflicts Between Communities

When two communities adopt incompatible non-core changes, the following approach applies:

1. **Separate practices permitted:** If the changes affect only internal community behavior (e.g., different festival dates), communities may maintain their own practices. No conflict exists.
2. **Negotiation through mediation:** If the changes affect interactions between communities (e.g., conflicting trade protocols), representatives from both communities should negotiate a common version. A neutral community or mediator may assist.
3. **Core conflict requires global resolution:** If the conflict involves the core—meaning one community's change allegedly violates the core or both claim the core supports different interpretations—only a global supermajority can resolve the dispute.

### Limitations

No system is perfect. The Path acknowledges that a small group of communities could theoretically block a needed core change, even if the majority supports it. Similarly, a determined minority could delay non-core changes through procedural tactics. The Path trusts that communities will act in good faith, prioritizing collective flourishing over narrow self-interest. The high thresholds exist not to prevent change, but to ensure that any change carries the weight of genuine consensus.
