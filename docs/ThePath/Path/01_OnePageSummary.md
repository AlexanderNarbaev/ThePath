## Part I: Introduction — What Is the Path?

### 1.1 The Purpose and Vision of the Path

The Path is a **minimal ethical framework** designed to enable cooperation among all human beings, regardless of their religious beliefs, cultural background, political views, or philosophical worldview. It does not replace existing traditions, religions, or personal belief systems — it provides an **additional layer of agreement** that allows people with fundamentally different views to work together toward common goals.

The central question the Path answers is not "What is the meaning of life?" or "What is the ultimate truth?" Instead, it answers a much more practical question: **"What can we agree on, so that we can cooperate effectively?"**

This framework was developed through extensive research into ethics, political philosophy, game theory, evolutionary biology, and the history of human cooperation and conflict. It synthesizes the best insights from these fields into a simple, practical system that anyone can use.

---

### 1.2 Why a Minimal Framework?

Throughout history, humanity has tried many approaches to creating lasting cooperation:

- **Religious frameworks** — These provide powerful shared narratives and moral guidance, but they require belief in metaphysical claims that not everyone accepts, and they often divide rather than unite people of different faiths.

- **Ideological frameworks** — These provide clear answers and collective goals, but they often become dogmatic, suppress dissent, and lead to totalitarianism when implemented politically.

- **Legal frameworks** — These formalize rules and consequences, but they are reactive (punishing violations rather than promoting positive action) and require enforcement mechanisms that can become oppressive.

- **Scientific frameworks** — These provide accurate descriptions of the world, but they do not prescribe what we should do; they describe what is, not what ought to be.

The Path takes a different approach. It identifies the **minimum necessary agreements** that enable cooperation across all differences. It does not try to answer all questions about life, meaning, or morality — it only tries to establish the red lines that prevent cooperation from breaking down, and the positive direction that guides it forward.

## Part II: The Foundational Axioms

The Path rests on two **pragmatic axioms** — statements that we accept not as revealed truth, but as useful starting points that enable effective action.

### 2.1 Axiom One: We Exist as Conscious Beings in a World Where Our Actions Have Consequences

**The formal statement:** All human beings experience themselves as existing — as subjects who perceive, feel, think, and choose. This experience of selfhood is inescapable, regardless of one's philosophical position on the nature of consciousness or reality. Furthermore, the world responds to our actions in predictable ways: pushing an object causes it to move, speaking kindly strengthens relationships, lying damages trust, harming others creates enemies.

**An intuitive example:** If you push a glass off a table, it falls and breaks — this is Axiom 1 in action in the physical world. If you lie to a friend, trust erodes — this is Axiom 1 in action in the social world. Actions have real consequences, whether we intend them or not.

**Why this matters:**

1. **Ethics becomes rational.** If actions have consequences, then some actions produce suffering (harm to self or others) while others produce flourishing. A purely selfish person who wants to achieve their own goals must account for consequences; a purely altruistic person who wants to help others must also account for consequences.

2. **Rights become practical necessities.** If I harm you, you will likely retaliate, withdraw cooperation, or suffer. Cooperation requires not harming others — not as a metaphysical claim about rights, but as a practical requirement for achieving one's own goals.

3. **Justice becomes possible.** Without consequences, there is no justice — only power. With consequences, we can design systems that deter harmful behavior and reward beneficial behavior.

**Objection response:** Some may argue that the world is deterministic, so our "choices" are illusions. Even if true, this doesn't change the practical need to account for consequences — because we cannot perceive the determined future in advance, we must act as if we have choice.

---

### 2.2 Axiom Two: The Future Is Open and Is Created by Us Through Cooperation, Science, and Awareness

**The formal statement:** The future has not been fixed in advance by any deity, fate, or physical law. While certain outcomes are more probable given certain actions, no outcome is absolutely determined. We create the future through the three engines of progress:

- **Cooperation:** Collective action achieves what individuals cannot — from building cities to curing diseases to exploring space.
- **Science:** Systematic inquiry reveals what works and doesn't work, allowing us to learn from mistakes and build on successes.
- **Awareness:** Mindfulness of consequences allows us to correct course before harm accumulates.

**An intuitive example:** Planning for tomorrow, educating a child, or planting a tree all assume the future can be changed. If the future were fixed, none of these actions would make sense. We create the future through cooperation, science, and awareness — every day, in everything we do.

**Why this matters:**

1. **Hope becomes rational.** If the future were fixed, the only rational response would be fatalistic submission. If the future is open, the only rational response is responsible effort.

2. **Long-term planning becomes meaningful.** Parenting, education, investment, and environmental stewardship only make sense if the future is not determined.

3. **Positive principles become justified.** The three principles (develop yourself, create and help, think long-term) are not arbitrary demands — they are rational responses to an open future.

**Objection response:** Some may argue that the future is determined by physical laws. Even in a deterministic universe, our actions create the future we perceive — because we cannot perceive the "real" future in advance, we must act as if it's open.

## Part III: The Three Absolute Prohibitions

These are not arbitrary moral rules — they are **red lines** that, if crossed, make long-term cooperation impossible.

### 3.1 Why Three Prohibitions?

The three prohibitions (killing, torture, enslavement) are singled out because they are the **three actions that permanently destroy the possibility of cooperation**:

- **Killing** eliminates the person entirely — no cooperation is possible with the dead, and their loved ones become enemies.
- **Torture** destroys the victim's mind and body, creates lifelong enemies, and poisons the social fabric for all witnesses.
- **Enslavement** treats a person as a means rather than an end, destroying their agency, creativity, and motivation — the very qualities that make cooperation valuable.

These three actions create irreversible damage to trust, the foundation of all cooperation. Every other moral wrong can, in principle, be addressed through dialogue, compensation, or change. These three permanently close the door.

---

### 3.2 Prohibition One: Do Not Kill Intentionally (Except Direct, Imminent, Proven Self-Defense)

**Rationale:** Killing destroys the possibility of cooperation with the person killed, their loved ones, and witnesses. It creates cycles of revenge, undermines trust, and sets precedents that lead to more killing.

**The exception:** The prohibition does not apply to **direct, imminent, proven threat to life** — your own or another's. This is not a license to kill, but a recognition that extreme situations exist where avoidance is impossible.

**Requirements for the exception to apply:**

- **Direct:** The threat is present NOW, not in the past or future.
- **Imminent:** The threat is about to happen NOW, not potentially someday.
- **Proven:** The threat is evident to any reasonable observer, not based on speculation or prejudice.

**Post-exception requirement:** There must be a **public review** — a transparent examination of whether the exception truly applied. This prevents the exception from becoming a pretext for murder.

**What the prohibition does NOT permit:**

- Revenge killing (the threat is not imminent)
- Capital punishment (the threat is not direct and imminent)
- Killing for property (the threat is not to life)
- Aggressive war (the threat is not direct and imminent to you personally)
- Killing based on race, religion, or ideology (the threat must be proven individually)

---

### 3.3 Prohibition Two: Do Not Torture

**Rationale:** Torture destroys the victim psychologically and physically, creates enemies (including the victim's loved ones), produces false information (people will say anything to stop torture), desensitizes the torturer to suffering, and makes negotiation impossible.

**There are NO exceptions.** Even if torture "worked" (it doesn't reliably), the prohibition would stand — because it destroys something essential to all cooperation: the trust that you will not be harmed by those with power.

**What torture includes:**

- Physical pain intentionally inflicted for punishment, information, or coercion
- Psychological torment (isolating prisoners, threatening families, etc.)
- Deliberate deprivation of basic needs (food, water, sleep, medical care)

---

### 3.4 Prohibition Three: Do Not Enslave

**Rationale:** Enslavement destroys the enslaved person's agency, creativity, and motivation. It exploits them for short-term gain while destroying long-term value. It creates resentment, rebellion, and cycles of violence.

**What enslavement includes:**

- Forced labor under threat
- Debt bondage (working to pay off debts with no freedom to leave)
- Human trafficking
- Any system that deprives a person of autonomy without their free, informed consent

**What enslavement does NOT include:**

- Voluntary employment (you can leave)
- Contracts with consequences for breaking them (different from slavery)
- Parental authority over children (limited and temporary by design)
- Criminal justice consequences (different from enslavement)

## Part IV: The Three Positive Principles

These are **desirable, not mandatory**. They represent the direction toward which we should move, not commands we must obey.

### 4.1 Principle One: Develop Yourself

**The meaning:** Continuously increase your awareness, empathy, and responsibility. Grow as a person through learning, reflection, and practice.

**Why it matters:** You cannot help others effectively if you have not first developed yourself. An untrained doctor harms more than helps; an unintelligent leader causes more problems than solutions.

**How to apply:**

- Education: Never stop learning
- Self-reflection: Examine your own motivations and biases
- Practice: Develop skills that benefit others
- Mindfulness: Pay attention to your thoughts and actions

---

### 4.2 Principle Two: Create and Help

**The meaning:** Add value to the world. Make things, solve problems, teach knowledge, support others — according to your abilities and opportunities.

**Why it matters:** A world of passive recipients is a world of scarcity. A world of active creators is a world of abundance.

**How to apply:**

- Create: Art, technology, organizations, solutions
- Help: Support others in need (your choice, not your obligation)
- Share: Knowledge, resources, opportunities
- Mentor: Pass on what you've learned

---

### 4.3 Principle Three: Think for the Long-Term Future

**The meaning:** Consider the consequences of your actions for future generations. Invest in things that will benefit your children, grandchildren, and beyond.

**Why it matters:** Short-term thinking leads to environmental collapse, institutional decay, and civilizations that crumble. Long-term thinking builds sustainable societies.

**How to apply:**

- Education: Invest in learning, not just degrees
- Environment: Leave the planet better than you found it
- Institutions: Build organizations that last
- Planning: Consider consequences 10, 50, 100 years out

## Part V: The Daily Practice (2 Minutes)

### 5.1 Morning Intention (1 minute)

Upon waking, before looking at your phone or starting your day:

1. Pause and take one deep breath.
2. Ask yourself three questions:
   - "What kind of person do I want to be today?"
   - "What will I do if I face anger / fear / conflict?"
   - "Whom can I help today?"
3. Set your intention for the day.

### 5.2 Evening Reflection (1 minute)

Before sleeping:

1. Pause and take one deep breath.
2. Ask yourself three questions:
   - "What did I do well today?"
   - "What could I have done better?"
   - "Who did I help or harm today?"
3. Let go of the day and rest.

## Conclusion

The Path is simple but not easy. It asks us to:

1. **Accept** that our actions have consequences
2. **Believe** that we can create a better future
3. **Refrain** from killing, torture, and enslavement
4. **Commit** to developing ourselves, creating value, and thinking long-term

It does not ask us to abandon our beliefs, surrender our reason, or submit to authority. It only asks us to cooperate with people who may be very different from us, on the basis of agreements we can all accept.

**Join the Path — the path to a better future.**
