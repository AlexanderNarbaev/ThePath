## Introduction: What Ethics Means in the Path

Ethics is not a set of abstract philosophical principles debated in university seminars — it is the practical question of **how we should act toward each other** to enable cooperation and flourishing. The Path's ethics is built on a simple foundation: we exist in a world where our actions have consequences, and the future is open for us to shape through our choices.

This document explains how the three prohibitions and three principles apply in practical situations: daily life, professional contexts, conflict resolution, and extreme circumstances. It provides clear guidance while acknowledging that real-world ethics often involves complex trade-offs that require wisdom and judgment.

---

## Part I: The Rationale Behind the Prohibitions

### Why These Three Prohibitions?

The three prohibitions are not arbitrary moral rules imposed by some authority. They are **rational red lines** — actions that, if permitted, make long-term cooperation impossible. They are derived directly from the two axioms:

1. If actions have consequences, then killing, torturing, and enslaving create negative consequences that destroy trust.
2. If the future is open, then we are responsible for shaping it — and these actions shape it toward destruction.

The prohibitions are "absolute" in the sense that they should never be violated except in the narrow self-defense exception. But they are "practical" in the sense that they exist to enable cooperation, not to serve as abstract moral commands.

---

### Prohibition 1: Do Not Kill Intentionally

**The Formal Statement:** You may not intentionally cause the death of another human being, except in the specific case of direct, imminent, proven threat to your own life or the life of another innocent person.

#### 1.1 Why Killing Destroys Cooperation

When someone is killed, several things happen that make future cooperation impossible:

- **With the victim:** Cooperation ends completely. They can no longer contribute, share, or interact.
- **With loved ones:** Family and friends lose trust, may seek revenge, and become potential enemies.
- **With witnesses:** Observers become afraid and less willing to cooperate with the killer.
- **With the killer:** The killer becomes known as someone who kills, affecting all future interactions.

This is why killing is not just another "bad thing" — it is qualitatively different from other harms. It ends the possibility of cooperation permanently with one person and threatens it with many others.

#### 1.2 The Exception: Self-Defense

The prohibition recognizes one exception: **direct, imminent, proven threat to life**. This is not a loophole but a recognition that extreme situations exist where avoidance is genuinely impossible.

**Requirements for the exception to apply:**

| Requirement | Meaning | Example |
|-------------|---------|---------|
| Direct | The threat is present NOW | A knife is being raised against you |
| Imminent | The threat is about to happen NOW | The attack will succeed in seconds |
| Proven | The threat is evident, not imagined | The attacker has intent and means |

**What does NOT qualify:**

- Revenge (the threat is in the past)
- Preemptive strike on someone who might attack (not imminent)
- Killing based on fear of what someone might do (not proven)
- Capital punishment (the threat is not direct and imminent)
- War against a country (not direct and imminent to you personally)

**Post-exception requirement:** There must be a **public review** — a transparent examination by the community of whether the exception truly applied. This prevents the exception from becoming a pretext for murder. The review should examine:

- Was the threat truly direct?
- Was the threat truly imminent?
- Was the threat truly proven?
- Was the force used truly necessary and proportionate?
- Could alternatives have been attempted?

#### 1.3 What Killing Is Prohibited

The prohibition applies to all intentional killing except self-defense:

- **Murder** — Killing for any reason (revenge, profit, anger)
- **Capital punishment** — State killing as punishment
- **Euthanasia without consent** — Killing someone "for their own good" without their request
- **Killing for property** — Murdering someone to get their belongings
- **Killing for ideology** — Murdering based on religious, political, or ethnic grounds
- **Aggressive war** — Starting a war that kills civilians

#### 1.4 Special Cases

**Abortion:** The Path does not take a position on when life begins. It takes a position on the woman's right to choose — because forced pregnancy is a form of removing autonomy (which is close to enslavement). The Path encourages careful reflection on the moral weight of potential life.

**Self-defense against animals:** Killing an attacking animal is not covered by this prohibition. The prohibition applies to **human beings** — animals are not moral agents in the same sense.

**Double effect:** Sometimes an action that causes death is morally permissible if the death is not intended but is an unintended side effect (e.g., destroying military target where civilians might die). The Path recommends **discrimination** (distinguishing combatants from non-combatants) and **proportionality** (the military gain must outweigh the civilian harm).

### Prohibition 2: Do Not Torture

**The Formal Statement:** You may not intentionally inflict severe pain on another human being for any purpose — punishment, information extraction, coercion, or any other reason.

#### 2.1 Why Torture Destroys Cooperation

Torture is even more destructive than killing in some ways:

- **It destroys the victim psychologically and often physically permanently**
- **It creates enemies** — the victim, their loved ones, and all who hear about it
- **It produces false information** — people will say anything to stop the pain, including lies
- **It desensitizes the torturer** — making future cooperation impossible
- **It destroys trust** — the victim (and observers) learn that those with power will cause severe suffering

#### 2.2 Why There Are NO Exceptions

Some argue that torture might be justified to prevent a terrorist attack or extract crucial information. The Path rejects this absolutely for several reasons:

1. **Torture doesn't work reliably.** The information extracted under torture is often false — the victim will say anything to make the pain stop.

2. **It creates more enemies than it prevents.** The tortured person, their family, and their community become lifelong enemies.

3. **It destroys the torturer.** People who torture become desensitized to suffering, affecting all their future interactions.

4. **It sets dangerous precedents.** Once torture is permitted in one case, it becomes easier to justify in others.

5. **It violates the foundation of cooperation** — trust that you won't be harmed by those with power.

The prohibition is absolute because the harm it does to cooperation is absolute. Even if "it works," the prohibition would still stand.

#### 2.3 What Counts as Torture

Torture includes:

- Physical pain deliberately inflicted (beatings, burning, etc.)
- Psychological torment (prolonged isolation, sensory deprivation, threats to family)
- Deliberate deprivation of basic needs (food, water, sleep, medical care)
- Forced positions or movement restrictions that cause severe pain

---

### Prohibition 3: Do Not Enslave

**The Formal Statement:** You may not deprive another human being of their autonomy and freedom without their free, informed consent.

#### 3.1 Why Enslavement Destroys Cooperation

Enslavement is different from other harms because it destroys the victim's ability to cooperate:

- **It destroys agency** — the enslaved cannot make their own decisions
- **It destroys motivation** — forced labor produces far less than willing effort
- **It destroys creativity** — the enslaved cannot innovate or solve problems
- **It creates resentment** — leading to rebellion and violence
- **It reduces total value** — exploitation for short-term gain destroys long-term value

#### 3.2 What Counts as Enslavement

Enslavement includes:

- **Forced labor** — Work under threat of violence or punishment
- **Debt bondage** — Working to pay off debt with no freedom to leave
- **Human trafficking** — Buying and selling people
- **Sexual slavery** — Forced prostitution or sexual labor
- **Child labor** — Children forced to work instead of going to school

#### 3.3 What Does NOT Count as Enslavement

Several things are often confused with enslavement but are different:

- **Voluntary employment** — You choose to work for someone, and can quit
- **Contracts with consequences** — You agree to a job and face consequences if you break it (different from being forced)
- **Parental authority** — Parents have authority over children, but it's limited and temporary by design
- **Criminal justice** — Society imposes consequences on criminals, different from private enslavement
- **Debt** — Owing money is not the same as being enslaved; you have legal rights

## Part II: Application in Daily Life

### How the Prohibitions Apply to Ordinary Situations

#### Road Rage

**Situation:** Someone cuts you off in traffic, and you feel enormous anger.

**Application:** The prohibition requires you not to kill or attack. You may:
- Yell in your car (not ideal, but not prohibited)
- File a complaint with police
- Choose to let it go

You may NOT:
- Ram their car
- Pull them out and attack them
- Follow them and confront them violently

#### Social Conflicts

**Situation:** Someone insults you publicly or harms your reputation.

**Application:** The prohibition requires you not to kill or physically harm. You may:
- Respond with words
- Avoid the person
- Seek mediation
- Take legal action if warranted

You may NOT:
- Physically attack them
- Threaten violence

#### Economic Disputes

**Situation:** A business partner cheats you out of money.

**Application:** The prohibition requires you not to harm them physically. You may:
- Seek legal remedies
- Cut off the relationship
- Damage their reputation through honest communication
- Refuse future business

You may NOT:
- Assault them
- Kidnap them
- Threaten their family

## Part III: Application in Professions

### How the Three Prohibitions Apply to Specific Careers

#### Healthcare (Doctors, Nurses, Therapists)

| Prohibited | Permitted |
|------------|----------|
| Killing patients (except compassionate end-of-life care) | Withdrawing extraordinary measures at patient/family request |
| Torture (forced treatment, non-consensual experiments) | Treatment based on informed consent |
| Enslavement (coerced organ donation, forced research) | Persuading patients to accept treatment |

**Additional obligations:**
- Develop yourself (keep learning)
- Create and help (heal the sick)
- Think long-term (preventive medicine, public health)

#### Education (Teachers, Professors, Trainers)

| Prohibited | Permitted |
|------------|----------|
| Killing (corporal punishment that risks death) | Discipline that doesn't cause physical harm |
| Torture (humiliation, degradation, coercion) | Challenging students academically |
| Enslavement (forced attendance beyond legal, forced labor) | Requiring completion of coursework |

**Additional obligations:**
- Develop yourself (model curiosity)
- Create and help (educate)
- Think long-term (teach how to learn, not just what to memorize)

#### Law Enforcement (Police, Security)

| Prohibited | Permitted |
|------------|----------|
| Killing except in direct imminent threat | Arresting suspects |
| Torture (coerced confessions, excessive force) | Using necessary force for arrest |
| Enslavement (forced labor citations, cash bail traps) | Detaining based on due process |

**Additional obligations:**
- Protect and serve
- Think long-term (community policing, crime prevention)
- Treat all people with dignity

#### Engineering (All Branches)

| Prohibited | Permitted |
|------------|----------|
| Designing things that will kill (unsafe bridges, etc.) | Designing safe structures |
| Creating systems designed to cause suffering | Creating beneficial technology |
| Designing systems that remove human autonomy | Creating tools that enhance choice |

**Additional obligations:**
- Develop skills (continue learning)
- Create useful things
- Think long-term (sustainability, safety)

#### Politics (Elected Officials, Civil Servants)

| Prohibited | Permitted |
|------------|----------|
| Supporting death penalty | Opposing death penalty |
| Supporting torture (solitary confinement, cruel punishment) | Humane imprisonment |
| Supporting enslavement (forced labor, deprivation of rights) | Protecting rights |

**Additional obligations:**
- Develop wisdom
- Create just institutions
- Think long-term (legacies, sustainability)

#### Business (All Levels)

| Prohibited | Permitted |
|------------|----------|
| Killing (dangerous products, unsafe conditions) | Profitable but safe products |
| Torture (hostile work environments, retaliation) | Performance management |
| Enslavement (forced overtime, coercive debt, trafficking) | Fair contracts, voluntary employment |

**Additional obligations:**
- Develop talent
- Create value
- Think long-term (sustainable business)

## Part IV: Conflict Resolution

### How to Resolve Disputes Within the Path

When conflicts arise between people who share the Path, the process is designed to preserve cooperation while addressing grievances.

#### Step 1: Direct Negotiation

The parties try to resolve the issue themselves:
- Identify the **interest** behind each position ("You want money" is a position; "You want security" is an interest)
- Seek **mutual benefit** — solutions where both parties gain
- Communicate **honestly** about needs and concerns

#### Step 2: Mediation

If direct negotiation fails, a neutral third party helps:
- A community member with good standing
- Both parties agree to participate
- The mediator helps identify solutions but doesn't decide

#### Step 3: Arbitration

If mediation fails, a panel decides:
- Three neutral community members
- Both parties present their case
- The decision is binding for both parties
- The decision must be justified in terms of the Path's principles

#### Step 4: Exit

If arbitration fails or is unacceptable:
- Either party may exit the community
- The right to exit is absolute
- The community must help them exit peacefully

#### What the Path Forbids in Conflict

- **Revenge** ("getting even") — We do not punish beyond restoration
- **Retaliation** — We do not "make them pay"
- **Ostracism without due process** — We do not "cancel" people without fair hearing

## Part V: Extreme Situations

### How the Prohibitions Apply in Difficult Circumstances

#### War

The Path does not forbid all violence — it recognizes the legitimate defense of one's community. However, it imposes strict limits:

**Permitted (defensive war):**
- War only as last resort, after diplomacy fails
- Discrimination between combatants and civilians
- Proportionality (response matches the threat)
- Minimum force necessary

**Prohibited:**
- Torture of prisoners
- Enslavement of conquered populations
- Targeting civilians
- Aggressive war (war of conquest or preemptive)

#### Self-Defense (Individual)

The prohibition on killing is relaxed only in direct, imminent, proven threat to life. The exception is narrow:

- Must be **direct** — the threat is present NOW
- Must be **imminent** — the threat is about to happen NOW
- Must be **proven** — the threat is evident, not imagined
- Must be **reviewed afterward** — community examines whether exception applied

#### Revolution

The Path does **not** support violent revolution:
- The Path seeks change through education and example
- Violence creates cycles of violence
- Revolution often produces tyranny worse than what it replaced

The Path supports:
- Peaceful reform
- Exit from unjust systems
- Building alternative communities

## Part VI: Edge Cases and Dilemmas

### How to Handle Difficult Ethical Questions

#### Assisted Suicide

**The Path permits.** The prohibition is on killing *another* without consent. Self-determination over one's own body is fundamental. If a person, in full possession of their faculties, chooses to end their own life, that is their right.

**Requirements:**
- Person must be competent (not coerced, not mentally ill in a way that affects judgment)
- Person must be informed (understand consequences)
- Person must consent freely

See [Document 9] for full guidance on end-of-life choices.

#### Mandatory Vaccination

**The Path recommends but does not mandate.** Vaccination protects the community (principle of helping others). However, forced medical treatment violates bodily autonomy (close to enslavement).

**The Path encourages:**
- Education about benefits and risks
- Incentive programs for vaccination
- Accommodations for those who cannot be vaccinated

**The Path opposes:**
- Forced vaccination
- Discrimination against the unvaccinated in basic services

#### The Death Penalty

**The Path recommends abolition.** It violates the prohibition on killing. It provides no public safety benefit (incapacitation can be achieved without death). It cannot be reversed if wrong.

**However**, the Path does not forcibly prohibit it — it leaves the decision to local communities who must live with the consequences. Communities that retain the death penalty should understand they are violating the spirit of the Path.

#### Climate Change and Environmental Destruction

While not explicitly covered by the three prohibitions, the Path's principles clearly apply:
- **Principle 3 (think long-term)** requires addressing climate change
- **Principle 2 (create and help)** requires environmental restoration
- **Prohibition 3 (don't enslave)** can be extended to "don't enslave future generations" by destroying their environment

## Part VII: The Positive Principles in Ethics

### How the Three Principles Guide Ethical Action

#### Principle 1: Develop Yourself

**In ethics:** To act ethically, you must understand ethics. This requires:
- Learning moral philosophy and its applications
- Developing empathy (the ability to imagine others' perspectives)
- Building moral reasoning skills through practice

**In practice:**
- Read ethical texts
- Reflect on your own moral development
- Seek feedback from others
- Practice ethical behavior in small situations

#### Principle 2: Create and Help

**In ethics:** Ethical behavior is not just about avoiding harm — it's about actively contributing to flourishing. This means:
- Creating value (art, knowledge, technology, relationships)
- Helping those in need (within your abilities)
- Supporting others' moral development

**In practice:**
- Find ways to add value to your community
- Help others who are struggling
- Mentor those learning ethical behavior

#### Principle 3: Think Long-Term

**In ethics:** Short-term thinking leads to collapsed institutions, environmental destruction, and civilizational decline. Ethical behavior requires:
- Considering consequences of actions decades ahead
- Building sustainable systems
- Investing in future generations

**In practice:**
- Consider how your actions affect your children and grandchildren
- Support policies and practices that are sustainable
- Don't sacrifice future well-being for present convenience

## Conclusion

The ethics of the Path is simple in principle but complex in application. It provides enough to guide most decisions — but requires wisdom, empathy, and reflection for edge cases. The key is to remember:

1. **The prohibitions are red lines** — not to be crossed except in genuine self-defense
2. **The principles are directions** — not commands, but guides toward flourishing
3. **Conflict resolution is cooperative** — we solve problems together, not through violence
4. **Extreme cases require community** — no one should decide alone; we need review and accountability
