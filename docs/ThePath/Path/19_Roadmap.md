## The Path — Evolutionary Framework of Flourishing

### Introduction

The Path works incrementally. This roadmap outlines phases for building a just, developing society using the Path.

### Why a roadmap?

Real social change takes time. History shows that rushed revolutions often collapse into tyranny, while slow, patient movements create lasting change. The roadmap provides a realistic path forward without promising quick utopia. It acknowledges that different communities will move at different speeds, and that setbacks are normal. The roadmap is a guide, not a mandate — it helps communities plan, measure progress, and stay grounded in practical action.

## Phase 0: Personal Preparation

**Purpose:** Before any community action, individuals should prepare themselves internally.

**Duration:** 30 days (optional but recommended)

**Actions:**

- Read [Document 1] (One-Page Summary) to understand the core framework.
- Practice the daily intention and reflection for 30 days straight.
- Reflect honestly: Does the Path align with my values? Do the prohibitions and principles make sense to me?
- Discuss with trusted friends or family if helpful.
- No external action required — this is purely internal work.

**Success metrics:**

- Completion of 30 consecutive daily practices.
- Self-assessment of alignment with Path values.

**Note:** This phase is optional but recommended. Skipping it is not penalized — the Path trusts individual judgment.

## Phase 2: Small Communities

**Typical duration:** 2–3 years

**Objectives:**

- Form local groups (2–10 people).
- Meet weekly (Assembly of Reason).
- Establish consensus and conflict resolution.

**Actions:**

- Find or form a small community.
- Establish regular meeting schedule.
- Draft community charter (see [Document 5]).
- Practice weekly Assembly of Reason.

**Success metrics:**

| Indicator | Target |
|-----------|--------|
| Communities formed | 20+ |
| Meetings held (annual) | 40+ |
| Conflict resolution success rate | >80% |

**Common challenges and solutions:**

- **"Finding space to meet"** → Use libraries, parks, online video calls, or rotating homes.
- **"People have different interpretations"** → Return to Document 1 and 2 for shared reference; practice patience.
- **"Group becomes inactive"** → Set minimum attendance expectations; celebrate small wins; rotate facilitation.
- **"Conflict escalates"** → Use the consensus process from Document 5; bring in neutral mediator from another community.

## Phase 3: Local Projects

**Typical duration:** 3–5 years

**Objectives:**

- Community projects (education, health, economic).
- Coordinate with other local communities.
- Begin local influence (schools, businesses).

**Actions:**

- Identify local needs.
- Organize community projects.
- Connect with sister communities.
- Begin outreach to local institutions.

**Success metrics:**

| Indicator | Target |
|-----------|--------|
| Projects completed | 10+ |
| Community satisfaction | >75% |
| Local institutions reached | 5+ (schools, businesses) |

**Common challenges and solutions:**

- **"Not enough resources"** → Start small (even sharing skills counts); seek grants or partnerships.
- **"Projects fail to gain traction"** → Survey community needs first; pilot with low-commitment initiatives.
- **"Competing priorities within community"** → Use Assembly of Reason to vote on priorities; document decisions.
- **"Resistance from local authorities"** → Build relationships slowly; demonstrate value through results; collaborate on shared goals.

| Communities in networks | 100+ |

## Parallel Phases

Communities may work on multiple phases simultaneously. For example:

- Individuals in Phase 1 can also help form small communities (Phase 2).
- Communities in Phase 3 can begin building regional networks (Phase 4).
- Early networks can start institutional advocacy (Phase 5).

The phases are not strictly sequential. The roadmap describes the typical progression, but communities should adapt to their context.

---

## Rolling Back or Pausing

Any community may decide to pause implementation at any phase, or even revert to an earlier phase, without penalty. Reasons may include:

- External pressures (economic, political, personal).
- Internal conflicts that need time to resolve.
- Loss of critical mass of participants.
- Re-evaluation of strategy.

The roadmap is a guide, not a mandate. There is no shame in pausing or reverting. The Path values honesty over forced progress. When ready, communities can resume at any point.

## Comparison with Other Social Change Models

| Model | Speed | Violence | Risk of Tyranny | Sustainability |
|-------|-------|----------|-----------------|----------------|
| Revolution | Fast | High | High | Low (often collapses) |
| Reform | Medium | Low | Medium | Medium (partial change) |
| Gradualist (Path) | Slow | None | Low | High (organic growth) |

The Path's gradualist approach prioritizes stability and lasting change over speed. By avoiding violence and imposed solutions, the Path reduces the risk of creating new forms of oppression while building genuine consensus.
