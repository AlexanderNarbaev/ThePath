Community Charter

How to start or join a community, conflict resolution, and the community charter.

What Is a Path Community?

A Path community is a group of people (typically 2 to 5000+) who:

  1. Share the two axioms — They accept that actions have consequences and that the future is open.
  2. Uphold the three prohibitions — They commit not to kill (except self-defense), torture, or enslave.
  3. Practice the principles — They aspire to develop themselves, create and help, and think long-term.

Starting a New Community

Minimal Community Checklist

  1. Find 2+ people who share the two axioms and three prohibitions
  2. Agree on the three prohibitions — explicit commitment
  3. Decide meeting frequency — weekly is typical
  4. Choose a facilitator (optional, rotates every 1-3 months)
  5. Adopt this charter — optional, can be adapted
  6. Start with daily intention and reflection
  7. Hold weekly Assembly of Reason

Community Structure

No Hierarchy

The Path has no priests, no clergy, no mandatory leaders. Leadership is functional, not spiritual.

Optional Roles

  • Council: 3-7 people for communities with 10+ members
  • Coordinator: Handle day-to-day tasks
  • Facilitator: Guides meetings
  • Treasurer: Manages community funds
  • Events Coordinator: Organizes gatherings

Decision-Making

Consensus (Small Communities)

Everyone must live with the decision. This doesn't mean everyone loves it — just that they can accept it.

Supermajority (Larger Communities)

For communities with 50+ members, use 2/3 supermajority for major decisions.

Membership

Joining

  1. Read the one-page summary
  2. Practice the daily intention and reflection for 30 days
  3. Declare intention to join

Exiting

The right to exit is absolute. Anyone can leave at any time, for any reason, without penalty.

Conflict Resolution

When conflicts arise, the Path provides a clear process:

  1. Direct Negotiation: Parties try to resolve the issue themselves
  2. Mediation: A neutral third party helps facilitate
  3. Arbitration: A panel of three decides
  4. Exit: The right to leave is always available

Sanctions

OffenseResponse
First offensePrivate conversation, reflection
Second offensePublic warning, temporary suspension
Third offenseExpulsion (75% supermajority)

Transparency

Trust requires transparency. Communities commit to:

  • All community funds are transparent
  • Monthly financial reports
  • Minutes of all council meetings available
  • Records kept for at least 7 years

Financial Self-Management

  • No required dues — participation is free
  • Contributions can be voluntary
  • No one excluded for non-payment
