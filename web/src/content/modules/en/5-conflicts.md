## For mentors, mediators, community coordinators, and all who strive for the fair resolution of disputes in the spirit of the Spiral of Consciousness

---

### Introduction

Conflicts are inevitable in any community. Offenses, misunderstandings, breach of commitments, disputes over resources, disagreements in values — all of this can destroy trust and split the community. Traditional approaches (courts, expulsion, punishment) often worsen the split, giving rise to a sense of injustice and a desire for revenge.

**Restorative justice** is an alternative model that places at the center not punishment, but the healing of relationships, the accountability of the offender, and the restoration of harmony in the community. This module provides practical tools for implementing restorative justice in communities of the Spiral of Consciousness, and also defines the boundaries and procedures for interaction with the state judicial system.

## Section 1. The Philosophy of Restorative Justice

### 1.1. Difference from Punitive Justice

| Aspect | Punitive Justice | Restorative Justice |
|--------|------------------|---------------------|
| **Goal** | Punish the guilty, uphold the letter of the law | Restore relationships, heal the community, repair the harm |
| **Who Is at the Center** | The state / judge / law | The victim, the offender, the community |
| **Key Question** | "What rule was broken? What punishment follows?" | "What harm was caused? What needs to be done to repair it?" |
| **Role of the Offender** | Passive recipient of punishment | Active participant in repairing the harm |
| **Role of the Victim** | Often reduced to a witness | Central figure: their voice, needs, and feelings determine the process |
| **Result** | Prison, fine, expulsion, criminal record | Reconciliation, compensation, a plan for correction, restoration of trust |

### 1.2. Key Principles for the Spiral of Consciousness

1.  **Harm is more important than rule-breaking.** We first ask: "Who and how was harmed?" rather than "Which clause of the charter was violated?"
2.  **Accountability is an action, not suffering.** The offender must actively participate in repairing the harm (apologize, compensate for damage, undergo education, perform community service).
3.  **The community is not a judge, but a participant in healing.** The victim and the offender are not left alone; the community supports them and guarantees that the reconciliation will be sustainable.
4.  **Forgiveness is not a mandatory condition, but a possible outcome.** The victim cannot be coerced into forgiveness. But if forgiveness arises naturally — it is the highest form of healing.
5.  **Openness and transparency with privacy protection.** The process is open to community members, but personal details may remain confidential at the request of the parties.

## Section 2. When to Use the Reconciliation Circle

### 2.1. Appropriate Cases

- Minor and medium conflicts between community members:
    - Offenses, slander, gossip.
    - Petty theft or damage to property.
    - Failure to fulfill commitments undertaken.
    - Violation of the community charter (not related to the absolute prohibitions).
    - Disagreements in working groups.
- Situations where there is an injured party ready for dialogue.

### 2.2. Inappropriate Cases (require other measures)

- **Violation of the three absolute prohibitions** (murder, torture, slavery). These cases **cannot be resolved solely within the community**. They require the immediate exclusion of the offender and referral of the case to state law enforcement agencies. A Reconciliation Circle may be conducted only after the court's decision (if permitted by law) and only at the victim's initiative, but does not replace criminal prosecution.
- **Sexual violence.** The victim is not obligated to meet with the offender. A separate support circle for the victim is conducted (see Section 3). The question of referring the case to the police is decided by the victim; the community provides full support for whichever decision they make.
- **Situations where one of the parties is in an extremely vulnerable position** and cannot participate voluntarily (children without parents, people with severe mental disorders without support).

### 2.3. Voluntariness — the Main Condition

**Participation in the Reconciliation Circle is exclusively voluntary.** Neither the victim nor the offender may be coerced. If one of the parties refuses — the community may apply other measures (mediation, arbitration, exclusion), but the Circle will not take place.

## Section 3. Separate Support Circles for Victims

In cases where the victim is traumatized and not ready for an immediate meeting with the offender, or where direct contact may cause additional harm, the community organizes **separate support circles**. This is a mandatory option that the facilitator is obligated to offer before beginning preparation for a joint Circle.

### 3.1. Support Circle for the Victim

- **Participants:** the victim, the facilitator (specially trained or an external mediator), trusted persons of the victim (by their choice, 1–3 people). The offender is not present.
- **Purpose:** to give the victim a safe space to express pain, anger, fears, and to formulate their needs. No pressure, no judgments.
- **Result:** the victim determines what they need for restoration (apologies, guarantees of safety, compensation, public acknowledgment of harm, education of the offender), and whether they are ready for a joint Reconciliation Circle or prefer other measures.

### 3.2. Support Circle for the Offender

- **Participants:** the offender, the facilitator, trusted persons (by the offender's choice).
- **Purpose:** to help the offender acknowledge the harm without aggression and condemnation. To understand motives, to recognize accountability, to develop readiness for correction.
- **Result:** the offender formulates whether they are ready to acknowledge the harm, offer apologies, and participate in the joint Circle.

### 3.3. Decision on Conducting the Joint Circle

After the separate circles, the facilitator assesses the possibility and safety of a joint meeting. If the victim is not ready — the joint Circle is not conducted. Instead, the community may apply arbitration or, in cases requiring state intervention, transmit materials to law enforcement agencies. The decision to refer the case to the state is made with the victim's consent, except in cases where the law obligates reporting of a crime (for example, grave violent crimes).

## Section 4. Preparation for the Joint Reconciliation Circle

### 4.1. Roles and Their Selection

| Role | Who Performs It | Responsibilities |
|------|-----------------|------------------|
| **Facilitator** | A trained mentor (not a party to the conflict) | Open the circle, explain the rules, monitor time, ask questions, ensure a safe atmosphere, close the circle. |
| **Victim (injured party)** | The one who was harmed | Relates their feelings, the harm, what is needed for restoration. |
| **Offender (violator)** | The one who caused the harm | Relates their version, acknowledges (or does not acknowledge) guilt, proposes methods of correction. |
| **Support Circle** | Trusted persons of the victim and the offender (1–2 people each) | Provide emotional support, help formulate thoughts. |
| **Witnesses (as needed)** | Community members who possess information | Share observations, without giving judgments. |

**Important:** the facilitator has no right to judge or dictate a solution. They are a neutral coordinator. In complex cases, it is recommended to invite an external mediator from another community.

### 4.2. Preliminary Conversations

Before the joint circle, the facilitator conducts **separate conversations with each party** (30–60 minutes each). Goals:

- Explain the principles of restorative justice.
- Ensure that the parties are participating voluntarily.
- Clarify expectations, fears, needs.
- Prepare for the fact that the dialogue may be emotional.
- For the victim: "What do you need to feel restoration?"
- For the offender: "Are you ready to acknowledge the harm and to repair it?"

If during the preliminary conversation it becomes clear that one of the parties is categorically opposed — the joint circle is not conducted.

### 4.3. Space and Symbols

- **Place:** a cozy room, chairs in a circle (without tables, to avoid barriers).
- **Center of the circle:** a candle (symbol of truth and attention) or a meaningful object.
- **"Talking piece"** (a staff, stone, soft toy) — only the person holding it may speak. Passed around the circle or upon request.

## Section 5. Scenario of the Joint Reconciliation Circle (Step by Step)

**Total duration:** 1.5–2.5 hours.

### 5.1. Opening (10 minutes)
- The facilitator welcomes everyone, lights the candle.
- States the rules: speak in turn, do not interrupt, maintain confidentiality of personal details, the goal is to restore, not to punish.

### 5.2. The Victim's Account (15–20 minutes)
- The facilitator passes the talking piece to the victim.
- The victim relates: what happened, what they feel, what harm was caused, what is needed for restoration.
- The offender and witnesses listen in silence.

### 5.3. The Offender's Account (15–20 minutes)
- The offender relates their version, what they felt, whether they acknowledge the harm, what they are ready to do.

### 5.4. Questions from the Circle (10–15 minutes)
- Witnesses and the support circle may ask clarifying questions (without accusations).

### 5.5. Dialogue Between the Victim and the Offender (15–20 minutes)
- The facilitator offers the parties the opportunity to speak directly. Possible questions: "What do you want to say to the offender now?" "What do you want to say to the victim?"

### 5.6. Seeking an Agreement (20–30 minutes)
- The parties propose concrete steps for repairing the harm (apologies, compensation, community service, education, guarantees of non-repetition).
- The agreement must be concrete, measurable, feasible.
- The facilitator writes down the points and reads them aloud.

### 5.7. Closing (10 minutes)
- If an agreement is reached, the parties sign it. The facilitator extinguishes the candle: "The circle is closed. Restoration has begun."
- If no agreement is reached, the facilitator records the disagreements and refers the case to arbitration or, if necessary, to state authorities.

### 5.8. Post-Circle (follow-up after 1–4 weeks)
- The facilitator checks compliance with the agreement. If it is not fulfilled, a repeat circle is conducted or arbitration is initiated.

## Section 6. Code of Conduct for the Reconciliation Circle Facilitator

The facilitator is the key figure. Their neutrality and ethical conduct guarantee the fairness of the process. Violation of the code leads to removal from facilitating circles and disciplinary accountability.

| Principle | What It Means | Example of Violation |
|-----------|---------------|----------------------|
| **Neutrality** | The facilitator does not take anyone's side, does not express personal sympathies or antipathies, has no conflict of interest. | The facilitator is a close friend of one of the parties. |
| **Non-judgment** | The facilitator does not give moral evaluations of actions, does not say "that's bad" or "you're wrong." They ask questions. | "Do you realize that you acted horribly?" |
| **Prohibition on Pressure** | The facilitator does not coerce acknowledgment of guilt, forgiveness, or apologies. Everything must come from the parties. | "You have to apologize, or we won't finish." |
| **Ensuring Safety** | The facilitator ensures that no one is subjected to threats, insults, or humiliation. If escalation occurs, they stop the process. | Allowing the offender to raise their voice at the victim. |
| **Confidentiality** | The facilitator does not disclose personal details heard in the circle without the consent of the parties (except in cases involving a crime subject to mandatory reporting). | Sharing details of a family conflict in the community chat. |
| **Professionalism** | The facilitator is trained, respects timing, keeps minutes, does not permit discrimination. | Ignoring the cultural specificities of a party. |

**Consequences of violations:**
- One of the parties may demand the replacement of the facilitator before the circle begins.
- If a violation occurs during the circle, the process is suspended. The community council reviews the complaint and may appoint a new facilitator or an external mediator.
- Systematic violations lead to the revocation of the right to facilitate circles.

## Section 7. Mechanism for Appealing a Reconciliation Circle Decision

Any of the parties has the right to appeal the process or the result if they consider that:

- The rules were violated (coercion, facilitator bias, absence of safety).
- The agreement was imposed by deception or under pressure.
- New significant circumstances have come to light.

**Appeal procedure:**
1.  **Submitting a complaint:** within 14 days after the circle, the party submits a written complaint to the community council specifying the reasons.
2.  **Review by the council:** within 7 days, the council reviews the complaint and is entitled to request explanations from the facilitator and participants.
3.  **Appointment of a review:** if the complaint is found to be well-founded, the council appoints an **external mediator** (from another community or a professional facilitator) to conduct a repeat circle or arbitration.
4.  **Final decision:** the result of the review is final at the community level. If the party is not satisfied, they may appeal to a state court (if the case has legal consequences).

## Section 8. Unified Algorithm for Exclusion of a Community Member

Exclusion is an extreme measure, meaning the termination of membership and all associated rights. The community does not apply violence and does not deprive a person of personal safety.

### 8.1. Grounds for Exclusion

1.  **Violation of the three absolute prohibitions (Level 4 on the scale of consequences, Module 2).** Entails immediate exclusion. The procedure is expedited.
2.  **Grave, systematic violation of the charter or principles (Level 3),** if Reconciliation Circles, mediation, and arbitration have not yielded results.

### 8.2. Expedited Procedure for Violation of the Three Prohibitions

1.  **Recording:** any community member who witnesses or receives reliable information about a violation immediately reports it to the community council.
2.  **Emergency council session:** within 24 hours, the council convenes and reviews the evidence. If the violation is obvious or admitted by the offender, the council issues a decision on exclusion by a majority of no less than 2/3 of the votes.
3.  **If there are doubts,** within 3 days, a general assembly is convened. Testimony is heard, the offender is given the floor. The decision on exclusion is taken by a 2/3 majority of the members present.
4.  **Notification of state authorities:** immediately after the decision on exclusion, the community submits a statement to law enforcement agencies. The community does not shelter a criminal.
5.  **Guarantees:** the person being excluded has the right to a defender from among community members and to appeal the decision to the community civil assembly (if one is provided for) or to external arbitration.

### 8.3. Procedure for Exclusion on Other Grounds

1.  **Initiation:** a proposal for exclusion may be brought by the community council, the circle coordinator, or a group of no less than 10% of the members.
2.  **Proceedings:** within 14 days, a council session or general assembly is held. The member is given the opportunity to speak and present witnesses.
3.  **Vote:** the decision is taken by a 2/3 majority of the council (or general assembly, if the council so decides).
4.  **Right to appeal:** the excluded person may appeal to the civil assembly or an external arbitrator.

## Section 9. Interaction with the State Judicial System

The Spiral recognizes the state as the lawful holder of the monopoly on legitimate violence (within the framework of the three prohibitions). The community does not replace the court and does not obstruct justice.

### 9.1. When the Community Is Obligated to Refer a Case to the State

- In any violation of the three prohibitions (murder, torture, slavery, as well as attempted commission thereof) — immediately.
- Upon receiving information about grave crimes prosecuted by law (rape, grievous bodily harm, human trafficking, etc.) — in accordance with legislation, even if the parties to the conflict prefer internal resolution. The community cannot cover up crimes.

### 9.2. How Reconciliation Circles Can Complement Official Justice

- **At the pre-trial stage (or in parallel):** the parties may undergo a Reconciliation Circle, and its results (an agreement on compensation of harm, reconciliation) may be presented to the court as a mitigating circumstance. This is possible only in cases where reconciliation of the parties is permitted.
- **After sentencing:** a Reconciliation Circle can help the victim and the community cope with the consequences of the crime, and help the convicted person to acknowledge the harm and prepare for return to society after serving their sentence.
- **Mediation in civil disputes:** the community may act as a mediator in property, neighbor, and family disputes, helping the parties reach an agreement without going to court. If an agreement is reached, it may be notarized or approved by the court as a settlement.

### 9.3. Limitations

- The community cannot compel participation in a Circle if one of the parties is the state or its representative.
- If the state itself systematically violates the three prohibitions (torture, extrajudicial executions), interaction with the judicial system may be unethical or dangerous. Under such conditions, the community acts according to Module 4 (repressive state), and its priority is the protection of people, not cooperation with illegitimate institutions.

## Section 10. Mediation as an Intermediate Stage

**When to use mediation instead of a circle:** low-intensity conflict, the parties want confidentiality, do not want to involve many people.
**Role of the mediator:** a neutral mentor, helps the parties reach an agreement, does not issue decisions.
**Simple structure:** joint statement, each person's account, identification of interests, generation of options, choice of a solution, recording.

---

## Section 11. Arbitration (An Extreme Measure)

**When it is applied:** the conflict has not been resolved by mediation and Circles; a party refuses voluntary participation; a binding decision is required for the safety of the community.
**Composition of the arbitration commission:** 3 community members not involved in the conflict.
**Procedure:** application, appointment, hearing (statements, questions), decision by majority vote within 3 days, announcement.
**Possible decisions:** obligation to apologize, compensation, community service, temporary restriction of rights, exclusion.
**Appeal:** only on procedural violations.

## Section 13. Checklist for the Reconciliation Circle Facilitator

- [ ] I have completed training and certification (no less than 8 hours + practice).
- [ ] I am not in a conflict of interest with any of the parties.
- [ ] The parties are participating voluntarily (preliminary conversations have been conducted).
- [ ] Separate support circles have been offered (where appropriate).
- [ ] The space has been prepared (circle, candle, talking piece, minutes template).
- [ ] I know the plan of action if the parties refuse to continue.
- [ ] I have the contacts of a lawyer and a psychologist in case of emergency situations.
- [ ] I pledge to abide by the Facilitator's Code of Conduct.

## Section 14. Common Mistakes and How to Avoid Them

| Mistake | Consequence | How to Avoid |
|---------|-------------|--------------|
| The facilitator gives advice instead of asking questions | Pressure on the parties | Use only open-ended questions. |
| Interrupting, cutting off | Escalation | Strictly observe the rule of the talking piece. |
| Coercion to forgive | Secondary trauma for the victim | Never say "you must forgive." |
| Ignoring power imbalance | Unfair agreement | Conduct separate circles, invite an external mediator. |
| Lack of follow-up on compliance | The conflict returns | Appoint someone responsible for monitoring, set deadlines. |
| The circle turns into a trial | The offender becomes defensive, the process fails | At the beginning, remind of the goals, moderate accusations. |

## Section 15. Training Mentors for Facilitating Circles

**Requirements for certification:**
- Completion of a training course of no less than 8 hours (theory of restorative justice, role-playing, analysis of real cases).
- Practice: co-facilitation of at least 3 circles under the supervision of an experienced facilitator.
- Knowledge of the Facilitator's Code of Conduct and the mechanism for appealing decisions.

**Training topics:**
- Philosophy and principles of restorative justice.
- Trauma-informed facilitation.
- Working with power imbalances.
- Cultural sensitivity in conflict resolution.
- Interaction with the state judicial system.

**Supervision:** facilitators undergo supervision at least once every 6 months (group discussion of complex cases with an experienced mentor). Without regular supervision, the right to facilitate circles may be suspended.

### 16.2. Case Study: Joint Work of the Circle and State Justice

**Situation:** In community N, a case of theft of a significant sum from the community fund by a trusted member was recorded. The community council recorded the violation, excluded the member from the community, and submitted a statement to the police. In parallel, a support circle was organized for community members who had lost trust and felt betrayed. After the court's decision (a suspended sentence with an obligation to compensate the damage), at the victim's initiative, a Reconciliation Circle was conducted where the offender publicly acknowledged the harm, offered a concrete plan for compensation, and committed to community service. The court took the results of the circle into account when making its decision. The offender gradually restored his reputation, though he was not reinstated to the community.

## Frequently Asked Questions and Practical Cases

### 1. "What to do if the offender refuses to participate in the Reconciliation Circle?"

The offender's refusal is not a dead end, but a fork in the road. The Reconciliation Circle rests on voluntariness (Section 2.3): coercion to participate destroys the very idea of restorative justice and often leads to a simulation of remorse. If the offender refuses, the algorithm is as follows: (1) the facilitator conducts a preliminary conversation to understand the reason for the refusal — perhaps it is fear, shame, distrust of the process, rather than an absence of conscience; (2) if the refusal persists, the community moves to the next tool — mediation (Section 10) or arbitration (Section 11), which do not require the consent of both parties to the same degree; (3) if the conflict involves a violation of the three prohibitions or a grave crime — the case is referred to state authorities (Section 9.1), and the offender's refusal of internal resolution does not relieve the community of the duty to report the crime. Important: non-participation in the Circle must not be interpreted as an automatic admission of guilt or grounds for expulsion — it only means that this particular tool is inapplicable in this particular case, and the community seeks another path to justice.

### 2. "Can a Reconciliation Circle replace a court?"

No, and this is a principled position of the Spiral (Section 9). A Reconciliation Circle can complement, but not replace, the state judicial system. In cases that are not criminal offenses (everyday offenses, petty theft, breach of commitments within the community), the Circle can indeed be the primary method of resolution — and often proves more effective than a court, because it restores relationships rather than simply punishing. But if a crime prosecutable by law has been committed — especially a violent one — the community is obligated to refer the case to state authorities. In parallel, a Support Circle for the victim (Section 3.1) may be conducted, or, after the trial, a Reconciliation Circle to aid in the healing of the community (Section 9.2). The Spiral does not create a parallel jurisdiction, does not shelter criminals, and does not arrogate to itself a monopoly on justice. Its task is to complement the state system where the punitive approach is insufficient: in restoring trust, acknowledging harm, and preventing recurrence.

### 3. "What if the victim is afraid of meeting the offender, even in a Circle?"

This is precisely why the mechanism of separate support circles was introduced (Section 3). The victim is never obligated to meet the offender face to face if it threatens their psychological safety. Order of actions: (1) the facilitator offers to conduct a Support Circle for the victim without the offender's presence — there, in a safe setting, the victim formulates their needs; (2) in parallel, a Support Circle for the offender may be conducted to help them acknowledge the harm; (3) if the victim is not ready for a joint meeting, the joint Circle is not conducted — this is written directly into the procedure (Section 3.3). Instead of a joint Circle, the community may apply arbitration, mediation through an intermediary (the facilitator conveys information between the parties without their personal meeting), or written apologies and compensation. Remember: the victim's safety is the first priority. Pressure along the lines of "you must meet and forgive" is a violation of the Facilitator's Code of Conduct (Section 6, principle "Prohibition on Pressure") and itself requires disciplinary proceedings.

### 4. "What if the Circle's decision seems unfair to one of the parties?"

The appeal mechanism is set out in Section 7. If you believe that the result of the Circle was imposed, the process was biased, or new circumstances have come to light — you have 14 days to submit a written complaint to the community council. Important: the complaint must not be about "I didn't like the decision," but about specific procedural violations (facilitator bias, coercion, deception, concealment of information). The council reviews the complaint within 7 days and, if it is well-founded, appoints an external mediator from another community for a repeat review. This is a key element of fairness: an independent view from outside breaks the "dead end of one's own" and gives a chance for reconsideration. If after the review the party is still unsatisfied and the case has legal consequences — they have the right to appeal to a state court (Section 9.2). The community does not impede this: the right to state protection is one of the fundamental rights that the Spiral recognizes and respects.

### 5. "How to resolve conflicts in a community where there is a power imbalance (for example, a boss and a subordinate)?"

Power imbalance is one of the main risks to the fairness of the Circle, and Section 14 ("Common Mistakes") directly states: ignoring the imbalance of power leads to unfair agreements. When a boss and subordinate, a mentor and student, a coordinator and an ordinary member participate in a Circle — the formal equality of the procedure is deceptive. What to do: (1) be sure to invite an external mediator from another community — one's own facilitator may involuntarily or under duress play along with the one who has more influence; (2) conduct separate support circles before the joint one (Section 3) — this will give the less powerful party an opportunity to speak out without fear of the "boss's" reaction; (3) in the joint Circle, the facilitator must explicitly articulate the fact of the imbalance and the rules for compensating for it (for example, the more powerful party speaks second, their word is not "heavier"); (4) the agreement must include verifiable commitments for both parties, so that the more powerful participant cannot "promise and forget." If the power imbalance is so great that any procedure would be a formality — the Circle is most likely inappropriate, and the community should use arbitration (Section 11) with an external composition of the commission.
