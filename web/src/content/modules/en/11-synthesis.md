### Introduction

Humanity is not a single monolithic culture but a constellation of civilizations, each possessing a unique language, history, system of knowledge, and conception of a worthy life. Attempts to impose one model as "universal" have invariably led to cultural imperialism, the suppression of dissent, and the loss of immensely valuable alternative ways of being. At the same time, isolation and mutual distrust (the "clash of civilizations") lead to conflicts, the dissipation of common resources, and an inability to solve planetary problems.

The "Spiral of Consciousness" proposes a third path: **civilizational dialogue**, in which no culture loses its identity, yet together they all create a more robust and wiser fabric of pan-human knowledge and cooperation. This module provides concrete tools and protocols for such dialogue — from the interpersonal to the inter-state level.

**Key position:** The Spiral does not serve any one civilization and does not promote any country's "national values" as universal. It is a **bridge** — a meeting space where every culture is valuable in and of itself and can enrich others without dominating.

## Section 1. From Universalism and Isolation to Civilizational Dialogue

### 1.1. Two Dead-End Extremes

- **Liberal universalism:** imposing the Western model of human rights, democracy, and the market as the only correct one. It ignores collectivist, spiritual, and communal values, provoking resistance and rejection.
- **Civilizational isolationism:** the notion of an inevitable "clash of civilizations," the building of walls, the refusal of dialogue. It leads to an arms race, xenophobia, and an inability to solve global problems (climate, pandemics, AI).

The Spiral rejects both extremes. It recognizes that truth does not precede dialogue but is born within it. No civilization holds a monopoly on morality, knowledge, or the future.

### 1.2. Civilizational Dialogue as an Alternative

Civilizational dialogue is a process of equal communication between cultures, in which:

1.  **Each participant preserves their identity.** No one is obliged to renounce their traditions, language, or faith.
2.  **The goal is not victory, but mutual understanding and the common good.** Dialogue is aimed at solving shared problems (ecology, technology, security) and at mutual enrichment.
3.  **The rules of dialogue are developed jointly.** Procedural norms are not imposed by one side.
4.  **The asymmetry of historical power is acknowledged.** Colonialism, racism, and economic dominance have left deep wounds. Dialogue cannot be blind to this — it must include the acknowledgment and redress of historical injustice.

## Section 2. Ubuntu and Sobornost: Two Ethics of Unification Without Uniformity

Two great philosophical traditions — the African Ubuntu and the Russian sobornost — serve the Spiral as key bridges toward building an ethics of a multipolar world. They differ in origin, but are remarkably resonant in the main thing: the person does not stand in opposition to the community but flourishes within it; freedom does not equal isolation; human dignity is inseparable from the dignity of others.

### 2.1. Ubuntu: A Person Through Other People

- **Essence:** Umuntu ngumuntu ngabantu — "a person becomes a person through other people." My humanity is inextricably bound to yours.
- **Application in global dialogue:** international relations cannot be built solely on interests and power. They must account for shared humanity. Sanctions, bombings, economic strangulation — these are not only pragmatic instruments but also the destruction of the fabric of common humanity.

### 2.2. Sobornost: Unity in Diversity

- **Essence:** free unity in love, not coercive uniformity. Every person is unique, yet their uniqueness is revealed only in communion with others. Truth is known not by an individual mind, but by the "conciliar mind" — the whole community.
- **Application in global dialogue:** the international community is neither a mechanical sum of atomized states nor a hierarchical empire. It is a living body, where each organ (civilization) performs its unique function. Decisions affecting all must be made soborno — through dialogue, not through the dictate of one superpower.

### 2.3. Synthesis of Ubuntu and Sobornost in the "Spiral"

Both ethics set a common vector:
- **Against isolation:** we are bound by an invisible web, and harm to one node reverberates throughout the entire network.
- **Against uniformity:** diversity is not a bug but a condition of resilience. A monoculture dies first.
- **For common cause:** humanity is capable of uniting not against a common enemy, but for a common project (space, climate, preservation of consciousness).

## Section 3. Two-Eyed Seeing and Braiding Knowledge: Protocols for Inter-Civilizational Synthesis

### 3.1. Two-Eyed Seeing (Concept of the Mi'kmaq People)

**Principle:** "Learn to see the world through one eye — the strength of science and Western knowledge, and through the other eye — the strength of Indigenous and traditional knowledge. Use both eyes for the benefit of all."

This principle scales to the inter-civilizational level:
- **One eye:** the scientific method, human rights, technological innovations.
- **The other eye:** traditional ecological knowledge, communal governance practices, spiritual traditions, alternative models of economy.

Neither eye is "dominant." Reality is seen more fully with two eyes.

### 3.2. Braiding Knowledge at the Inter-State Level

This is a methodology for the co-creation of knowledge and political decisions, applicable not only in research but also in diplomacy.

**Key Stages for an Inter-State Project:**

| Stage | Actions |
|-------|---------|
| **0. Acknowledging Power and History** | Open discussion of historical grievances, asymmetries, the colonial past. Without this stage, trust is impossible. |
| **1. Joint Definition of Goals** | Each side formulates its goals. Intersections are sought. Goals are fixed in a written agreement with a right of exit. |
| **2. Mapping Knowledge** | Each side presents its methods of analysis and its sources of knowledge (scientific models, traditional practices, statistics, oral testimonies). |
| **3. Joint Analysis** | Data are not coercively translated into a single format. Divergences are interpreted as a source of new knowledge, not as an error by one side. |
| **4. Formulation of Decisions** | Decisions are made by consent (no reasoned objections), not by majority vote, which can ignore a minority. |
| **5. Joint Ownership of Results** | Project results (data, reports, technologies) belong to all participants. Publications — in the languages of all sides. |
| **6. Regular Reflection** | Once a year — a circle on: "Are we reproducing old hierarchies? Are all voices heard?" |

### 3.3. Prohibited Practices

- Extraction of knowledge without consent and without fair compensation.
- Publication of results without joint authorship.
- Imposition of one side's methodology as the only "scientific" one.

## Section 4. Council of Civilizations at the UN: Proposal of the "Spiral"

Contemporary international institutions are built on the principle of national sovereignty: each state has one vote, regardless of whether it represents an entire civilization or a small enclave. This creates both problems of representativeness and problems of cultural depth. States come and go; civilizations live for millennia.

The Spiral proposes supplementing the existing system with a **Council of Civilizations** — a consultative and ethical body at the UN.

### 4.1. Status and Composition

- **Status:** a consultative body attached to the UN General Assembly. It has no veto power over Security Council resolutions, but it possesses the right of ethical assessment and public report.
- **Composition:** representatives of the main civilizational areas (for example: Western, Latin American, African, Islamic, Indian, Chinese/Confucian, Orthodox/Eurasian, Buddhist, Pacific, Indigenous peoples). The specific list is developed through dialogue.
- **Criteria of representation:** representatives are nominated not by states, but by a network of universities, cultural institutes, and recognized spiritual leaders of each area. They are not state officials.

### 4.2. Functions

1.  **Ethical expertise.** Evaluation of major international projects (genetic engineering, strong AI, space colonization, geoengineering) from the perspective of long-term consequences for humanity and the ethical norms of different civilizations.
2.  **Civilizational dialogue.** A platform for informal meetings and discussion of conflicts between civilizations before they escalate into military clashes.
3.  **Report "State of Human Diversity."** An annual public report on the preservation of languages, cultural practices, traditional knowledge, and threats of cultural imperialism.
4.  **Mediation in value-based conflicts.** When an international dispute has a deep-seated value nature (e.g., human rights vs. traditional ways of life), the Council may propose deliberation procedures involving all affected parties.

### 4.3. Limitations

The Council of Civilizations does not substitute the UN Security Council in matters of the use of force. It is not a "world government" and does not possess coercive powers. Its strength lies in moral authority, the quality of its expertise, and its ability to formulate pan-human ethical orientations rooted in the wisdom of different traditions.

## Section 5. Protocols for Inter-State Synthesis of Knowledge

These protocols may be used when concluding bilateral and multilateral agreements on matters of science, ecology, technology, and culture.

### Protocol 1. Preliminary Acknowledgment of Historical Injustice
Before starting any project, the parties publicly acknowledge:
- The facts of colonialism, genocide, slavery, and cultural suppression in the history of their relations.
- The asymmetry of resources and power in the present.
- The obligation not to use current dominance to impose decisions.

### Protocol 2. Equitable Mapping of Knowledge
- Each side presents its sources of knowledge as equally valid.
- Western science is not declared the "gold standard."
- Traditional knowledge is neither romanticized nor discriminated against.

### Protocol 3. Braiding of Results
- Results are published in the languages of all sides.
- Formats: not only scholarly articles but also oral reports, maps, rituals, video diaries.
- Joint authorship and joint ownership of intellectual property.

### Protocol 4. Right of Exit and Data Withdrawal
Any party may exit the project, taking their data and knowledge with them. This prevents a situation where a weaker party finds itself locked into disadvantageous cooperation.

### Protocol 5. Regular Reflection on Power
Every 6–12 months, participants gather and openly discuss: are old hierarchies being reproduced in the project, are the voices of less influential participants being silenced.

## Section 7. Checklist for an Inter-Civilizational Project

Before starting any project involving different cultures or states:

- [ ] The history of relations (including colonialism, racism, exploitation) has been studied and publicly acknowledged.
- [ ] Free, prior, and informed consent (FPIC) obtained from all communities.
- [ ] The steering committee includes members of all affected cultures.
- [ ] Project goals jointly formulated in an equal circle.
- [ ] The knowledge of all sides recognized as equally valid; Western science does not monopolize methodology.
- [ ] A protocol for joint ownership of results has been developed.
- [ ] A mechanism for exit and data withdrawal has been provided.
- [ ] Regular circles of reflection on power dynamics have been scheduled.
- [ ] The project does not serve to promote one culture at the expense of others.

## Section 8. Conclusion: The Spiral as a Bridge

The Spiral of Consciousness is neither a Western, nor an Eastern, nor anyone else's project. It does not promote the "national values" of any country as a universal standard. It does not serve geopolitical interests. It is a bridge.

A bridge does not belong to either shore. It connects them, allowing free passage in both directions. It does not force travelers to renounce their homeland. It simply offers the chance to meet and to see in each other not an enemy, not a competitor, but a fellow conscious node who has chosen the pain of growth.

In an era when the forces of degradation seek to set civilizations against one another, building bridges is not a utopia but a strategy of survival and flourishing.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### 1. "Isn't the Spiral an attempt to impose Western values on other cultures?"

No, and here is why. First, the Three Absolute Prohibitions (do not kill, do not torture, do not enslave) are not "Western" — they appear in the ethical systems of all major civilisations: in Confucianism, in Ubuntu, in Islamic jurisprudence, in Buddhist ahimsa. The Spiral does not invent them; it illuminates them as the pan-human minimum. Second, the Spiral explicitly rejects Western universalism as a form of cultural imperialism (Section 1.1) and insists on civilisational dialogue, where no culture is the "gold standard." Third, the two key ethical pillars of the Spiral — African Ubuntu and Russian sobornost — are deliberately chosen as non-Western traditions, to demonstrate that a deep ethics of unity without uniformity exists in many cultures. The Spiral is not a Western project in Eastern packaging, but a conscious attempt to build a bridge where every shore is of equal worth. If someone uses the Spiral to promote Western or any other "national values" — they violate its spirit.

### 2. "How do Ubuntu and sobornost fit with individual freedom?"

This apparent contradiction is a product of the Western dichotomy of "the individual versus society." Both Ubuntu ("a person becomes a person through other people") and sobornost ("unity in diversity, where the person does not dissolve but flourishes") proceed from a different premise: the person neither precedes nor opposes the community — they *emerge* in communion and *unfold* through it. Imagine a musician in an orchestra: they are free enough that their part can be heard, and constrained enough not to drown out others — yet it is precisely within these bounds that a symphony is born, impossible for a soloist. Individual freedom in these ethics is not freedom *from* others (isolation), but freedom *with* and *for* others. This does not negate personal boundaries or the right to solitude, but places them in context: you are free not when you are alone, but when your uniqueness is recognised and called for by a community that does not absorb you.

### 3. "What about cultural practices that contradict the Spiral's prohibitions?"

This is the most difficult question, and the Spiral offers no simple answer. The Three Prohibitions are absolute — this means that no cultural tradition can serve as justification for torture, killing, or enslavement. Female genital mutilation, ritual killing, caste enslavement, "honour killings" — the Spiral cannot recognise them as acceptable, even if they are hallowed by tradition. But — and this is critically important — the *method* of abandoning such practices cannot be neo-colonial. One cannot simply prohibit from the outside: that breeds resistance and drives the practice underground. What is needed is internal dialogue within the culture itself — with the participation of its bearers, especially women and youth, who are often the primary victims of such practices. The protocols of inter-civilisational synthesis (Section 3) offer a methodology: acknowledgment of history, joint mapping of knowledge, deliberation, the right of voice for all affected parties. Changing a cultural practice is work for generations, and it can proceed only from within, with support from outside, but not under external dictate.

### 4. "Isn't the Council of Civilizations at the UN a utopia?"

The Council of Civilizations is not a utopia in the sense of an "impossible dream," but a project analogous to already existing institutions. The UN already has consultative bodies with moral authority but without coercive force: the Permanent Forum on Indigenous Issues, the Commission on Human Rights (prior to its replacement by the Council). The Council of Civilizations is proposed precisely as a consultative, ethical body — without veto power over Security Council resolutions, without an army, without a budget comparable to military expenditures. Its strength lies in the quality of its expertise, the public nature of its reports, and its capacity to create space for dialogue before a conflict escalates into a military phase. Utopia is expecting it to solve all problems. Realism is recognising that humanity today has no institution representing the interests of civilisations as integral cultural realms, not merely states. The Council of Civilizations is a step toward filling this gap, not a replacement for the existing security architecture.
