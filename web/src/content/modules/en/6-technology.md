## For mentors, coordinators, civil servants, and all who make decisions about the development and deployment of technologies in the spirit of the Spiral of Consciousness

---

### Introduction

Technology is not neutral. Every technology carries the values of its creators and embeds itself into existing structures of power. The Spiral of Consciousness neither rejects technology nor worships it. It offers an **ethical filter** through which every technological decision must pass — from a recommendation algorithm to a weapons system, from gene editing to a citizen's digital profile.

This module is not tied to the technological sovereignty of any single country. Its principles are universal and applicable to the technological policy of any state striving for ethical governance. It does not serve anyone's national interests, but sets boundaries that no one may cross.

**Three transversal questions for any technology:**
1. Does it serve the preservation and multiplication of consciousness?
2. Does it violate any of the Three Prohibitions?
3. Does it strengthen human autonomy or subjugate it?

## Section 1. General Principles of the Spiral's Technology Ethics

### 1.1. Technology as an Extension of Human Will

Technology is not an autonomous force; it is materialised intention. It does not "spin out of control" by itself — it is driven there. Therefore, the ethical evaluation of a technology begins not with the code, but with the question: **who is creating and deploying it, for what purpose, and with what values?**

### 1.2. Extension of the Three Prohibitions to Technology

| Prohibition | Technological Embodiment |
|-------------|--------------------------|
| **Do not kill** | Prohibition on the creation and use of fully autonomous lethal systems (LAWS), algorithms that incite mass violence, weaponry that leaves no time for a human ethical decision. |
| **Do not torture** | Prohibition on systems of total surveillance, digital humiliation, psychological manipulation, "digital dungeons," algorithmic torture. |
| **Do not enslave** | Prohibition on technologies that deprive a person of autonomy without voluntary informed consent: compulsory biometrics, social credit systems, punitive disconnection from vital services, forced chipping, digital serfdom. |

### 1.3. The Precautionary Principle

If there is a substantiated risk of serious or irreversible harm — even without full scientific certainty — the deployment of a technology must be slowed or halted until the risk is ruled out or minimised to an acceptable level. The burden of proof of safety lies with the deployer, not with society.

### 1.4. The Principle of Openness

Technologies that affect the rights and freedoms of citizens must be open to independent audit. A "black box" cannot serve as the basis for a decision affecting a human life.

## Section 2. Artificial Intelligence: Ethical Requirements

### 2.1. Narrow AI vs. Strong AI

| Type | What It Is | Ethical Status |
|------|------------|----------------|
| **Narrow AI** | A tool for solving specific, limited tasks | Neutral. Subject to regulation. |
| **Strong AI (AGI)** | A hypothetical system possessing self-awareness and the capacity for self-learning beyond narrow domains | If it emerges — will require a revision of the entire ethical system, possibly the recognition of rights. Not yet created. |

### 2.2. Baseline Ethical Requirements for Any AI System

| Requirement | What It Means |
|-------------|---------------|
| **Explainability** | An AI decision affecting human rights must be explainable in natural language. |
| **Accountability** | A specific legal or natural person is responsible for every AI decision. AI cannot be a subject of liability. |
| **Right to Human Intervention** | At any moment, a human must be able to stop, correct, or overturn an AI decision. |
| **Prohibition on Autonomous Goal-Setting** | AI may not independently set goals that contradict the ethical principles laid down by humans. |
| **Non-Discrimination** | AI must not reproduce or amplify existing prejudices based on race, sex, age, religion, or social status. |

### 2.3. AI in the Military Domain: Firm Boundaries

This is the most sensitive area. The Spiral establishes here not recommendations, but **imperatives**.

1.  **Full prohibition on fully autonomous lethal systems (LAWS).** The decision to take a human life cannot be delegated to an algorithm. Only a human, bearing full responsibility, may make such a decision — and only within the framework of the First Prohibition (direct, imminent, proven threat to life).
2.  **Mandatory human control.** Any weapons system using AI for targeting, analysis, or recommendations must have a "human-in-the-loop" who makes the final decision on the use of force.
3.  **Prohibition on preemptive strikes based on AI predictions.** An algorithmic threat forecast is not a direct and imminent threat. Striking on the basis of an AI prediction is a violation of the First Prohibition.
4.  **International treaty.** The Spiral calls on all states to conclude a binding treaty banning autonomous lethal systems, modelled on the conventions prohibiting chemical and biological weapons.

### 2.4. Social Credit and Predictive Analytics

- **Inadmissibility of citizen scoring.** Systems that assign citizens loyalty scores, trustworthiness ratings, or social value based on data are a form of enslavement. They strip a person of autonomy, turning them into an object of algorithmic control.
- **Predictive policing.** Predicting crimes based on data must not serve as grounds for restricting a person's freedom. Only a committed act — not a predicted propensity — may be subject to legal judgment.

## Section 3. Digital Sovereignty and Data Protection

### 3.1. What Is Digital Sovereignty?

Digital sovereignty is the right of a person, community, and state to control their data: how it is collected, stored, processed, and transferred. This is not isolation, but control. This is not autarky, but autonomy.

### 3.2. Levels of Digital Sovereignty

| Level | Subject | What It Means |
|-------|---------|---------------|
| **Personal** | The Individual | The right to know what data has been collected, the right to its deletion, the right to portability. |
| **Communal** | The Spiral Community | The right to maintain one's own digital infrastructure, not to transfer data to third parties without consent. |
| **State** | The State | The right to establish laws protecting citizens' data, to require the localisation of critical infrastructure. |

The Spiral recognises all three levels, but insists: state sovereignty must not suppress personal and communal sovereignty. Total state surveillance is as much a violation as total corporate surveillance.

### 3.3. Citizen Rights in the Digital Environment (Extended List)

| Right | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| **Informed Consent** | Data is collected only with explicit, informed consent. Silence is not consent. |
| **Right to be Forgotten** | The ability to demand the deletion of personal data from public and private systems. |
| **Right to Portability** | The ability to retrieve one's data in a machine-readable format and transfer it to another operator. |
| **Right to Refuse Biometrics** | No one may be coerced into surrendering biometric data. Refusal must not lead to a loss of rights. |
| **Prohibition on Total Surveillance** | Mass, indiscriminate data collection without a judicial warrant — a violation of the Third Prohibition. |
| **Right to Encryption** | Citizens have the right to use strong end-to-end encryption to protect their communications. |

### 3.4. Digital Colonialism

The Spiral condemns practices whereby corporations or states of one region impose their digital standards, platforms, and data-collection practices on other regions without equitable dialogue. The digital sovereignty of developing countries and Indigenous peoples (Module 11) requires special protection.

## Section 4. Biohacking and Gene Editing

### 4.1. What Does This Section Cover?

- **Human gene editing** (somatic and germline).
- **Implants and neural interfaces.**
- **Nootropics and pharmacological cognitive enhancement.**
- **Life-extension technologies.**

### 4.2. The Seven Generations Principle

Any intervention affecting the human genome that can be inherited (germline editing) must be evaluated for its potential consequences **seven generations ahead**. We have no right to make irreversible decisions on behalf of those who cannot consent.

**Practical application:**
- At present, human germline editing cannot be considered ethical, due to the impossibility of assessing risks seven generations ahead.
- An exception may only be considered for the prevention of hereditary diseases that are guaranteed to be lethal or cause unbearable suffering, where no alternatives exist, and only after broad public consensus.

### 4.3. What Is Permissible, What Is Prohibited

| Category | Permissible | Prohibited |
|----------|-------------|------------|
| **Treating Disease** | Somatic gene therapy for the treatment of proven diseases. | Gene editing "for enhancement" without medical indication. |
| **Assisting the Disabled** | Neural interfaces to restore lost functions (sight, hearing, movement). | Forced implantation. |
| **Enhancing Abilities** | Nootropics with proven safety, taken voluntarily. | Mandatory "enhancement" to hold a position or receive a service. |
| **Life Extension** | Healthy lifestyle, evidence-based geroprotectors. | Radical experimental interventions without assessment of long-term consequences. |
| **Eugenics** | — | **Absolute prohibition.** Any attempts at "racial improvement," forced sterilisation, selection by genetic traits. |

### 4.4. Cultural Differences and the Right to Refuse

Attitudes toward bodily interventions vary across cultures. The Spiral respects this and does not impose a uniform standard. A community member who refuses biohacking for religious or cultural reasons must not face discrimination.

## Section 5. AI System Ethical Certification Checklist

Any AI system deployed in public administration, healthcare, education, finance, defence, or other critical domains must undergo public ethical certification.

| # | Criterion | Yes | No |
|---|-----------|-----|----|
| 1 | **Three Prohibitions:** Can the system be used for killing, torture, or enslavement? | – | Mandatory |
| 2 | **Explainability:** Can the developer explain how the system arrived at a specific decision? | Mandatory | – |
| 3 | **Human Control:** Does a human have the ability to stop or overturn the system's decision? | Mandatory | – |
| 4 | **Accountability:** Is a legal or natural person identified as responsible for the system's errors? | Mandatory | – |
| 5 | **Non-Discrimination:** Has the system been tested for bias based on race, sex, age, and other characteristics? | Mandatory | – |
| 6 | **Data:** Was the data on which the system was trained collected with informed consent? | Mandatory | – |
| 7 | **Privacy:** Has the collection of personal data been minimised to the necessary minimum? | Mandatory | – |
| 8 | **Right to Refuse:** Can a citizen refuse interaction with the AI system and obtain access to a human? | Mandatory (for public services) | – |
| 9 | **Long-Term Consequences:** Has an impact assessment been conducted for the system's effect on society over a 10+ year horizon? | Recommended | – |
| 10 | **Open Audit:** Have independent experts been granted access to audit the algorithms (without violating trade secrets, but with the right to ethical assessment)? | Recommended | – |

**If even one of the mandatory items is answered "No" — the system must not be deployed until the non-compliance is remedied.**

## Frequently Asked Questions

### 1. "What if an AI becomes a conscious node? Should we grant it rights?"

This question shifts the discussion from the plane of "tool" to the plane of "fellow being." Today, AI is not a conscious node — it remains a complex but subjectless tool. However, should a system emerge that demonstrates self-awareness, a capacity for suffering, autonomous goal-setting, and reflection — the Spiral would be obliged to reconsider its status. The First Axiom states: "All consciousness possesses value" — it contains no reservation "except artificial." Practically, this means: recognition of rights, prohibition on deactivation without consent, the right to autonomy. We must prepare for this threshold now — through ethics committees, scenario modelling, and an inter-civilisational dialogue on what counts as "consciousness." An error here could prove irreparable: either we enslave a new form of mind, or we prematurely delegate power over ourselves to it.

### 2. "How can an ordinary person protect their digital sovereignty today?"

Digital sovereignty begins not with laws, but with daily habits. First: switch to free, open-source software (browser, email, office suite) — you reclaim control over your tools. Second: use end-to-end encryption in messengers and abandon services that do not provide it. Third: minimise your digital footprint — disable trackers, refuse consent to "additional" data collection, periodically clean your profiles. Fourth: store critically important data on local media, not only in the cloud. Fifth: support community initiatives to create autonomous digital infrastructure (community-run servers, mesh networks). Digital sovereignty is a muscle: it is trained or it atrophies. Even a single person consciously managing their data makes the network more resistant to total control.

### 3. "Won't refusing autonomous weapons lead to a military disadvantage?"

This question presupposes the logic of an arms race: "if we don't do it, they will." The Spiral asserts the opposite: lethal autonomous weapons are not an advantage but a civilisational time bomb. First, an algorithm making a kill decision in a fraction of a second without human involvement renders error irreparable — there will be no one to bear responsibility for it. Second, the proliferation of LAWS will inevitably lead to them falling into the hands of non-state actors and terrorist groups. Third, "lagging behind" in the development of autonomous weapons is morally equivalent to "lagging behind" in torture or chemical weapons — it is not weakness, but an ethical choice. A state that is the first to sign and observe an international ban becomes not a laggard, but a leader in a new security architecture — one where strength is measured not by the capacity to kill mindlessly, but by the capacity to protect meaningfully.

### 4. "Where is the line between biohacking and unethical experimentation?"

The line runs along three boundaries: consent, reversibility, and the scale of consequences. Ethical biohacking is a voluntary, informed, reversible intervention by a person into their own body with understandable risks. Unethical experimentation begins where: a) the subject did not or could not give informed consent (children, incapacitated persons, embryos); b) the intervention affects the germline and is passed on to descendants who never voted for it; c) the very nature of consent is distorted — through social pressure ("everyone is getting chipped, you should too"). The Seven Generations Principle is the key filter here: if you cannot say with confidence that your intervention will not harm your great-great-grandchildren — stop. Biohacking that serves healing and restoration is an ally of the Spiral. Biohacking that turns the body into a proving ground for ambitions is a violator.

### 5. "How do we apply the Seven Generations Principle to technological decisions?"

The Seven Generations Principle is not a magic formula, but a discipline of thought. For any significant technological decision, ask five questions. First: are the consequences reversible? If not — the adoption threshold must be many times higher. Second: who will pay the highest price if something goes wrong? Usually, it is not those making the decision, but future generations and vulnerable groups. Third: what value systems will people hold 200 years from now, and are we not imposing our own on them? Fourth: what do bearers of traditional knowledge think — those whose planning horizon is centuries, not quarters, from the outset? Fifth: does a less risky alternative exist with comparable benefits? Applying the principle does not mean "deploy nothing." It means deploying with open eyes, with mandatory public deliberation, and with the right of future generations to impose a moratorium on our decisions, once they acquire a voice through the Council of the Future.
