## For mentors, education coordinators, and all who teach others within the "Spiral of Consciousness" system

---

### Introduction

Education in the "Spiral of Consciousness" is neither the transmission of ready-made answers nor the formation of loyalty to any ideology or state. It is the creation of conditions in which a person learns to think independently, to take responsibility for their choices, and to see the consequences of their actions in the long term. We do not raise "patriots" or "soldiers." We cultivate conscious nodes capable of cooperation, compassion, and critical analysis.

This module provides:

- A pedagogical philosophy grounded in dialogue and liberation (Paulo Freire).
- A mentor training program (4 weeks, 8 modules), including a specialization for work in state institutions on the principles of ethical consulting.
- Age-specific programs for children and adolescents, including an optional course "Foundations of the Spiral" for schools and universities.
- Assessment tools free of indoctrination and coercion.

**References:**
- Module 0 (The Canon) — axioms, prohibitions, principles, section "The Spiral and the State."
- Module 2 (Ethics) — the ethics of power, code of honor, the dirty hands dilemma.
- Module 3 (Practices) — the coming-of-age ritual.
- Module 5 (Conflicts) — Circles of Reconciliation, teaching children non-violent dispute resolution.
- Module 9 (Psychology) — age-specific characteristics, burnout prevention, moral injury.

## Section 1. Pedagogy of Liberation (Paulo Freire) — Application within the "Spiral"

### 1.1. Critique of the "Banking Model" and Ideological Indoctrination

Traditional education is often built as a "banking model": the teacher deposits knowledge into passive students. Even more dangerous is when education turns into an instrument of ideological indoctrination — cultivating loyalty to the state, national exceptionalism, or blind patriotism. The Spiral rejects both.

- Knowledge is not transmitted; it is constructed in dialogue.
- Value lies not in memorizing correct answers, but in the ability to ask questions.
- A mentor is a facilitator who creates a safe space for reflection, not a transmitter of doctrine.

### 1.2. Dialogue as Method

In dialogical pedagogy:

- **Teacher and student learn from each other.** The student's experience is as important as the teacher's knowledge.
- **Questions are worth more than answers.** A good question launches an inquiry.
- **Mistakes are opportunities for growth, not grounds for punishment.**

### 1.3. Cultivating Responsibility and Critical Thinking

The goal of education in the Spiral is not "patriotic upbringing" but the formation of:

- **Critical thinking:** the ability to analyze information, recognize manipulation, see cause-and-effect relationships.
- **Ethical awareness:** understanding how personal and collective actions affect other people and future generations.
- **Civic responsibility:** readiness to participate in the life of the community and society, without delegating decisions to "chieftains" and "experts."

## Section 2. Mentor Training Program (4 Weeks, 8 Modules)

**Format:** 4 weeks, 2 modules per week (each module — 2–3 hours).  
**Methods:** minimal lectures, role-playing, case study analysis, practical assignments.  
**Outcome:** mentor certification (valid for 2 years).

### Module 1. Philosophy and Ethics of the Spiral (Week 1)
In-depth study of Module 0: axioms, prohibitions, principles, relationship to the state. Case study analysis.

### Module 2. Dialogue Facilitation and Active Listening (Week 1)
Principles of Nonviolent Communication (Rosenberg). Role-play "Difficult Conversation."

### Module 3. Sociocracy and Decision-Making (Week 2)
How to conduct a circle meeting, protocol, timing, processing objections. Practice.

### Module 4. Conflict Resolution and Circles of Reconciliation (Week 2)
Full scenario of a Circle of Reconciliation (Module 5). Role-play. Facilitator's code of conduct.

### Module 5. Psychological Support and Burnout Prevention (Week 3)
Signs of burnout, active listening techniques in crisis situations, Circle of Empathy (Module 9).

### Module 6. Education for Children and Adolescents (Week 3)
Age-specific characteristics, how to explain Spiral principles through fairy tales, games, dialogues. Lesson development.

### Module 7. Dialogue Pedagogy in Practice (Week 4)
How to build a course where participants themselves formulate questions. Assessment without grades. Mini-seminar by each participant.

### Module 8. Final Examination and Certification (Week 4)
Oral interview, demonstration of skills (leading a circle or seminar). Awarding of certificate.

## Section 3. Specialization: Mentor in State Institutions (Army, Police, Emergency Services)

This specialization is intended for mentors who plan to work (or already work) in state structures associated with the use of force or work in extreme conditions. Its purpose is **ethical consulting**, not ideological indoctrination of personnel.

### 3.1. Who May Complete the Specialization?

- A certified Spiral mentor (basic program).
- Preferably, experience of service in relevant structures or an understanding of their specific nature.

### 3.2. Content of the Specialization (Additional 20 Hours)

| Block | Topics |
|-------|--------|
| **Ethics of Power and Force** | The Three Prohibitions in the context of service duties. Criminal orders and the duty to refuse. Analysis of the "dirty hands" dilemma (Module 2). |
| **Psychology of Extreme Situations** | Basics of combat trauma, secondary traumatization, moral injury (Module 9). Principles of psychological first aid. |
| **Ethical Consulting** | How to conduct conversations about values without violating chain of command or undermining morale. Format of the "ethical minute" — a brief discussion of dilemmas within service time. |
| **Boundaries of the Mentor** | A mentor is neither a commander nor a psychotherapist. They do not give orders, do not make diagnoses, and do not collect compromising material. Their role is to be a neutral ethical mirror. |
| **Secular Nature of the Work** | In state institutions, a Spiral mentor does not preach, does not proselytize, and does not create "Spiral cells" without a voluntary request. They offer tools of ethical reflection to those who are interested. |

### 3.3. Formats of a Mentor's Work in State Institutions

- **Voluntary ethical conversations** (upon request from colleagues or command).
- **Participation in incident reviews** (assistance in making sense of difficult situations from an ethical perspective).
- **Pre-service preparation** (helping recruits become aware of ethical boundaries).
- **Post-extreme-event support** (Circles of Empathy, assistance in overcoming moral injury).

**Important:** a mentor must not be used by the command as an instrument for "raising morale" or justifying questionable decisions. Their loyalty is only to the Three Prohibitions, not to a flag or a superior.

#### Optional Course "Foundations of the Spiral" (for Schools and Universities)

**Status:** secular, voluntary, non-compulsory. May be taught within social studies, ethics, or philosophy classes, or as a separate club.

**Course Objectives:**

- To acquaint students with universal ethical principles (the Three Prohibitions, the Four Principles of Flourishing).
- To develop skills of critical thinking, argumentation, and dialogue.
- To form an understanding of the long-term consequences of personal and collective decisions.

**Course Structure (12 Lessons of 1 Hour Each):**

| Lesson | Topic | Method |
|--------|-------|--------|
| 1 | What is a "conscious node"? The Two Axioms. | Dialogue, examples from life |
| 2 | The Three Prohibitions: why are they absolute? | Analysis of historical and contemporary cases |
| 3 | The Principles of Flourishing: how to apply them in life? | Group work, creating a personal plan |
| 4 | The Spiral and the State: ethics of power and citizen. | Debate "Where are the boundaries of obedience?" |
| 5 | Digital Hygiene: how to protect your attention. | Workshop on screen time analysis |
| 6 | Economics for Life: cooperatives and the common good. | Game "Create Your Own Cooperative" |
| 7 | Conflicts and How to Resolve Them Without Violence. | Role-play "Circle of Reconciliation" |
| 8 | Technology and Ethics: AI, biohacking, data. | Discussion "Does a robot have the right to kill?" |
| 9 | Psychology of Stress and How to Help Others. | Grounding and empathy practices |
| 10 | Global World: Ubuntu, sobornost, dialogue of cultures. | Project "Bridge Between Cultures" |
| 11 | The Long-Term Future: space, ecology, generations. | Essay "Letter to a Great-Grandchild in 2126" |
| 12 | Final Lesson: my personal ethical code. | Presentation and discussion |

**Teaching Principles:**

- No propaganda. The course does not agitate to join Spiral communities.
- Respect for diverse worldviews. Students may agree or disagree with the material.
- Voluntary participation in discussions. No one is obliged to speak.
- Grades — only in the form of self-assessment and feedback, without coercion.

### 4.4. The Coming-of-Age Ritual as a Voluntary Civic Act

Upon reaching 16–18 years of age, an adolescent may voluntarily undergo the coming-of-age ritual (Module 3). This is neither a state oath nor a compulsory rite. It is a personal act of accepting responsibility for one's choices and one's future. The community and family may support the adolescent in this decision, but may not impose it.

## Section 5. Assessment and Certification Without Indoctrination

### 5.1. What Is Prohibited

- Exams on knowledge of "correct answers" (this is the banking model).
- Punishment for doubt or criticism (in the Spiral, doubt is the engine of development).
- Coercion to participate in classes or rituals.
- Hierarchical grades as an instrument of pressure.

### 5.2. What Is Recommended

- **Portfolio** — the student collects their works (reflections, projects, records of participation in Circles).
- **Self-assessment** — the student evaluates their own progress.
- **Peer feedback** — without grades, only constructive comments.
- **Attestation through demonstration of skills** — conducting a seminar, Circle of Reconciliation, ethical consultation.

### 5.3. Mentor Certification

The basic mentor certificate grants the right to conduct educational sessions for children and adults, and to moderate Circles of Reconciliation.

The mentor certificate with "state institutions" specialization additionally grants the right to work in the army, police, and emergency services as an ethical consultant.

Both certificates **do not grant power** over the community or over colleagues. A mentor may not give orders or exclude anyone.

**Validity period:** 2 years, after which advanced training is required.

## Section 6. Checklist for Creating an Educational Program in a Community

For a community of any size:

- [ ] Age groups identified (or mixed groups with individual assignments).
- [ ] At least one mentor trained in dialogue pedagogy.
- [ ] Materials developed (fairy tales, case studies) that account for local culture and secularity.
- [ ] The optional course "Foundations of the Spiral" (or its elements) available for adolescents and youth.
- [ ] Classes for children held at least once a month (2–4 times recommended).
- [ ] A feedback mechanism in place (anonymous suggestions and criticism).
- [ ] Courses for adults held upon request (at least once a year).
- [ ] Attendance and portfolio tracking maintained (without compulsion).
- [ ] No class contains calls for violence, national exceptionalism, or blind loyalty to the state.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### 1. "How do we teach children the Spiral without imposing it?"

The key word is "dialogue," not "lesson." The pedagogy of liberation by Paulo Freire (Section 1) provides the method: do not deposit answers, ask questions. Not "the Spiral says killing is forbidden," but "what do you think — can killing ever be justified? What if someone threatens you?" Not "here are the Three Prohibitions — memorise them," but "what rules do you consider most important for all people?" The child will arrive at conclusions resonant with the Spiral on their own, or will propose different ones — both are valuable as objects of reflection. Second: do not separate "the Spiral's teaching" from the rest of life. The Three Prohibitions run through a fairy tale about a little hedgehog for five-year-olds and through debates about drones for adolescents — the form changes, the content emerges naturally. Third: your life is the main textbook. Children absorb not words but behaviour: if you preach non-violence and shout at your loved ones — you are teaching hypocrisy, not the Spiral. Fourth: respect the child's right to say "I disagree." Doubt is not a failure of pedagogy, but its success.

### 2. "What if the school forbids mentioning the Spiral?"

The Spiral does not require mentioning itself by name. The Three Prohibitions can be discussed as "universal ethical principles." Cooperatives — as "the economy for the common good." Circles of Reconciliation — as "restorative justice." Content is more important than brand. The optional course "Foundations of the Spiral" is designed as secular, voluntary, and compatible with the framework of social studies, ethics, or philosophy (Section 4.3). If the school forbids even this — seek extracurricular formats: a club, a debate society, a volunteer project. The community can organise an educational space outside the school. And remember: prohibition often intensifies interest. History knows many examples where ideas banned at school became subjects of the deepest independent study by adolescents. Do not fight the school — that will exhaust you and harm the children. Seek parallel paths, and let the quality of your pedagogy speak for itself.

### 3. "How do you distinguish imposition from mentoring?"

Three tests. The test of voluntariness: "Can the student refuse the session without consequences for their status in the community?" If yes — it is mentoring. If refusal entails condemnation or a loss of rights — it is imposition. The test of doubt: "Can the student say 'I disagree' and receive in response not an argument, but an exploration of their position?" If yes — it is dialogue. If disagreement is met with "you're just not mature enough to understand" — it is indoctrination. The test of purpose: "Do I want the student to think like me, or to think for themselves?" A mentor cultivates thinking; a propagandist cultivates loyalty. The certification programme (Section 2) specifically includes a module on active listening and dialogue facilitation — these are skills opposite to imposition. The best mentor is not the one whose students repeat their words, but the one whose students ask questions to which the mentor themselves has no ready answer.

### 4. "Should mentors be certified, and won't that create a hierarchy?"

Certification is necessary, but it is structured differently from traditional systems. Its purpose is not to create a privileged caste, but to ensure quality and safety. A mentor works with people's psyches, leads Circles of Reconciliation where what is at stake is people's ability to live together after conflict. A dilettante here can do harm. The certificate confirms that the person has mastered skills (facilitation, active listening, ethical analysis), not that they are "above" others. The certificate grants no power to command or exclude, is valid for two years, and requires re-qualification (Section 5.3). Moreover, in a sociocratic community, the mentor is embedded in the system of double links and rotation — they are accountable to the circle of mentors and cannot transform their role into a throne. Hierarchy arises not from certification as such, but from whether the certificate can be monetised into power. In the Spiral — it cannot. Competence and equality do not contradict each other; what contradicts are competence and unaccountability.
