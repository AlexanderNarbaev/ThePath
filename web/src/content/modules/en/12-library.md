## For all community members, researchers, mentors, and statespersons seeking to deepen their understanding of the principles of the "Spiral of Consciousness"

---

### Introduction

The Library of the "Spiral of Consciousness" is not a "sacred canon" but a guide to the sources upon which the system is founded. Collected here are books that help make sense of the key ideas: from ethics and politics to psychology, technology, and the global dialogue of civilizations. They are not set in opposition to one another but complement and enrich understanding.

Each source is accompanied by a brief annotation explaining how it relates to specific modules or principles of the "Spiral." You may read them in any order, according to your interests and current tasks.

In this updated edition, special attention is given to the themes of state governance, military ethics, and international law, and the representation of thinkers from various civilizational traditions — Russian, Asian, African, Latin American — has been expanded.

## Section 1. Philosophy and Ethics (Foundations)

- **Plato. "The Republic"** — a classic inquiry into justice, the nature of the ideal society, and the role of knowledge in governance. *Connection to Module 0 (principles), Module 8 (politics).*

- **Aristotle. "Nicomachean Ethics"** — an introduction to virtue ethics, the concept of eudaimonia (flourishing) as the highest human goal. Served as the basis for the Four Principles of Flourishing. *Connection to Module 0, Module 2.*

- **Immanuel Kant. "Groundwork of the Metaphysics of Morals"** — the categorical imperative and the idea that a person must never be used merely as a means. A direct foundation for the Three Absolute Prohibitions. *Connection to Module 0, Module 2.*

- **John Stuart Mill. "Utilitarianism"** — the principle of "the greatest good for the greatest number of people." Used for evaluating consequences in ethical dilemmas. *Connection to Module 2.*

- **Friedrich Nietzsche. "Beyond Good and Evil"** — a critique of traditional morality, the concept of the "will to power." Important for realizing that not all moral systems are the same. *Connection to Module 11 (global synthesis).*

- **Karl Popper. "The Open Society and Its Enemies"** — a critique of totalitarianism and utopian thinking. Argumentation in favor of gradual, verifiable changes. *Connection to Module 4, Module 8.*

- **Hannah Arendt. "The Origins of Totalitarianism"** — an analysis of the nature of totalitarian regimes, the phenomenon of the "banality of evil." Basic reading for understanding the "dark side" of power. *Connection to Module 0, Module 8.*

- **Albert Camus. "The Myth of Sisyphus"** — an absurdist response to meaninglessness: one must live, rebel, and love, even if there is no higher meaning. *Connection to Module 0 (cosmology).*

- **Søren Kierkegaard. "Either/Or"** — the existential choice and the leap of faith. Important for understanding a person's responsibility for their worldview. *Connection to Module 3, Module 9.*

## Section 2. State Governance, Law, and Political Theory

- **Charles Montesquieu. "The Spirit of the Laws"** — the classical theory of the separation of powers and the influence of climate and mores on legislation. A foundation for reflections on subsidiarity and the ethical limitation of power. *Connection to Module 8.*

- **John Rawls. "A Theory of Justice"** — the concept of "justice as fairness," the principles of equality and difference. Applied when constructing an ethical tax system and distributing resources. *Connection to Module 7, Module 8.*

- **Alexis de Tocqueville. "Democracy in America"** — an analysis of democratic society, the role of associations, and the dangers of the "tyranny of the majority." Useful for understanding citizens' assemblies and communal self-governance. *Connection to Module 8.*

- **Jürgen Habermas. "Between Facts and Norms"** — discursive theory of law and democracy. The justification for citizens' assemblies and deliberative democracy. *Connection to Module 8, Module 11.*

- **Elinor Ostrom. "Governing the Commons: The Evolution of Institutions for Collective Action"** — how communities can independently manage common resources without privatization and state control. *Connection to Module 7 (economics), Module 8.*

- **Gabriel Almond, Sidney Verba. "The Civic Culture"** — a study of political attitudes and values that contribute to stable democracy. *Connection to Module 8, Module 10 (civic education).*

- **Robert Dahl. "Polyarchy: Participation and Opposition"** — the conditions for democratic governance, the importance of competition and participation. *Connection to Module 8.*

## Section 3. International Law and Global Governance

- **Hugo Grotius. "On the Law of War and Peace"** — a foundational work on international law, the ideas of natural law and limitations on the conduct of war. *Connection to Module 6 (ethics of war), Module 11, Module 13.*

- **Hans Kelsen. "Pure Theory of Law"** — the philosophy of law, the idea of a "basic norm" and the hierarchy of legal norms. Useful for constructing the constitutional foundations of the Ethical State. *Connection to Module 8, Module 13.*

- **Lassa Oppenheim. "International Law" (ed. H. Lauterpacht)** — a classic textbook covering sources, subjects, and the laws of war and peace. *Connection to Module 13.*

- **Martti Koskenniemi. "From Apology to Utopia: The Structure of International Legal Argument"** — a critical analysis of international law, its role in legitimizing power, and alternative approaches. *Connection to Module 11, Module 13.*

- **UN Human Rights Council. "Universal Declaration of Human Rights" (with commentaries)** — a foundational document, but the Spiral reminds us: human rights do not cancel cultural diversity and must be compatible with the Three Prohibitions. *Connection to Module 0, Module 11.*

## Section 4. Military Ethics and the Ethics of Force

- **Michael Walzer. "Just and Unjust Wars"** — a modern classic: the theory of just war (jus ad bellum, jus in bello). An analysis of moral dilemmas, including extreme cases. *Connection to Module 2 (ethics of power and force), Module 6, Module 13.*

- **David Rodin. "The Ethics of War: Classic and Contemporary Readings"** — a collection of key texts from Thucydides to contemporary authors. *Connection to Module 2, Module 13.*

- **Paul Robinson (ed.). "Military Ethics: An Anthology"** — a systematic survey of ethical problems: military duty, humanitarian intervention, torture, autonomous weapons. *Connection to Module 2, Module 5, Module 6.*

- **Jeff McMahan. "Killing in War"** — an ethical analysis: who may be a legitimate target, the moral equality of combatants, responsibility. *Connection to Module 2 (the prohibition on killing), Module 13.*

- **Fyodor Dostoevsky. "Notes from a Dead House"** — not a theoretical treatise but a profound artistic exploration of violence, punishment, and human dignity. Useful for understanding moral injury. *Connection to Module 9 (working with trauma), Module 0.*

## Frequently Asked Questions

### 1. "Why so many books? Is it enough just to read the Canon?"

The Canon (Module 0) is the core, the ethical minimum sufficient for membership in a community. But the "Spiral of Consciousness" system is not a catechism; it is an invitation to a lifetime of reflection. The Library is not an "obligatory list," but a map of a territory you may enter in any order and at any depth. The Canon answers the question of "what" (the Three Prohibitions, the Four Principles). The Library answers the question of "why" and "from where do we know this." A person who has read only the Canon can be a fine community member. A person who has also read Aristotle, Freire, Walzer, and Kimmerer will be a deeper mentor, because they will see how the same ethical truths are refracted in different eras and cultures. The Library is a resource for growth, not an exam ticket.

### 2. "How do I choose what to read if I have little time?"

Use the modules as a navigator. If politics and governance concern you most — start with Section 2 (Plato, Montesquieu, Rawls, Ostrom). If you work with trauma — Section 7 (Frankl, van der Kolk). If you are an engineer thinking about AI — Section 10 (Bostrom, Zuboff). For a first rapid immersion, three books suffice: Aristotle — "Nicomachean Ethics" (the foundation of virtue ethics), Frankl — "Man's Search for Meaning" (how to preserve humanity), and Ostrom — "Governing the Commons" (how people self-organise without the state or the market). These are three books from entirely different angles, yet all of them lead to the core of the Spiral. After them, you will know yourself which direction to dig further. And remember: one deeply read book is worth more than ten skimmed ones. Quality of reading is more important than quantity.

### 3. "Are non-Western thinkers represented?"

Yes, and consciously and systematically. Section 13 (African Philosophy) includes Chinua Achebe, Kwasi Wiredu, Desmond Tutu, Léopold Sédar Senghor. Section 14 (Asian Thought) — Laozi, Confucius, Gandhi, Amartya Sen. Section 15 (Russian Philosophy) — Khomyakov, Fyodorov, Vernadsky, Berdyaev. Section 16 (Latin American Thought) — Freire, Dussel, Gutiérrez, Galeano. Section 12 (Indigenous Knowledge) — Marshall, Kimmerer, Linda Smith. This is not a "diversity checkbox" for show, but a structural feature of the Spiral: if a system claims to engage in global ethical dialogue, its intellectual foundation cannot be monocultural. We do not claim that the library is perfectly balanced — it will continue to be expanded. But the direction is set: from the Western canon as the "only one" — toward a global chorus, where every voice matters.
