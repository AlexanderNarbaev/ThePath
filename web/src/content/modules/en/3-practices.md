## A Complete Practical Guide for Followers of the Spiral of Consciousness

---

### Introduction

The practices and rituals of the Spiral of Consciousness are not magical rites and not religious sacraments. They are **psychological and social tools** that help strengthen attention, develop awareness, resolve conflicts, and reinforce bonds within the community. All practices are voluntary and adapt to any culture, ability, and circumstances.

This document contains a complete description of all practices — from daily 3 minutes to annual festivals, special rituals, and civic assemblies.

**References to other modules:**
- Module 0 (Canon) — for understanding the axioms, prohibitions, and principles.
- Module 4 (Resilience) — for crisis preparedness plans and role rotation.
- Module 5 (Conflict Resolution) — for the detailed Reconciliation Circle scenario.
- Module 9 (Psychology) — for burnout prevention practices and earth-healing.

## Part 1. Daily Practices (for everyone, 3 minutes)

These three practices constitute the **minimum core**. Any person can perform them regardless of circumstances.

### 1.1 Morning Intention (1 minute)

**When:** immediately upon waking, before touching the phone.

**How to perform:**
1. Take one deep breath.
2. Say mentally or aloud:
   > *"Today I choose the ascending spiral. I will not violate the three prohibitions and I will perform one conscious action for the growth of consciousness — my own or another's."*
3. Plan one concrete action for the day (for example: "I will read 10 pages," "I will help a neighbor," "I will spend one hour without the phone").

**Adaptations:**
- For children: "Today I will be kind and learn something new."
- For the elderly: may be done seated, with eyes closed.
- For the busy: 30 seconds is enough.

### 1.2 Digital Shield (1 minute)

**When:** before the first touch of the screen of a phone, tablet, or computer.

**How to perform:**
1. Stop.
2. Ask yourself:
   > *"Why am I opening this? Is this a conscious choice or an escape from silence?"*
3. If the answer is "escape" — put the device aside for 5 minutes and do something else (wash your face, look out the window, stretch).

**Adaptations:**
- If you are checking work messages — ask yourself: "Is this urgent? Can it be done in 5 minutes?"

### 1.3 Evening Reflection (1 minute)

**When:** before sleep, in silence.

**How to perform:**
1. Take a deep breath.
2. Ask yourself three questions:
   - *"What did I do to preserve and multiply consciousness?"*
   - *"Where did I succumb to entropy?"*
   - *"What will I do differently tomorrow?"*
3. Praise yourself for successes. Simply note mistakes, do not torment yourself. Let them go.

**Adaptations:**
- If it's hard to answer — just think about the best moment of the day and what can be improved tomorrow.

## Part 2. Weekly Practices (for those who wish)

These practices require 1–2 hours per week and are usually performed in a group.

### 2.1 Gathering of Reason (1–2 hours)

**Purpose:** discussion of science news, ethical dilemmas, joint projects.

**Structure:**
1. **Opening (5 min)** — the facilitator lights a candle (or initiates a minute of silence), says: "We gather to grow together in knowledge and good deeds."
2. **Science News (15 min)** — one participant shares one scientific discovery from the week.
3. **Ethical Dilemma (20 min)** — a short life situation is discussed (for example: "a friend asks to borrow money for gambling — should you give it or not?").
4. **Plans and Projects (15 min)** — each person shares a useful deed from the week and plans for the next.
5. **Closing (5 min)** — reflection, well-wishes, the phrase: "We grow together. Until the next meeting."

**Adaptations:**
- Online (Zoom, Discord) — use "raised hand" for speaking order.
- For small groups (2–4 people) — an informal conversation over tea.
- For large groups (10+ people) — strict timing and a moderator.

### 2.2 Apophatic Silence (15–30 minutes)

**Purpose:** a practice of awareness through the renunciation of thought-images.

**How to perform (group or individual variant):**
1. Sit comfortably, close your eyes.
2. Mentally go through negations: "I am — not the body, not thoughts, not desires, not social roles, not gender, not age, not a believer, not an atheist…"
3. Having reached the limit of negations, remain in pure silence without images.

**Adaptations:**
- For beginners: 5 minutes.
- For children: 1 minute of silence with the phrase "I am — not the shout, not the stomping, not the fight…"
- An audio recording with a voice going through the negations may be used.

### 2.3 Deep Attention Practice (1 hour without screens)

**Purpose:** training the capacity for prolonged concentration.

**How to perform:**
- Choose one activity (reading a book, drawing, handicrafts, cleaning, a walk without the phone).
- Remove all distracting devices.
- Immerse yourself in the activity for 1 hour, without switching.

**Adaptations:**
- Start with 30 minutes.
- For children: 15 minutes of play without gadgets.

## Part 3. Monthly Practices (for those who wish)

### 3.1 Digital / Hedonistic Fast (24–72 hours)

**Purpose:** restoring sensitivity to reality, detoxifying from artificial stimuli.

**What is excluded:**
- Social media (Instagram, TikTok, Twitter, VK, Facebook).
- Video games.
- Pornography.
- Fast food, alcohol, sweets (optional).
- News feeds.

**What is included:**
- Nature, walks.
- Reading one difficult (paper) book.
- Physical labor (garden, cleaning, construction).
- Conversations with loved ones.
- Journaling.

**Adaptations:**
- For beginners: start with 24 hours.
- Family with children: a joint day off without gadgets, with outdoor games.

### 3.2 The Hour of Acknowledged Ignorance (30–60 minutes)

**Purpose:** developing intellectual humility and curiosity.

**How to perform (in a group):**
1. Sit in a circle.
2. Each person in turn says: *"One thing I do not know is…"*
3. No one gives advice, no one solves problems. Only listen.
4. A prize for the best question (for example, a symbolic badge).

**Adaptations:**
- Individually: a journal entry of "10 things I do not know."

### 3.3 The Civic Ritual (open monthly gathering)

**Purpose:** to create an open space for all who share the values of the Spiral but are not necessarily members of the community. This is not an oath and not a pledge of allegiance — it is a place for dialogue about how ethical principles can be applied in public life.

**Who may participate:** any person who shares the axioms and prohibitions of the Spiral, regardless of community membership, citizenship, religion, or profession.

**Structure (1.5–2 hours):**
1. **Opening (5 min):** the facilitator lights a candle and says: "We have gathered as free people to think together about how to make our society more just and more conscious. There are no oaths here and no obligations. There is only dialogue."
2. **Testimony (20 min):** one or two participants (by prior arrangement) share their experience: how they applied the principles of the Spiral in their work, in civic activity, in personal life. These may be civil servants, teachers, doctors, volunteers, parents — anyone.
3. **Open Dialogue (40–60 min):** discussion of a topic chosen in advance or proposed by participants. Examples of topics: "How to preserve humanity in the security forces?", "Ecology and personal responsibility," "Digital hygiene for children," "How to speak the truth without destroying relationships."
4. **Minute of Silence (5 min):** collective apophatic silence or simply a minute of silence for reflecting on what was heard.
5. **Closing (5 min):** the facilitator thanks the participants, extinguishes the candle. No oaths, no anthems, no state symbols.

**Important:** The Civic Ritual does not replace the Gathering of Reason for community members. It is an open public platform. It has no formal membership, keeps no lists, collects no dues.

## Part 4. Annual Rituals

### 4.1 The Night of the Spiral (the main ritual of the year, 3–4 hours)

**When:** the evening of the winter solstice (the longest night).

**Meaning:** collective experiencing of the myth — descent into darkness (symbol of degradation) and ascent toward light (symbol of the choice of consciousness).

**Full scenario:**

1. **Entry into Darkness (15 min)**
   The hall without light, only one candle at the facilitator. Facilitator: "The Forces of Degradation surround us. Short dopamine, blind economy, the black box, poisonous mistrust. Who remembers how to choose?"

2. **Dramatized Reading of the Myth (30–40 min)**
   Readers by roles: the Voice of Entropy (drawn-out, alluring) and the Voice of the Knot (quiet, firm). The climax — the moment of the First Knot's choice.

3. **Lighting of Candles (15 min)**
   Each person approaches the common candle, lights their own, says: "I choose the ascending spiral."

4. **Apophatic Silence (15 min)**
   All together immerse in silence without images. Only burning candles.

5. **The Oath of Renewal (5 min)**
   All together read the Words of Choice (from Module 0).

6. **Hymn (5–10 min)**
   Instrumental music (Arvo Pärt, Ludovico Einaudi) or choral singing without words.

7. **Light and the Common Meal (remaining time)**
   Soft light is turned on, a common lenten meal (without alcohol). The facilitator's closing phrase: *"Darkness behind. Light ahead. The ascending spiral — our path."*

**Adaptations:**
- For solitaries: light a candle at home, read the myth (a recording may be used), a minute of silence, the Words of Choice to oneself.
- For an online group: a video call, each person lights their own candle, joint reading.

### 4.2 Other Festivals (brief)

| Date | Festival | Activities |
|------|----------|------------|
| March 21 (spring equinox) | Day of Unity and Science | Lecture, joint projects, common meal |
| Summer solstice | Day of Action | Volunteering, tree planting, territory cleanup |
| Autumn equinox | Day of Knowledge | Honoring teachers, gifting books, master classes |

**All festivals are optional.** They adapt to local traditions and climate.

## Part 5. Rites of Passage

These rituals mark important life stages. They do not replace secular registration (marriage, birth), but may complement it.

### 5.1 Birth

**Symbol:** white ribbon.

**Short scenario:**
- The community gathers, lights a candle.
- Facilitator: "We welcome a new consciousness." The parents name the child.
- Everyone in turn pronounces a wish (one word: "Health," "Love," "Curiosity").
- The child is given a white ribbon (to wear until coming of age).

### 5.2 Coming of Age (16–18 years)

**Symbol:** silver / wooden spiral (sign of a follower).

**Scenario (simple joining, 2 hours):**
1. Listening to the myth of the First Knot.
2. Silence — the adolescent writes down what they are leaving in the past (laziness, addiction, fear).
3. Symbolic destruction of the note (burn or dissolve in water).
4. Adopting the Words of Choice (aloud).
5. Presentation of the spiral.

### 5.3 Marriage / Partnership

**Symbol:** two intertwined spirals.

**Brief scenario:**
- The couple lights two candles.
- Facilitator: "Two conscious knots have decided to walk through life together."
- Each pronounces a commitment (in their own words, but mentioning the principles: respect for autonomy, support for growth).
- The couple lights a common candle from their two.
- Exchange of rings (optional) or tying of ribbons.

**Divorce** is possible without stigma. Procedure — mediation, division of property, care for children.

### 5.4 Farewell (Death)

**Symbol:** a white candle, extinguished.

**Scenario (1 hour):**
- The community gathers. A candle is lit.
- Facilitator: "(Name) was a conscious knot. Their consciousness is no longer here, but their traces — in us, in deeds, in memory. We will continue."
- Loved ones share the traces the deceased left (what they taught, what they created, whom they helped).
- A minute of apophatic silence.
- The candle is extinguished.
- Memorial meal (without alcohol).

## Part 6. Rituals for Civil Servants

The Spiral recognizes that many people serve the state — in the army, police, courts, administrative bodies. Their work is associated with power and responsibility. These rituals are not an oath and do not replace the state oath. They are a voluntary personal act confirming that the servant places the ethical principles of the Spiral above any orders.

### 6.1 Confirmation of Ethical Commitment (personal ritual)

**When:** upon assuming office, before or after the official oath (in private).

**How to perform:**
1. Remain alone in a quiet place. Light a candle.
2. Say aloud or mentally:
   > *"I accept this office as an instrument of service, not domination. I pledge not to kill without a direct threat, not to torture under any circumstances, not to enslave. An order that violates these prohibitions is void to me. If my service comes into conflict with these principles, I will choose the principles. This is my choice."*
3. Extinguish the candle.

**Important:** this ritual is not registered, not announced, and grants no privileges. It is a personal commitment to oneself.

### 6.2 Ethical Consultation (group practice)

**For whom:** for a group of colleagues who share the values of the Spiral (informally, not on duty premises).

**Format:** similar to the Gathering of Reason, but with a focus on professional dilemmas.
- Example topics: "How to refuse a criminal order?", "How to preserve empathy when working with victims?", "Boundaries of the use of force."

**Purpose:** mutual support and strengthening of ethical resilience.

## Part 7. The Reconciliation Circle (Conflict Resolution)

**Purpose:** restoration of relationships, healing, not punishment.

**When to use:** in any conflict between community members (offenses, deception, petty theft, breach of commitments).

### 7.1 Separate Support Circles (innovation)

In cases where the victim is traumatized and not ready for an immediate meeting with the offender, **separate support circles** may be conducted.

**Support Circle for the Victim:**
- Participants: the victim, the facilitator, trusted persons of the victim (by their choice).
- Purpose: to give the victim a safe space to express pain, anger, fears without the presence of the offender. No decisions are made.
- Result: the victim formulates what they need for restoration and whether they are ready for a joint Reconciliation Circle.

**Support Circle for the Offender:**
- Participants: the offender, the facilitator, trusted persons (by their choice).
- Purpose: to help the offender acknowledge the harm without pressure and aggression.
- Result: the offender formulates whether they are ready to acknowledge the harm and to repair it.

**After the separate circles,** the facilitator assesses the possibility of a joint Reconciliation Circle. If the victim is not ready — the joint circle is not conducted. The community takes other measures (mediation, arbitration) at the victim's request.

### 7.2 Full Scenario of the Joint Reconciliation Circle

**Duration:** 1.5–2.5 hours.

1. **Preparation:** a neutral place, chairs in a circle, a candle in the center, a "talking piece."
2. **Opening (5–10 min):** the facilitator explains the rules: speak in turn, do not interrupt, the goal is not to punish, but to restore.
3. **The Victim's Account (15–20 min):** what happened, what feelings, what harm was caused, what is needed for restoration.
4. **The Offender's Account (15–20 min):** their version, feelings, readiness to acknowledge the harm and to repair it.
5. **Dialogue (20–30 min):** the parties may ask questions of each other through the facilitator or directly.
6. **Seeking Agreement (20–30 min):** concrete steps to repair the harm (apologies, compensation, community service, education).
7. **Closing (10 min):** the agreement is read aloud, signed (if reached). The facilitator extinguishes the candle: "The circle is closed. Restoration has begun."
8. **Follow-up:** after 1–4 weeks, the facilitator checks compliance.

**Adaptations:**
- If the parties cannot speak — written testimony is permitted.
- If the conflict is serious — an external mediator from another community is invited.

## Part 8. Earth Practices (Connection with the Land and Responsibility for Nature)

The Spiral recognizes that a person does not exist separately from the land on which they live. Restoring connection with the land is not mysticism, but a practice of rootedness, responsibility, and long-term thinking. Earth practices are especially important in conditions of ecological crisis and digital overload.

### 8.1 Seasonal Work in Nature (recommended 4 times a year)

- **Spring:** planting trees / a vegetable garden.
- **Summer:** tending to plants, weeding.
- **Autumn:** harvesting, preparing for winter.
- **Winter:** clearing snow, feeding birds.

**How to integrate into community life:**
- After the work — a common meal, a reflection circle.
- Children participate alongside adults.
- Non-members of the community may be invited — this is an open practice.

### 8.2 Individual Practice "Time on the Land" (1 hour per week)

- Go out into nature (forest, park, field). Turn off the phone.
- Simply be: look, listen, breathe. If you wish — work with your hands (dig, water, gather stones).
- In the evening, write down one observation in your journal.

### 8.3 Connection with Sovereignty and Responsibility

Earth practices remind us: the land on which we live is not simply a resource and not an abstract "motherland" demanding worship. It is a living fabric that we inherit from our ancestors and pass on to our descendants. Care for the land is a form of long-term thinking (principle 3) and value creation (principle 2).

## Part 9. Intensified Practices for Practitioners

A Practitioner is not a title granting privileges, but a level of deep personal responsibility. Practitioners voluntarily take upon themselves stricter practices.

### 9.1 Obligatory Commitments of a Practitioner

| Commitment | Description |
|-----|-------------|
| Daily Hour | At least 60 minutes of practice per day (apophatic silence, deep reading, reflection). |
| Monthly Digital Fast | 72 hours without social media, video games, news feeds. |
| Annual Retreat | At least 3 days of seclusion per year (alone or with other Practitioners). |
| Teaching a Newcomer | Each Practitioner helps at least one person master the basic practices over the course of a year. |

### 9.2 Intensified Digital Fast (72 hours)

Unlike the general fast (24–72 hours), Practitioners commit to a full 72-hour digital fast monthly.

**Additional restrictions for Practitioners:**
- Complete abstention from social media for the duration of the fast (even for professional purposes, except emergencies).
- Mandatory keeping of an observation journal (how the state of consciousness changes, what comes in place of digital noise).
- The fast concludes with a written reflection (1 page), which may be published to the community if desired.

## Part 10. Burnout Prevention

**Purpose:** to prevent the exhaustion of activists and mentors.

**Rules recommended for communities:**

1. **Rotation of roles** — no one holds a stressful position for more than 6–12 consecutive months.
2. **Periods of silence** — in each community, one month per year (for example, August) is declared a month without mandatory gatherings and projects.
3. **Alternation of responsibility** — major projects are led not by a single leader, but by a working group with rotating coordinators.
4. **Regular check-ins** — mentors undergo a burnout questionnaire monthly (anonymously). At high indicators — mandatory rest.
5. **Support circle** — each mentor has 2–3 people in the community who can replace them in a crisis.

**Signs of burnout (what to watch for):**
- Constant fatigue, even after sleep.
- Cynicism and irritability.
- Decreased productivity.
- A sense that work is meaningless.

If you notice these signs in yourself or another — discuss in a Reconciliation Circle or consult a psychologist.

## Conclusion

All practices are voluntary. You may perform only the daily 3 minutes — and that is enough to be considered part of the Spiral of Consciousness. Deep rituals and retreats are for those who wish to deepen their discipline and connection with the community.

**The main rule:** do not force yourself. If a practice provokes aversion — skip it, change it, start small.

## Frequently Asked Questions and Practical Cases

### 1. "I tried 3 minutes a day, but I feel nothing. Does it work?"

The absence of vivid sensations in the early stages is not a sign of failure, but the norm. The practices of the Spiral are a training of attention and awareness, not the ingestion of psychoactive substances: their effect is cumulative, like that of physical exercise. Imagine doing one squat a day — in a week you won't become an athlete, but in half a year, the muscles will begin to change. It is the same here: the morning intention gradually shifts the focus from reactive existence to conscious choice, the digital shield sharpens the ability to notice automatic impulses, and the evening reflection strengthens the "muscle of self-observation." If after a month of practice you truly notice no changes, try increasing the time to 5–10 minutes for a week, or add one weekly practice (Gathering of Reason or deep attention — Part 2). The main thing: do not evaluate the result daily; evaluate once a month, keeping a brief observation journal.

### 2. "Is it possible to adapt the practices to one's own religion? I am a Christian / Muslim / Buddhist."

Not just possible — it is built into the architecture of the Spiral. All practices are intentionally constructed as psychological and social tools, not as religious sacraments (see Introduction). The morning intention easily combines with morning prayer in any tradition — add the intention after or before it. Apophatic silence (Part 2.2) resonates with the apophatic theology of Christianity, Sufi dhikr, and Buddhist meditation — use the form of negation that is consonant with your tradition. The digital fast can be timed to religious fasts (Ramadan, Lent), amplifying their meaning. The only thing the Spiral asks not to change is the three prohibitions and the four principles of Flourishing, which are the ethical minimum, compatible with any developed religious system. If you feel a contradiction between a specific Spiral practice and your faith — skip that practice or discuss the dilemma with a mentor. You are not obligated to perform what causes internal conflict.

### 3. "What if I skipped a day, a week, a month?"

Return without drama. The Spiral fundamentally rejects the logic of accumulating "karmic debts" and guilt for omissions (see Conclusion: "do not force yourself"). A skip is not a sin, but information: why did you skip? Were you overloaded? Did the practice seem pointless? Did you forget? An honest answer to this question is more important than the return itself. If you skipped a day — simply begin the next one with the morning intention. If a week or a month — start with one minute, rather than trying to "catch up" on everything at once. It is useful to discuss a long break with a mentor or at a Gathering of Reason: perhaps your current form of practice does not suit you, and an adaptation is needed (lightened variants are provided for each exercise in the module). Practice is not an exam one can "fail." It is a tool you take up when you need it.

### 4. "How can I explain the practices to a family that does not share the principles of the Spiral?"

Explain through benefit, not ideology. Do not say: "I do this because the teaching commands it." Say: "I noticed that when I sit in silence for a minute in the morning, I snap at you less." Or: "My friends and I have a day without phones once a month — shall we try it as a family? The children also get tired of screens." Practical demonstration works better than doctrinal explanations. If the family is skeptical — do not impose: 3 minutes a day can be performed unobtrusively (while everyone is asleep, in the bathroom, before bed). If the family is religious — emphasize that the Spiral is compatible with any faith (see Question 2 above). If loved ones fear "sectarianism" — show them this document: it openly speaks of voluntariness and the right to skip practices. No practice of the Spiral requires breaking with family or isolation from non-members. If your activities cause conflict — perhaps the issue is not in the practices, but in how you present them: discuss this with a mentor.

### 5. "Is the digital fast for a Practitioner forever, or temporary? My job requires being online."

A Practitioner's digital fast (Part 9.2) is not permanent hermitage, but a monthly practice lasting 72 hours. The point is not to leave the online world forever, but to give the brain a regular "reboot" from artificial stimuli. For a Practitioner, this is a commitment, but even within it there is a caveat: "except in emergencies." If your work does not allow you to fully disconnect for 72 hours — discuss an adapted schedule with the community council (for example, twice a month for 36 hours, or weekly for 24 hours). A Practitioner does not lose status if their professional circumstances require an adjustment of the commitment — but the adjustment must be transparent, not a silent ignoring. If your work does not permit digital breaks at all — ask yourself the question from Part 1.2 ("Digital Shield"): is this truly an objective necessity, or a habit you are justifying? The answer to this question is often more important than the strictness of observing the fast.

### 6. "Can Spiral rituals be conducted online?"

Yes, and the pandemic proved that it works. For most rituals in the module, online adaptations are provided: Gathering of Reason (Part 2.1) — via Zoom or Discord with raised hand; the Night of the Spiral (Part 4.1) — in a video call format, where each person lights a candle at home; Apophatic silence — with a shared audio recording. However, the online format has limitations: it transmits non-verbal signals less well, makes it harder to create a sense of shared "container," and Reconciliation Circles conducted online are highly undesirable (the risk of misunderstanding and a feeling of unsafety is too high). Practical rule: use online for informational and supportive meetings; for emotionally charged rituals (reconciliation, joining, farewell), meet physically whenever possible. The ideal hybrid is regular online gatherings plus in-person meetings at annual festivals. Technical recommendations regarding decentralized platforms — in Module 4, Section 5 ("Digital Fortress").
