## For all community members, mentors, civil servants, and everyone striving to preserve mental health and humanity in the spirit of the Spiral of Consciousness

---

### Introduction

Mental health is not a luxury, but the foundation for moving along the ascending spiral. Without it, it is impossible to develop oneself, to create, to help others, or to think about the long-term future. The Era of the Great Trial is not only about technological and ecological challenges, but also about an unprecedented load on the psyche: information noise, the loss of meaning, collective traumas of wars and catastrophes, and burnout among the helping professions.

This module provides **concrete, proven practices** for:

- Enhancing psychological resilience (personal and collective).
- Preventing burnout — especially among activists, mentors, military personnel, doctors, and rescue workers.
- Working with depression, anxiety, and anhedonia.
- Healing individual and collective traumas (war, disaster, violence).
- Restoring connection with the body, the land, and the community.

All practices are voluntary, adaptable to any culture, age, ability, and income level. They do not replace professional medical and psychotherapeutic help, but supplement it with community-based support tools.

## Section 1. Foundational Models of Well-Being

### 1.1. The PERMA Model (Martin Seligman)

Five elements of authentic flourishing:

| Element | What It Means | How to Cultivate in the Community |
|---------|---------------|-----------------------------------|
| **P** – Positive Emotions | Joy, gratitude, hope | Gratitude rituals, celebrations |
| **E** – Engagement | A state of total absorption, flow | Deep attention practice, retreats |
| **R** – Relationships | Meaningful connections, support | Circles of Empathy, Assemblies of Reason, Ubuntu care |
| **M** – Meaning | Belonging to something larger | The Myth of the First Knot, common projects, service |
| **A** – Accomplishment | A sense of mastery, progress | Levels of belonging, mentorship |

### 1.2. Post-Traumatic Growth (PTG)

Research shows that after severe upheavals, people can not only recover but **grow**:

- Strengthened personal power ("I became stronger than I thought").
- Deepened relationships (greater appreciation for loved ones).
- New possibilities (change of vocation, re-evaluation of values).
- Greater gratitude for life.
- Spiritual development.

**The community's task:** to create conditions where a person can travel the path from trauma to growth — through safety, support, and the opportunity for meaning-making.

## Section 2. Burnout Prevention (for Everyone, Especially the Helping Professions)

Burnout is not weakness, but a signal that the system is overloaded or the person lacks resources.

### 2.1. Signs of Burnout

- **Emotional exhaustion:** constant fatigue, even after sleep; apathy.
- **Depersonalisation:** indifference toward those one helps; cynicism; irritability.
- **Reduced personal accomplishment:** a feeling of "I'm not changing anything," procrastination.
- **Physical symptoms:** headaches, insomnia, gastrointestinal problems.

### 2.2. Recovery Programme

| Phase | Actions | Duration |
|-------|---------|----------|
| **Immediate actions** | Full release from duties, sleep, walks, abstinence from digital devices (minimum 3 hours per day) | 3 days |
| **Meaning-making** | Journaling, an individual session with a mentor or psychologist, role review | 1–2 weeks |
| **Return** | Gradual reintegration into light roles, training in self-regulation techniques, a new schedule with mandatory days off | 1 month |

### 2.3. Preventive Measures in the Community

- **Role rotation** – no one holds a stressful position for more than 1 year.
- **Max 2 roles per person.**
- **Check-in at every Assembly of Reason** – a brief circle where each person says how they are feeling.
- **Monthly anonymous burnout questionnaire.**
- **Budget for psychological support** in the mutual-aid fund.

## Section 3. Working with Depression, Anhedonia, and Anxiety

### 3.1. Behavioural Activation (for Depression and Anhedonia)

**Principle:** action precedes motivation, not the other way around.

**The "Micro-Steps" practice:** break the task down into the tiniest steps ("leave the house" → "open the door" → "stand on the threshold" → "take 5 steps"). Complete the smallest step and do not demand more. Reward yourself. Tomorrow, add one more step.

### 3.2. The "Grounding" Technique (5‑4‑3‑2‑1) for Anxiety or Panic

Name **5** things you can see → **4** sounds you can hear → **3** sensations in the body → **2** smells → **1** action you will take right now.

### 3.3. Circles of Empathy (Group Support)

**Important distinction:** a Circle of Empathy is **not** a Circle of Reconciliation. In a Circle of Empathy, there is no conflict and there are no sides. The goal is not to resolve a dispute, but simply to listen and support.

| | Circle of Empathy | Circle of Reconciliation |
|---|---|---|
| **Goal** | Emotional support, listening | Restoring relationships after conflict |
| **Participants** | Anyone in need of support | Victim, offender, witnesses |
| **Rules** | Only the speaker speaks; advice and judgment are forbidden | Structured dialogue; seeking agreement |
| **Facilitator** | A facilitator ensuring safety | A trained facilitator observing the Code (Module 5) |

**Rules of the Circle of Empathy:**
- Only the person holding the "talking piece" may speak.
- Others remain silent, do not give advice, do not judge, do not interrupt.
- Words of support are permitted: "Thank you for sharing," "I hear you," "That must be hard."

## Section 4. Land-Based Healing

Contact with the land (gardening, forest, river) lowers cortisol levels and restores a sense of connection with the cyclical nature of time. In the community: a community garden, "Friday on the land," seasonal work.

**Connection to trauma:** for people who have experienced violence or catastrophe, working with the land often proves to be a gentler and safer path to recovery than verbal therapy.

## Section 5. Ubuntu Care: Mutual Support Networks

**Principle:** "I am because we are." Caring for mental health is a collective responsibility.

**Circle of Care (4–6 people):**
- Meets once a week for 30 minutes.
- Participants share: "How am I? What do I need? How can I help others?"
- Care coordinator (rotated every 3 months).

**Tasks of the circle:** to notice burnout, depression, isolation; to organise practical help; simply to be present.

## Section 6. Culturally Sensitive Approaches

The Spiral respects traditional healing practices, rituals, and the role of elders and community leaders. Western models of therapy must not be imposed. When working with trauma linked to colonialism or repression, facilitators from the affected group must be involved. Cultural humility is not optional — it is essential to ethical practice.

## Section 7. Working with Collective Trauma: War, Disaster, Terrorist Attack

### 7.1. What Is Collective Trauma?

It is a wound inflicted not on one person, but on an entire group, community, or people. It destroys the basic sense of safety, trust in the world, and trust in one another. Examples: war, genocide, a major terrorist attack, a devastating earthquake, forced displacement.

The Spiral acknowledges that collective trauma cannot be healed by individual therapy alone. Community rituals, collective meaning-making, and the restoration of shared memory are needed.

### 7.2. Protocol for Psychological Aid in Natural Disasters and Terrorist Attacks (for the Community)

**Phase 1. Immediate Actions (First 72 Hours)**
1.  **Ensuring physical safety** – priority. Remove people from the danger zone, provide shelter, water, food, medicine.
2.  **Restoring connection** – help people find their loved ones. The community activates notification chains (Module 4).
3.  **Psychological First Aid (PFA)** – simple actions that anyone can be trained to perform:
    - Be present. Do not demand that they talk about what they went through.
    - Speak calmly, in a quiet voice.
    - Offer simple help: water, a blanket, a place to sit, a phone call.
    - Do not promise that "everything will be fine." Promise that "I am here, and we will get through this together."
    - Do not invalidate feelings: do not say "don't cry," "calm down," "others have it worse."

**Phase 2. Stabilisation (First 2 Weeks)**
1.  **Circles of Empathy** – daily or every other day. No coercion. Anyone may come and simply remain silent.
2.  **Land-based practices** – collective cleaning of the territory, planting, cooking. Physical labour helps restore a sense of control over the world.
3.  **Children's circles** – separate, using games, drawing, fairy tales. Children experience trauma differently from adults.

**Phase 3. Meaning-Making and Integration (Months)**
1.  **Memory rituals.** The community creates a symbolic space (a memory wall, candles, a tree) and holds a collective ritual of farewell or honouring. This is not a religious ceremony, but a psychological tool for completing the cycle.
2.  **Weaving a shared narrative.** Those who wish may record or tell their stories. The community collects them in a "Book of Memory." This helps transform the chaos of the experience into meaningful experience.
3.  **Referral to specialists.** Those who still have acute symptoms after a month (flashbacks, insomnia, panic attacks, refusal to eat) are referred to professional psychologists or psychiatrists at the community fund's expense.

### 7.3. The Community's Role in Recovery

The community does not replace emergency services and state agencies, but acts faster and more humanely at the local level. Its main resource is the trust and cohesion formed before the catastrophe.

## Section 8. Working with the Trauma of Combatants (Without Glorifying War)

The Spiral recognises that people who have been through war — whether soldiers, militia members, or civilians caught in the conflict zone — carry a deep psychological burden. Their experience must be neither glorified nor stigmatised.

### 8.1. Key Principles

1.  **No glorification of war.** War is always a failure of human reason and ethics. People who have been through it are not "superhumans" and not "psychos," but the same conscious nodes who have sustained a severe trauma.
2.  **No stigmatisation.** Guilt, nightmares, aggression, alienation — these are normal reactions to abnormal circumstances. The Spiral does not condemn those who were forced to kill in self-defence (direct threat to life), but it does not exempt them from the necessity of making sense of that experience.
3.  **Recovery through meaning-making.** The psyche heals not through forgetting, but through integrating the experience into one's personal story. The community provides space for this.

### 8.2. Practical Steps for the Community

1.  **Circles of Empathy for returnees.** Special circles where participants may speak of their experience without judgment and without coercion to share. The facilitator must understand the specifics of combat trauma or work in tandem with a consultant who has such experience.
2.  **Working with the body.** For many veterans, verbal therapy is ineffective or painful. Land-based practices, physical labour, breathing exercises, apophatic silence (Module 3) provide access to the body where trauma is locked, bypassing words.
3.  **The ritual of laying down the burden.** A voluntary symbolic ritual in which a participant may "leave" their burden — write on paper what torments them and burn it; or speak it to a tree, water, fire. This is not magic, but a psychological act of release.
4.  **Integration into the community.** Do not rush. Give opportunities to participate in simple common tasks (garden, repairs, cooking) without demanding immediate socialisation.
5.  **Ethical meaning-making.** For those who are ready — an examination of the situation through the lens of the Three Prohibitions and the principles of the Spiral. Not to pass judgment, but to understand: where was there a direct threat to life, and where was there revenge, cruelty, or the execution of a criminal order. This is a difficult but necessary path to restoring ethical integrity.

### 8.3. The Community and the State

The Spiral calls on states to create psychological rehabilitation programmes for veterans, to fund research on combat trauma, and to ensure access to quality therapy. Spiral communities may act as partners, providing informal support practices.

## Section 9. Psychological Resilience for Professionals Confronting Violence

Doctors, rescue workers, police officers, firefighters, social workers, journalists working in hot spots — all of them come into daily contact with suffering, death, and cruelty. This leads to **vicarious trauma** — a state in which the helper themselves begins to experience symptoms of trauma, even without being a direct victim.

### 9.1. Prevention of Vicarious Trauma

1.  **Regular supervision.** Discussion of difficult cases with colleagues or a mentor with psychological training. No less than once a month.
2.  **The "Taking Off the Uniform" ritual.** A symbolic action that separates the work role from personal life. For example: before leaving work, wash your hands, change clothes, take three deep breaths, say: "I leave others' pain here. I am going home to myself." This is not magic, but a practice of psychological hygiene.
3.  **Prohibition on heroism at any cost.** The professional is not obliged to be impenetrable. Asking for help is not weakness. The community and the professional community must encourage seeking support, not stigmatise it.

### 9.2. Ethical Dilemmas and Psychological Defence

Professionals working in the security forces may encounter orders that violate the Three Prohibitions. **Moral injury** is the wound that arises when a person commits or witnesses an act that contradicts their deepest values. It is often heavier than the fear of death.

**Tools for working with moral injury:**
- Acknowledgement: "I saw this / did this, and it was wrong."
- Speaking about it in a safe environment (Circle of Empathy, confession before a mentor, individual therapy).
- Restorative action: if possible, offer apologies, participate in helping the victims, work toward changing the system.

## Section 10. Psychology Under a Repressive State

Life under conditions of repression generates particular psychological challenges: chronic fear, learned helplessness, a split personality (public and private), denunciation as a norm, collective denial.

**Practices for preserving the psyche:**
- **Small circles of trust.** 3–5 people with whom one can be completely candid. This need not be a Spiral community.
- **Documenting reality.** Keeping a diary, recording events — this is a way to maintain a connection with reality when official propaganda denies it.
- **Preserving meaning.** Even in inhuman conditions, a person can retain dignity: help another, speak the truth when possible, refuse complicity in a crime.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### 1. "How do I know if it's burnout and not just fatigue?"

Fatigue goes away after a weekend. Burnout does not. Three markers that distinguish burnout from ordinary fatigue. First: you wake up tired, even after eight hours of sleep, and this repeats for weeks. Second: what used to bring joy or at least neutral interest now causes irritation or dull indifference — this is depersonalisation, a defence mechanism of the psyche that disengages empathy for self-preservation. Third: a persistent feeling of "I'm not changing anything" or "it's all pointless," which is not alleviated by objective successes. Add somatic symptoms: headaches, insomnia without an obvious cause, gastrointestinal problems. If three of these signals persist for more than two weeks — it is not fatigue, it is burnout. The recovery programme from Section 2 begins with immediate release from duties for three days — do not ask yourself whether you "deserve" rest, simply give it to yourself as a medical prescription.

### 2. "Can collective trauma across generations be healed?"

Not "erased," but "integrated" — yes. Collective trauma (war, genocide, forced displacement) becomes inscribed in the fabric of culture and is transmitted through family patterns, silence, rituals of avoidance. Complete "healing" in the sense of returning to a state "as if nothing happened" is impossible — and unnecessary: trauma becomes part of identity. But it is possible and necessary to travel the path from trauma to post-traumatic growth. Three conditions are required: safety (physical and psychological), the possibility of speaking (not compulsory!), and a collective ritual of meaning-making. Section 7 describes concrete steps: memory rituals, weaving a shared narrative through the "Book of Memory," Circles of Empathy without pressure. The most important principle: what heals is not forgetting, but integration — when a community says: "This happened to us, we remember, we grieve, and we choose to live on, without allowing trauma to define our future." This is work not for months, but for generations.

### 3. "How do I help a veteran who violated the prohibitions in war and now suffers?"

This is the most difficult case of moral injury — the wound not from what was done to you, but from what you yourself did. First and foremost: do not judge. If the veteran has come with this burden voluntarily — they already acknowledge that what was done contradicts their deepest values, and that is a step toward healing. Second: offer a Circle of Empathy specifically designed for returnees, where one may speak without judgment and without coercion to share. Third: working with the body is often more effective than words — land-based practices, physical labour, breathing exercises provide access to trauma locked in the body. Fourth: when the person is ready, help them conduct an ethical examination of the situation through the lens of the Three Prohibitions — not to pass judgment, but to distinguish: where was a direct threat to life, and where was revenge or the execution of a criminal order. And finally, the ritual of laying down the burden (Section 8) — a voluntary symbolic act that allows one to "leave the load." It is important to remember: the restoration of ethical integrity is possible, but it requires time, a safe space, and a community that neither stigmatises nor whitewashes.

### 4. "What if I can't find a therapist who shares the Spiral's principles?"

A therapist need not share the Spiral's principles in order to help you effectively. Far more important is that they share professional ethical principles: non-judgment, confidentiality, respect for client autonomy. Seek a specialist who is proficient in evidence-based methods (CBT, EMDR for trauma, logotherapy, client-centred therapy) and has experience working with themes that matter to you — moral injury, existential crisis, burnout in the helping professions. At the first consultation, ask directly: "I hold certain ethical principles — in particular, an absolute prohibition on violence and coercion. It is important to me that therapy does not push me to revise them. Are you comfortable with this?" A good specialist will answer honestly. In parallel, use community tools: Circles of Empathy, Circles of Care, mentorship — they do not replace therapy, but create a supportive environment that even the best therapist cannot provide. If your region has no access to quality help at all, consider online therapy — it expands the geographic scope of the search.
