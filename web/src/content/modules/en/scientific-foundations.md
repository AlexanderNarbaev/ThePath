## For those who want to understand — not only feel

The Spiral of Consciousness does not require belief in the supernatural. Its principles — preserve and multiply consciousness, think long-term, do not kill, do not torture, do not enslave — are derived from observations of reality and are testable through daily experience. But the Spiral did not emerge in a vacuum. Many of its intuitions find confirmation — or rather, resonance — in modern scientific disciplines and philosophical traditions.

This appendix is not a "proof" of the Spiral. The Spiral does not depend on the correctness of any specific scientific theory. It stands on its own axioms — as geometry stands on its postulates, regardless of who formulated them and how. But for those who find it important to see the connection between intuition and knowledge, between myth and fact — these parallels may strengthen trust in the path.

After all, if the Spiral describes reality — it is unsurprising that reality describes the Spiral.

## 1. The Second Law of Thermodynamics and Negentropy

The central metaphor of the Spiral — the opposition between consciousness and entropy — has a direct correspondence in physics. The Second Law of Thermodynamics states: in an isolated system, entropy (a measure of disorder) does not decrease. Everything hot cools down, everything ordered tends toward chaos, everything complex tends toward simplicity. This is not a metaphor but one of the most reliable laws of nature. Leave a system alone, and it will degrade — always.

But there is a nuance: an *isolated* system. Living systems are open. They exchange matter and energy with their surroundings. Erwin Schrödinger, in *What Is Life?* (1944), formulated this with crystalline clarity: an organism "feeds on negative entropy" — it exports disorder outward, maintaining internal order at the cost of increasing the total disorder of the universe. Life is not a violation of the Second Law; it is a local island of order in a sea of chaos.

Ilya Prigogine (Nobel Prize, 1977) went further: he showed that far from thermodynamic equilibrium, matter is capable of *self-organization*. Dissipative structures — from vortices in fluids to chemical clocks — spontaneously form complex, ordered patterns while dissipating energy and entropy. Order does not contradict the Second Law; it emerges within it, using the flow of energy.

The Spiral extends this logic to the level of consciousness. Consciousness is not merely the maintenance of order (all living systems do that). Consciousness *creates new forms of order* — art, science, institutions, meaning. It does not simply consume negative entropy — it generates it, crystallizing the chaos of experience into structures of understanding. Where entropy simplifies everything toward a gray homogeneity, consciousness complexifies everything — toward a symphony, a theorem, a moral choice.

This is not poetry. It is observation: informational entropy (Shannon) and thermodynamic entropy (Boltzmann) are connected — the formula is the same, up to a constant. By creating meaningful information, consciousness literally creates local minima of entropy — islands of complexification. The Spiral describes this process as an ethical imperative: multiply consciousness = create negentropy.

**Limits of knowledge:** The connection between thermodynamic and informational entropy remains a subject of ongoing debate. Experimental demonstrations of Landauer's principle (erasing a bit of information requires the dissipation of heat) confirm the connection at the micro level, but the macroscopic picture is more complex. The Spiral does not require this connection to be complete and proven — it is sufficient that it is heuristically fruitful.

## 2. The Neuroscience of Attention

The Fourth Principle of the Spiral — "preserve and multiply consciousness" — may sound abstract. Neuroscience gives it the utmost concreteness.

Conscious attention is the most limited human resource. The bandwidth of conscious information processing is estimated at roughly 4 bits per second — approximately one short word. Everything else is processed automatically, below the threshold of awareness. Evolution created mechanisms that economize attention — automatisms, habits, filters. Without them, we would drown in the sensory stream.

But this scarce resource has become the primary target of the digital economy. The dopamine system — an ancient learning mechanism that signals unexpected reward — has become the target of algorithms designed to maximize "engagement." Infinite feeds, notifications, autoplay, likes — all exploit vulnerabilities in our neurobiology. Dopamine is released not upon receiving a reward, but in *anticipation* of one — which is why we keep scrolling, further, further, further. This is not a weakness of character. It is a precise technological targeting of the brain's architecture.

Neuroimaging shows that excessive social media use activates the same brain regions and neurotransmitter pathways as chemical addictions. The amygdala (a center of fear and aggression) becomes more reactive. The prefrontal cortex (a center of self-control and long-term planning) becomes less active. A feedback loop forms: less control → more scrolling → less control.

The reverse process is also well-documented. MRI studies of mindfulness meditation show:
- **Increased gray matter density** in the prefrontal cortex, insula, and hippocampus — regions responsible for attention, self-awareness, and memory.
- **Decreased volume and reactivity of the amygdala** — a literal reduction in baseline anxiety and emotional reactivity.
- **Strengthened connectivity between the prefrontal cortex and amygdala** — the brain learns to "brake" emotional surges.

Importantly, these changes do not require decades of practice. They occur within 8 weeks of regular practice (Sara Lazar's study, Massachusetts General Hospital, 2011; replicated many times). The brain is plastic. Attention is trainable.

The Spiral's "digital shield" — the practice of attention management: periods of focused work without notifications, conscious information consumption, evening reflection, digital fasting — these are not moralistic prescriptions. They are, in essence, evidence-based attention training aimed at strengthening the neural networks that support self-control and long-term thinking.

**The Default Mode Network (DMN)** deserves special mention. This is a network of brain regions active when we are not engaged in a specific task — "mind-wandering." The DMN is associated with self-reference, autobiographical memory, and future planning. During meditation, DMN activity decreases while its internal connectivity increases. The mind does not "go quiet" — it reconfigures. It becomes not quieter, but *more gathered*. This is precisely what the Spiral calls "depth of consciousness."

**Limits of knowledge:** The neuroscience of attention is a young field, and many of its conclusions are based on small samples and correlational studies. The mechanisms of transferring attention training to everyday life remain insufficiently studied. However, the direction of effects is robust, and practical recommendations — limit multitasking, train sustained attention, alternate periods of focus and diffusion — are confirmed by both laboratory and field studies.

## 3. Evolutionary Ethics and Cooperation

The Three Prohibitions of the Spiral — do not kill, do not torture, do not enslave — may appear to be arbitrary moral rules. Evolutionary biology offers a different view: they are rational conditions for the existence of any cooperative system.

Is Darwinian evolution a "war of all against all"? No. From the very beginning, biologists observed that cooperation is as fundamental as competition. Kropotkin, in *Mutual Aid: A Factor of Evolution* (1902), documented cooperative behavior in thousands of species. But it took a century to understand the *mechanisms*.

**Kin selection:** William Hamilton (1964) demonstrated mathematically that an organism may sacrifice itself for relatives because they carry copies of its genes. Altruism within a family is evolutionarily rational.

**Reciprocal altruism:** Robert Trivers (1971) showed that cooperation between unrelated individuals can evolve if interactions are repeated. "I help you today, you help me tomorrow" — provided that tomorrow actually arrives and the partner can be recognized.

**Group selection:** a controversial but revived idea, championed by David Sloan Wilson and Edward O. Wilson — groups with higher levels of internal cooperation win the competition against less cooperative groups, even if within a group, egoists have an advantage. Multilevel selection: selfishness wins within a group, but cooperative groups defeat selfish ones.

**Game theory** gave mathematical rigor to these intuitions. Robert Axelrod, in *The Evolution of Cooperation* (1984), conducted a famous tournament: different strategies competed in the iterated prisoner's dilemma. A simple strategy won — Tit-for-Tat: begin with cooperation, then do what your partner did in the previous round. The conditions for its success:
1. Interactions are repeated (the shadow of the future).
2. Participants can identify each other.
3. The payoff from mutual cooperation exceeds the payoff from one-sided cheating.

Martin Nowak, in *SuperCooperators* (2011), generalized: cooperation evolves through five mechanisms — kin selection, direct reciprocity, indirect reciprocity (reputation), network reciprocity (spatial structure), and group selection.

What does this mean for the Three Prohibitions? Killing, torture, and enslavement destroy the basic conditions of the game — they make repeated interactions impossible (killing), they destroy trust (torture), or they abolish voluntariness (enslavement). In a system where such actions are permitted, cooperation collapses: no one will cooperate with someone who could kill, torture, or enslave them. The Spiral's Prohibitions are not a whim of moralists. They are *necessary conditions for an infinite repeated game* in which humanity is engaged.

**Limits of knowledge:** Evolutionary ethics explains the origin of moral intuitions but does not justify them — these are different logical levels (the naturalistic fallacy: from "is" does not follow "ought"). The Spiral derives its Prohibitions not from evolution but from the axiom of the value of consciousness. Evolutionary biology shows why beings with such axioms could have arisen — nothing more. But this "why" is useful: it removes the suspicion that ethics is an arbitrary invention. It has deep roots in the structure of reality.

## 4. Systems Thinking and Long-Term Planning

The Third Principle of the Spiral — "think long-term" — runs counter to deeply embedded properties of the human psyche. Behavioral economics and cognitive science have documented our systematic inability to adequately evaluate the future.

**Hyperbolic discounting:** We discount future benefits and threats not linearly, but steeply. 1,000 dollars today are subjectively more valuable than 1,200 dollars a month from now — but 1,000 dollars in 10 years and 1,200 dollars in 10 years and 1 month are perceived almost identically. The discounting curve drops steeply near the present and becomes nearly flat in the distant future. This explains procrastination, harmful habits, the inability to save for retirement — and, at the scale of civilization, climate inaction.

**Present bias** is a special case: we do not merely discount the future, we overvalue the *immediate* present. The choice between "eat the cake now" and "preserve health later" is consistently lost by the present.

Evolutionarily, this is understandable: our ancestors lived in an environment where the future was radically unpredictable, and immediate threats and opportunities were mortally important. But civilization has created a new environment — and our cognitive tools have not kept pace.

How institutions can correct for this systemic failure:
- **Sovereign wealth funds for future generations** (Norway, Alaska) — transform today's non-renewable resources into long-term financial assets.
- **Constitutional commitments** to future generations — enshrine long-term interests at the level of fundamental law, removing them from electoral cycles.
- **Independent long-term planning institutions** — the Spiral's proposed "Council of the Future": a body empowered to assess legislation and policy in terms of their impact on future generations, without veto power but with the right to issue public findings.

Philosophers working in this area:
- **Nick Bostrom** (*Future of Humanity*, 2024) — the concept of existential risks and the astronomical value of the future.
- **Toby Ord** (*The Precipice*, 2020) — quantitative assessment of human extinction risks.
- **William MacAskill** (*What We Owe the Future*, 2022) — longtermism: the ethical priority of influencing the long-term future.

**The Spiral's position** differs from MacAskill's longtermism. Longtermism asserts that influence on the distant future is the *primary* moral priority of our time, given the astronomical number of potential future lives. The Spiral agrees on the importance of long-term thinking but places it within the framework of the Four Principles, where it is *one of four*. Concern for the distant future must not become a sacrifice of the present. We cannot exchange the suffering of people alive today for the hypothetical good of people a million years from now. Long-term thinking is a compass, not blinders. It points the direction but does not blind us to what is happening here and now.

Moreover, the Spiral insists: the present is a bridge to the future. One cannot build the future by destroying the present — that is, by sacrificing the consciousness, cooperation, and dignity of those alive today. This is systems thinking in action: seeing the interconnection of times, rather than choosing between them.

**Limits of knowledge:** Longtermism is a young and actively debated field. Critics point to the speculative nature of estimates of the number of future lives, the problem of intergenerational utility aggregation, and the risk of neglecting the needs of currently living and non-human beings. The Spiral acknowledges these debates and takes a balanced position within them: the long-term future matters, but not absolutely.

## 5. Philosophical Foundation: From Stoicism to Ubuntu

The Spiral does not borrow ideas from philosophical traditions — rather, it recognizes itself in them. This is convergent evolution of ethical wisdom: different cultures, reflecting on human nature, independently arrived at insights resonant with the Spiral's axioms. Here are several key parallels.

**Stoicism** (Zeno, Epictetus, Marcus Aurelius, 3rd century BCE — 2nd century CE). The dichotomy of control — distinguishing what is in our power (judgments, intentions, reactions) from what is not (reputation, circumstances, others' opinions) — directly corresponds to the Spiral's practice of "focus on your choice, not the outcome." The inner citadel — the capacity to maintain ethical sovereignty regardless of external circumstances. Stoic cosmopolitanism — "I am a citizen of the world" — anticipates the Spiral's trans-tribal principle. The Spiral adds: an inner fortress is good, but bridges are also needed — stoicism without cooperation becomes isolation.

**Buddhism** (Siddhartha Gautama, 5th century BCE). The practice of mindfulness — sati — is one of the oldest documented methods of attention training. The concept of pratityasamutpada (dependent origination) — everything exists through relation to another — resonates with the Spiral's understanding of the network of Nodes. The Middle Way — avoiding the extremes of asceticism and hedonism — is structurally analogous to the balance between the "pain of growth" and the "joy of creation." A distinction: the Spiral does not seek liberation from desires but their conscious direction. The desire to create, to love, and to know is not a source of suffering but the engine of complexification.

**Ubuntu** (African philosophy). "Umuntu ngumuntu ngabantu" — "A person becomes a person through other people." Archbishop Desmond Tutu explained: ubuntu means that my humanity is inextricably intertwined with yours; when I diminish you, I diminish myself. This is a precise correspondence to one of the Spiral's fundamental principles: the Third Prohibition (do not enslave) — because by enslaving, you deny the subjecthood of another, and thus your own, for "you are a Node in the common Network." We are not isolated atoms; we are a network.

**Russian Cosmism** (Nikolai Fyodorov, Konstantin Tsiolkovsky, Vladimir Vernadsky, late 19th — early 20th centuries). The "common cause" — a voluntary, non-state unification of humanity to overcome death, chaos, and discord. The "regulation of nature" — not domination, but harmonization. The expansion into space not for flags, but for the expansion of consciousness. Vernadsky's noosphere — the planetary shell transformed by scientific thought — anticipates the Spiral's understanding of consciousness as a geological force. In contrast to techno-optimism, the Spiral insists: technology is a servant, not a master. And unlike certain currents of Cosmism, the Spiral rejects forced mobilization and state messianism.

**Immanuel Kant** (1724–1804). The Categorical Imperative in its second formulation: "Act so that you treat humanity — both in your own person and in the person of every other — always as an end, and never merely as a means." This is a direct philosophical formulation of the Third Prohibition (do not enslave). The Spiral extends it: not only to humanity, but to all consciousness capable of suffering or choosing. Kant's idea of the "kingdom of ends" — a community of rational beings recognizing each other's autonomy — resonates with the Spiral's Community.

**Confucius** (551–479 BCE). Ren — "humaneness," "benevolence" — the central virtue connecting two persons (the character is composed of the elements "person" and "two"). Self-cultivation through ritual (li), learning, and reflection. Harmony (he) — not uniformity, but coordinated diversity: "The noble person seeks harmony, not sameness; the petty person seeks sameness, not harmony." All of this directly resonates with the Spiral's practices: evening reflection, the Reconciliation Circle, the recognition of differences within a shared direction.

These parallels are not an attempt to appropriate others' traditions. On the contrary: the more cultures that independently arrived at similar conclusions about human nature and the conditions of a dignified life, the more reliable those conclusions are. The Spiral does not compete with philosophical and spiritual traditions — it offers a meeting point, a language in which they can recognize allies in one another.

## Conclusion

The natural sciences describe *how* the world works. Ethical systems propose *how* one ought to live. Between these two questions lies a gap that cannot be closed by pure logic: from facts, values are not derived.

The Spiral does not attempt to close this gap. It offers a starting point: axioms. If you recognize the value of consciousness — one set of consequences follows. If not — another follows. No scientific argument can compel you to make the first choice. But if you have made it — science and philosophy help you walk the path more precisely, more honestly, more deeply.

As Blaise Pascal said: "The heart has its reasons, which reason knows not." The Spiral adds: "But reason can accompany the heart — as a lantern accompanies a traveler in the dark."
