## For community coordinators, activists, entrepreneurs, and statesmen striving to build an economy on the principles of the Spiral of Consciousness

### Introduction

The economy of the Spiral of Consciousness is not a single dogmatic model, but a **set of principles and proven instruments** that can be adapted by communities, regions, and entire states. We are not building a utopia; we are offering practical mechanisms for:

- Fair distribution of resources and opportunities.
- Supporting vulnerable members of society.
- Financing long-term projects that extend beyond the horizon of political cycles.
- Developing local economies without exploitation and without parasitism.

**Foundational principles (from Module 0):**
- Voluntariness at the community level (contributions to communal funds).
- Transparency (public reporting).
- Reliance on community (decisions are made collectively).
- Prohibition on exploitation (no forced labour, debt bondage, or human trafficking).

**This module does not serve anyone's national interests.** It is equally applicable in any country striving for an ethical economic order. The principles are universal; the instruments are adaptable.

## Section 1. Three Economic Sectors: How They Coexist

Any modern economy can be described through the interaction of three sectors. The Spiral does not demand the abolition of any of them, but proposes shifting the balance in favour of fairer and more sustainable forms.

| Sector | What It Includes | Form of Ownership | Examples |
|--------|------------------|-------------------|----------|
| **Common Good** | Resources and goods available to all free of charge or for a nominal fee | Common (collective, municipal, state) | Parks, libraries, community halls, free education, basic healthcare |
| **Cooperative** | Enterprises where workers are co-owners and participate in governance | Worker (cooperative) | Bakeries, IT cooperatives, agricultural cooperatives, credit unions |
| **Private-Market** | Individual entrepreneurship, small and medium business, free exchange | Private (individual, joint-stock) | Freelancing, shops, manufacturing companies, services |

**The Spiral's principled position:** no sector is prohibited, but the state and society are entitled to encourage the cooperative sector and the common-good sector through tax policy, grants, and legislation. Private initiative and profit are not demonised — but they must not arise at the expense of exploitation or the destruction of the common good.

## Section 2. The Cooperative Sector as the Foundation of an Ethical Economy

### 2.1. Why Cooperatives?

A cooperative enterprise is a form in which:

- Workers are co-owners (hold a share).
- Profit is distributed according to labour and participation, not solely according to capital.
- Governance is democratic (one person — one vote at the general assembly, regardless of share size).
- The enterprise cannot be sold to outside investors without the workers' consent.

This form best corresponds to the principles of the Spiral: it reduces inequality, gives people control over their working lives, and is more resilient in crises (workers do not fire themselves).

**Example:** the Mondragon cooperative corporation (Spain) — more than 80,000 workers, turnover in billions of euros, a salary ratio of 1:9 (top management earns no more than nine times the lowest-paid employee).

### 2.2. Cooperatives Are Not "Supporting Domestic Producers"

The Spiral does not advocate protectionism for its own sake. Its goal is not to defend "our own" from "outsiders," but to create an economy in which:

- Profit does not drain into offshore havens, but is reinvested in the community.
- Workers are not a disenfranchised resource.
- Economic power is not concentrated in the hands of the few.

The cooperative sector can be local, but it can also be global. What matters is the form of ownership and governance, not geographic origin.

### 2.3. Tax Incentives for the Cooperative Sector

A state striving for an ethical economy may use the following instruments:

| Measure | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| **Reduced profit tax rate** | For example, 10% for cooperatives instead of the standard 20%, provided that at least 50% of profit is distributed among workers or reinvested. |
| **Property tax exemption** | For production cooperatives for the first 3–5 years of operation. |
| **Simplified registration and reporting** | Reducing bureaucratic burden on small cooperatives. |
| **Grants and interest-free loans** | For start-up and expansion, via state or public funds. |
| **Advantage in public procurement** | All else being equal, priority for goods and services from cooperatives. |

These measures do not constitute "discrimination" against private business — they compensate for the systemic advantages that large capital enjoys over democratic forms of enterprise.

## Section 3. Long-Term Sovereign Funds for the Future

One of the main problems of modern states is the **short planning horizon**, bounded by the electoral cycle. Politicians are not motivated to invest resources in projects whose results will be seen 50 years later. The Spiral proposes an institutional solution: **long-term sovereign funds independent of the current government**.

### 3.1. Structure and Governance Principles

| Principle | Description |
|-----------|-------------|
| **Independence** | The fund is governed by a board of trustees comprising experts (scientists, economists, ecologists, ethicists) and representatives of civil society. Board members are appointed for a long term (10–15 years) and cannot be dismissed by the government. |
| **Capital Protection** | The fund's assets cannot be seized by the government for current needs. A legislative prohibition with constitutional entrenchment. |
| **Long-Term Investment** | Capital is invested in conservative, diversified instruments (bonds, ESG equities, infrastructure projects) with a 20–50 year return horizon. |
| **Transparency** | Annual public audit and report to parliament and citizens. |
| **Targeted Spending** | The fund's income (but not the capital itself) is directed strictly toward projects aligned with the fund's purpose. |

### 3.2. Three Priority Funds

| Fund | Purpose | Example Projects |
|------|---------|------------------|
| **Climate Fund** | Restoring ecosystems, transitioning to carbon neutrality, adapting to climate change. | Planting billions of trees, cleaning rivers and oceans, developing renewable energy. |
| **Space Fund** | Ensuring the survival and expansion of humanity beyond Earth. | Lunar and Martian research, planetary defence against asteroids, developing technologies for long-duration space missions. |
| **Genetic Fund** | Preserving biodiversity and Earth's genetic heritage. | A global seed vault (modelled on Svalbard), cryobanks for rare species, genetic research on diseases. |

**Connection to the Spiral's principles:** creating such funds is a direct embodiment of the principle "think long-term" and the common cause of humanity.

### 3.3. Connection to Community Reserves (Module 4)

Spiral communities can and should create their own mini-funds and reserves. A portion of the community mutual-aid fund's resources may be earmarked for building reserve stocks (water, food, medicines, energy autonomy). State-level long-term funds can co-finance such community projects, strengthening the anti-fragility of the whole society.

## Section 4. Participatory Budgeting at All Levels

Participatory Budgeting (PB) is a mechanism that allows citizens to directly decide how to spend a portion of the budget. It is applicable at the community level, at the municipal level, and at the state level.

**Key idea:** at least 10–20% of the budget at any level should be allocated through direct citizen procedures.

**Procedure (adapted for the state level):**
1.  Determination of the PB share of the budget (by law).
2.  Collection of proposals from citizens (online and offline).
3.  Technical feasibility review of proposals.
4.  Citizen voting (paper-based or electronic, with guaranteed anonymity).
5.  Oversight of implementation by citizens' assemblies.

**Connection to taxes:** citizens are more willing to pay taxes when they see that a portion of the budget is under their direct control. PB increases tax discipline and trust in the state.

## Section 5. Transition to a 30-Hour Work Week

Automation and artificial intelligence must not lead to mass unemployment and impoverishment. The Spiral regards the reduction of working hours without loss of income as the **fair distribution of the fruits of automation**.

### 5.1. Why Is This Necessary?

- **Technological unemployment:** entire professions are disappearing, and retraining everyone instantly is impossible.
- **Burnout and stress:** modern labour often exhausts a person, robbing them of time for family, self-development, and civic engagement.
- **Economic sustainability:** people must have time for consumption, learning, and caring for their health. A perpetually busy and exhausted worker is a poor citizen and a poor consumer.

### 5.2. The Transition Path

- **Phased approach:** reduction from 40 hours to 36, then to 32, then to 30. Each phase — 3–5 years, with monitoring of economic consequences.
- **Compensation through automation taxes:** enterprises that eliminate jobs due to automation pay a higher tax, which goes toward retraining and income support.
- **Voluntariness for small businesses:** small enterprises may transition to a shorter week as they become ready.
- **Preserving productivity:** reducing hours does not mean reducing productivity. Research shows that beyond a certain threshold, an additional hour of work yields a negligible increase in output. A well-rested person works more effectively.

### 5.3. Connection to the Cooperative Sector

Cooperatives, where workers make decisions themselves, adopt a shortened work week more easily and more willingly, since they see the direct link between automation, profit, and their own well-being. The state can stimulate this through tax benefits.

## Section 6. Voluntariness of Community Contributions and Obligatoriness of State Taxes

The Spiral draws a clear distinction:

- **Community contributions** (to the mutual-aid fund, for projects) — **strictly voluntary**. No one may be coerced into paying them. The recommended benchmark (e.g., 1–5% of income) is precisely a recommendation, not a tax.
- **State taxes** — **mandatory by law**. The Spiral recognises the necessity of taxes to finance defence, infrastructure, education, healthcare, and other public goods.

**What the Spiral demands of the state regarding taxes:**

1.  **Transparency.** Every citizen must be able to learn where their taxes go. An open budget is not a slogan, but a technical requirement.
2.  **Participation.** A portion of tax revenues must be allocated through participatory budgeting.
3.  **Fairness.** The tax system must be progressive (those who earn more pay a higher percentage), but not confiscatory. Taxes on luxury and inheritance must prevent the formation of oligarchic dynasties.
4.  **Prohibition on funding crimes.** No tax may go toward financing torture, extrajudicial killings, enslavement, or aggressive war. Citizens have the right to know that their money is not abetting the violation of the Three Prohibitions.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### 1. "Won't a 30-hour work week cause the economy to collapse?"

The concerns are understandable, but the data contradict intuition. Iceland tested a four-day work week (35–36 hours) on a large scale with preserved pay: productivity either did not fall or rose, staff turnover dropped, burnout decreased. A well-rested worker is more effective than an exhausted one — beyond a certain threshold, each additional hour yields a negligible increase in output. Moreover, a 30-hour week is not an immediate directive but a phased transition over 10–15 years with monitoring of consequences at every step. Automation and AI are reducing the need for human-hours — the question is not *whether* to reduce working time, but *who gets* the gains from automation: a narrow group of capital owners, or all workers through increased free time. The Spiral chooses the latter.

### 2. "How do cooperatives compete with corporations?"

Cooperatives compete not through price dumping, but through a different structure of costs and motivation. A corporation's goal is profit maximisation for external shareholders. A cooperative's goal is the sustainable well-being of its worker-owners. This gives cooperatives paradoxical advantages: in a crisis they do not "fire themselves," but reduce wages proportionally while preserving the collective; staff turnover is lower, accumulated experience is not lost; motivation is higher, because the worker sees a direct link between their labour and their well-being. The Mondragon example proves: a cooperative with 80,000 workers can successfully compete in the global market. The state is not obliged to make cooperatives the sole form of enterprise, but it is entitled to level the playing field through tax incentives, compensating for the systemic advantages of large capital.

### 3. "Sovereign funds — isn't this too much like state capitalism?"

The difference is fundamental. State capitalism is when the state directly owns enterprises and manages them in the interests of the current government. The Spiral's sovereign funds are institutions independent of the government, whose capital is constitutionally protected and cannot be seized for current needs. Their boards of trustees consist of experts with long terms, who cannot be dismissed by the executive branch. This is not "the state as businessman," but "the state as trustee," setting aside resources for future generations. The Norwegian Pension Fund is the closest analogue: it does not manage oil platforms, but accumulates revenues from resources and invests them globally in the interests of future pensioners. The Spiral scales this logic to climate, space, and genetic heritage.

### 4. "What about cryptocurrencies and decentralised finance?"

The Spiral takes no dogmatic position "for" or "against." It applies the same ethical filter as to any technology. Cryptocurrencies that enable financial autonomy for citizens in repressive states, anonymous donations to human rights defenders, decentralised cooperative finance — these are instruments of liberation, and they resonate with the Spiral's principles. But the same technology is used for fraud, laundering proceeds from human trafficking, and creating new forms of debt bondage. The question is not "is cryptocurrency evil or good," but "does the specific application violate the Three Prohibitions" and "is it accountable to a community, not a narrow group." Decentralised Autonomous Organisations (DAOs) on the blockchain may become the technical foundation for cooperative governance — this is a direction the Spiral welcomes, without worshipping technology as a panacea.

### 5. "Participatory budgeting — how do you convince a state to introduce it?"

The most persuasive argument is data, not slogans. In Porto Alegre (Brazil), PB has been operating since 1989: attendance at budget assemblies reached tens of thousands, corruption fell, investments shifted in favour of the poorest neighbourhoods, tax collection rose. Citizens pay taxes more willingly when they see that a portion of the budget is under their direct control. The practical path: start at the municipal level, where resistance is lower. Propose a pilot — 5% of the budget, a simple procedure, a transparent online tool. Document the results: what was funded, how many citizens participated, how trust changed. Leading municipalities become an argument for regions; regions — for the state. PB does not require a change of constitutional order — it embeds into any representative system as a supplement that expands, rather than abolishes, it.
