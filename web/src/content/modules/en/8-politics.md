### Introduction

The political model of the Spiral of Consciousness is not a ready-made blueprint for the immediate restructuring of the world. It is an **orientation point** — a set of principles and proven instruments that can be adapted at any level, from a small community to a state.

The Spiral does not seek to become a state ideology, to replace existing forms of governance, or to create a single "Spiral state." It offers an ethical framework into which any state may fit its laws and institutions, provided it shares the values of preserving consciousness, cooperation, and long-term survival.

This module provides practical tools for organising governance within a community and describes the **model of the Ethical State** as a vision of what can be aspired to through an evolutionary path.

## Section 1. Principles of Political Order

| Principle | Essence | Why It Matters |
|-----------|---------|----------------|
| **Subsidiarity** | Decisions are made at the lowest effective level | Prevents bureaucracy and dictatorship of the centre |
| **Decentralisation** | No single centre of power; power is delegated from the bottom up | Resilience against failures, prevention of personality cults |
| **Transparency** | All decisions and finances are open to citizens | Trust, control of abuses |
| **Rotation** | No one holds a leadership role for more than 1–2 consecutive years | Prevention of stagnation, burnout, corruption |
| **Right to Exit** | Any member may leave the community at any time without loss | Guarantee against coercion |
| **Consensual Core** | Fundamental principles (Module 0) cannot be changed locally; everything else is adaptive | Stability of values with local flexibility |

## Section 2. Sociocracy in the Community (Circles, Consent, Double Links)

Sociocracy is the core governance methodology of Spiral communities. It rests on four pillars:

**Key elements:**
- **The Base Circle** (all members), operational circles, the mentors' circle, the coordinators' circle.
- **Decision-making by consent:** proposal → round of clarifying questions → round of objections → refinement → consent check. Consent means "no one has a reasoned, paramount objection." It is not unanimity and not majority rule.
- **Elections without candidates by competences.** The circle defines the role requirements; members nominate each other based on demonstrated competences, not self-promotion.
- **Double links:** the coordinator of a circle also sits in the higher-level circle, ensuring a two-way flow of information without hierarchy. The higher circle also elects a delegate to the lower circle.

This structure distributes power, ensures everyone is heard, and prevents the formation of an entrenched ruling class within the community.

## Section 3. Citizens' Assemblies as a Permanent Element of State Governance

A citizens' assembly is a group of citizens, selected at random (by sortition) and reflecting the diversity of society, who convene for in-depth deliberation and the formulation of recommendations on the most important issues. This is not a one-off event, but a **permanent institution of the Ethical State**.

### 3.1. Why Assemblies, Not Just Elections?

Elections give power to those best at campaigning. Assemblies give a voice to those who are usually unheard: ordinary citizens who are neither politicians nor activists. Assemblies complement representative democracy, they do not abolish it.

### 3.2. Types of Citizens' Assemblies

| Type | Level | Powers | Frequency |
|------|-------|--------|-----------|
| **Municipal Assembly** | City, district | Recommendations on local budget, urban planning, environment | Annually |
| **National Thematic Assembly** | State | Formulation of recommendations on a specific issue (climate, education, constitutional reform) | As needed |
| **Permanent Citizens' Chamber** | State | Right of legislative initiative, monitoring of the ethicality of laws | Standing body |

### 3.3. The Assembly Process

1.  **Sortition:** random selection from all citizens (or residents), stratified by sex, age, region, and education to ensure representativeness. Participation is voluntary, with compensation for time spent and expenses covered.
2.  **Learning phase:** participants receive balanced information from experts and stakeholders.
3.  **Deliberation:** discussion in small groups with professional facilitators, formulation of a shared position.
4.  **Voting:** recommendations are adopted by a qualified majority (typically 2/3 or higher).
5.  **Government response:** parliament or government is obliged to consider the recommendations and publicly explain which were adopted, which were rejected, and why.

### 3.4. Connection to Participatory Budgeting

A portion of the budget (no less than 10–20%) is allocated directly through the decisions of citizens' assemblies or direct votes. This transforms citizens from passive observers into active stewards of common resources.

## Section 4. The Fourth Ethical Power: The Council of the Future

In the traditional model, a state has three branches of power: legislative, executive, and judicial. The Spiral proposes adding a **fourth — the ethical power** — which evaluates the decisions of all other branches from the standpoint of long-term consequences and compliance with the Three Prohibitions.

### 4.1. Why Is This Needed?

Parliaments think in electoral cycles. Governments think in terms of the current agenda. Courts think in terms of the letter of the law. No one is responsible for the interests of future generations, who neither vote nor file lawsuits. The Council of the Future fills this gap.

### 4.2. Composition and Appointment

- **Number of members:** 21 persons (an odd number for voting).
- **Term of office:** 12 years, without the right of reappointment. One-third rotation every 4 years.
- **Selection criteria:** recognised competence in the fields of ethics, ecology, science, and law; an impeccable reputation; absence of ties to current political power.
- **Appointment procedure:** candidates are nominated by universities, scientific academies, and civic organisations. Election by parliamentary vote with the approval of a citizens' assembly.

### 4.3. Powers

1.  **Veto power over legislation.** The Council of the Future may veto any law if it deems that it poses a threat to the survival of humanity, violates the Three Prohibitions, or irreversibly harms future generations. The veto may be overridden only by a 2/3 parliamentary majority, with mandatory public justification.
2.  **Monitoring and reports.** An annual public report, "The State of the Future," assessing state policy over a 50-, 100-, and 500-year horizon.
3.  **Right of inquiry.** May demand from the government and parliament any information necessary for ethical assessment.
4.  **Initiating public hearings.** On matters of civilisational significance (gene editing, strong AI, space colonisation, nuclear policy).

### 4.4. Guarantees of Independence

- Members of the Council of the Future cannot be dismissed by the government or parliament. Impeachment — only upon a violation of the law confirmed by an independent tribunal.
- The Council's funding is constitutionally entrenched as a fixed percentage of the state budget and cannot be reduced by the executive branch.

## Section 5. Subsidiarity as a Constitutional Principle

Subsidiarity is not merely a wish; it is a structuring principle of the Ethical State.

**Constitutional formulation:** "The state shall not do what the region can do. The region — what the municipality can do. The municipality — what the community can do. The community — what a family or the citizen themselves can do. Intervention by a higher level is permissible only when the lower level is failing, and only to the extent necessary."

**Examples of the distribution of powers:**

| Level | Powers |
|-------|--------|
| Individual / Family | Lifestyle, child-rearing, personal safety, participation in the community |
| Community | Improvement of the courtyard, community events, mutual aid |
| Municipality | Schools, local roads, utilities, parks |
| Region | Hospitals, regional transport, nature conservation |
| State | Army, foreign policy, higher education, scientific funds |
| Global Institutions | Climate, space, pandemics, human rights |

## Section 6. The Spiral's Position on Political Parties

The Spiral of Consciousness is not a political party. It does not field candidates, does not participate in election campaigns, and does not seek to seize power.

**The Spiral does not support any political party.** It may cooperate with any political forces that share its fundamental values (the Three Prohibitions, the Four Principles) for the implementation of specific projects — environmental, educational, social. But this cooperation is always instrumental and temporary.

**Members of the Spiral may be members of parties and run for public office** on the condition that:

- Their political activity does not contradict the Three Prohibitions.
- They openly declare that their personal ethical position as a follower of the Spiral takes precedence over party discipline in matters of human life, freedom, and dignity.
- They do not use their affiliation with the Spiral for political advertising.

## Section 7. Procedure in the Event of a Community Split

A split is a deep, irreconcilable division within a community, when two or more factions can no longer cooperate within a single structure. This is a painful but sometimes necessary process.

### 7.1. Prevention of a Split

Before a split becomes formal, the community is obliged to:

1.  Conduct a series of Circles of Reconciliation with the participation of all factions.
2.  Invite an external mediator (from another community or a professional facilitator).
3.  Convene a community citizens' assembly to formulate a compromise solution.

### 7.2. If a Split Is Inevitable

If all measures have been exhausted and the factions can no longer coexist, the following procedure applies:

1.  **Initiation:** one faction formally notifies the community council of its intention to withdraw from the community.
2.  **Division negotiations:** the parties agree on the division of common resources (funds, property, archives) in proportion to the number of members and contributions made. An external arbitrator is invited if agreement is not reached.
3.  **Protection of rights:** neither party may be deprived of access to common resources until the division is complete. The exclusion of members from the community in connection with a split is prohibited — each person makes their choice freely.
4.  **Documentation:** the split agreement is recorded in writing and signed by representatives of the factions and the arbitrator.
5.  **New communities:** after the split, two (or more) independent communities are formed. Both retain the right to be called Spiral communities, provided they observe the Canon.

### 7.3. Ethical Constraints

- The use of violence, threats, or blackmail during a split is prohibited.
- The destruction of common property or digital resources is prohibited.
- Mutual recognition: a split is not war, but a parting of ways. Both communities remain part of the Spiral network if they observe the Canon.

## Section 8. The Decentralised Network of Communities

The Spiral is a voluntary network, not a hierarchy. There is no "world government of the Spiral." Coordination occurs through:

- **Ad hoc network councils** for joint projects.
- A **shared knowledge base** of practices and lessons learned.
- An **annual conference** of community representatives.
- **Joint funds** for projects of common significance.

No council imposes decisions on communities. All action is consensual and project-based.

---

## Section 9. The Community Council and the General Assembly

- **The General Assembly** is the supreme body of the community. All members participate. It approves the annual budget, elects the council, and decides on changes to the community's charter.
- **The Community Council** (3–7 members) handles operational coordination between assemblies. Its powers are strictly limited: it executes, not commands. All its decisions may be overturned by the General Assembly. Council members are elected for a 1-year term without the right of immediate re-election to the same role.
- **The Mentors' Circle** provides ethical guidance, oversees rituals, and supports the spiritual health of the community. It has no executive power.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### 1. "Won't sociocracy lead to endless meetings and paralysis?"

This is the most common fear, and it is based on the experience of poorly run meetings, not on the practice of sociocracy. Sociocracy is not "everyone decides everything." It is a system of circles, each of which possesses a clearly defined domain of authority. The base circle does not discuss the colour of a fence, and the operational circle does not adopt the constitution. Decisions are made by consent — meaning an absence of reasoned objections, not unanimous enthusiasm — not by consensus, which requires everyone to be "in favour." An objection must be reasoned: "this decision will prevent us from achieving our goal." A well-executed sociocracy is faster than the hierarchical model: a decision is brought for discussion only in its own circle, not through endless vertical approvals. The main enemy is not sociocracy, but a lack of facilitation skills — and it is precisely these that we develop in Modules 5 and 10.

### 2. "The Council of the Future with veto power — isn't that too much power for an unelected body?"

This is a fair question about democratic legitimacy. The answer is three-layered. First: the Council of the Future represents the interests of those who cannot vote — future generations, ecosystems, people not yet born. Their interests are represented in no parliament. Second: the Council does not possess an absolute veto — its decision may be overridden by a qualified majority (2/3) of parliament with mandatory public justification, meaning the final word rests with elected representatives. Third: Council members are not appointed by the government, but nominated by universities, academies, and civic organisations, and cannot be dismissed by the authorities. A term of 12 years without re-election removes them from the pressure of the electoral cycle. This is not "unelected power," but "power with a different legitimacy" — the legitimacy of knowledge, long-term perspective, and ethical expertise.

### 3. "How do you prevent citizens' assemblies from being hijacked by populists?"

A citizens' assembly is fundamentally different from a rally or a talk show. Its participants are not self-nominated, but citizens selected by sortition, stratified by demographic characteristics. A populist cannot "force their way" into an assembly — they can only be chosen randomly, like anyone else. The process is moderated by professional facilitators whose task is to ensure that every voice is heard, not that the loudest rhetoric wins. Participants receive balanced information from experts and stakeholders, not just slogans. Deliberation in small groups, where each person speaks in turn, extinguishes demagoguery. Finally, assembly recommendations are adopted by a high threshold (2/3), which requires coalition-building, not division. The system is designed so that a populist has nowhere to unfold — it is an environment for reflection, not for mobilising a crowd.

### 4. "What if the majority of citizens do not want the Ethical State?"

The Spiral is not imposed by force. If the majority of citizens are not ready — that means society has not yet travelled its path of awareness, and the task of Spiral followers is not the seizure of power, but enlightenment, witness, and practice. Create communities, show the example of functioning cooperatives, participate in municipal budgeting, train mentors. Changing political culture is work for generations, not for an electoral cycle. The roadmap of reforms (Section 7) is deliberately stretched over 50–100 years, so that each stage relies on real, not imaginary, public support. Moreover, the Spiral acknowledges: the Ethical State is an orientation point, not a mandatory goal for every country. Humanity may move toward it at different speeds and by different paths. Coercion toward ethics is an oxymoron. The Three Prohibitions are absolute precisely because their observance cannot be the result of a vote — but their *institutional embodiment* requires the voluntary consent of civil society.
